Lead (Pb)

Atomic Number: 82

Basic Information

Symbol: Pb
Atomic Mass: 207.2 u
Category: Post-Transition Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p²

Key Properties

  • Very dense and soft metal
  • Excellent radiation shielding
  • Can be cut with a knife
  • Highly toxic (neurotoxin)
  • One of the oldest known metals

Discovery & History

Known since ancient times – lead beads dating to 7000-6500 BCE have been found in Turkey. The Romans used it extensively for pipes, coins, and cookware.

The symbol Pb comes from the Latin "plumbum" (meaning lead), from which we get "plumber" – Roman pipes were made of lead.

Historical Timeline:
  • ~7000 BCE: Earliest known lead artifacts
  • Roman era: Widespread use in plumbing
  • 1920s: Leaded gasoline introduced
  • 1970s: Health concerns lead to phase-out

Common Uses

Car batteries (lead-acid)
Radiation shielding (X-ray rooms)
Fishing weights and diving belts
Solder (electronics)
Stained glass windows
Chemical-resistant equipment
Power cable sheathing
Paint and gasoline (historical)

Interesting Facts

  • Lead is so soft that it can be scratched with a fingernail and leaves a mark on paper like a pencil
  • A sheet of lead just 4 mm thick blocks 90% of X-rays – that's why radiologists wear lead aprons
  • Lead poisoning (saturnism) contributed to the fall of Rome – some historians believe Roman elites suffered from lead toxicity due to lead pipes and wine sweeteners
  • Lead is the final stable element in the decay chain of uranium and thorium – all uranium eventually becomes lead
  • "Pencil lead" is actually graphite (carbon) – no lead at all! The name stuck because ancient writing materials used lead
  • Lead is so dense that a cubic foot weighs over 700 pounds – it's used for ballast in ships and submarines

Atomic Structure

82 protons, 125 neutrons, 82 electrons
