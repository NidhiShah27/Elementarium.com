Nonmetals

The essentials of life • gases, liquids & reactive solids

What are Nonmetals?

Position: upper right corner of periodic table (except hydrogen) — groups 14–18, periods 1–6
Electron configuration: high electronegativity, usually 4–8 valence electrons (p-block except H & He)
Typical traits:
  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity (except carbon as graphite)
  • Brittle when solid, often gases or liquids at room temperature
  • High ionization energies & electronegativities
  • Form acidic oxides (except neutral H₂O, CO)
  • Usually gain electrons to form anions in chemical reactions
  • Essential for biology: C, H, O, N, S, P are building blocks of life

From the Greek non + metallon — they lack metallic luster, malleability, and conductivity. Yet they are the most abundant elements in living organisms.

Key Properties

  • Diverse states: 11 gases (H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, noble gases), 1 liquid (Br₂), and many solids (C, P, S, Se, I)
  • Reactivity: halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) are extremely reactive; noble gases are inert.
  • Electronegativity: fluorine is the most electronegative element (4.0), followed by oxygen.
  • Oxides: typically acidic (SO₂, CO₂, N₂O₅) or neutral (H₂O, NO).
  • Biological role: carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur make up 98% of living matter.
  • Allotropes: carbon (diamond, graphite, graphene), oxygen (O₂, O₃), phosphorus (white, red).
Did you know? Despite being a nonmetal, graphite conducts electricity due to delocalized electrons — it’s used in electrodes and batteries.

Interesting facts

  • Hydrogen is the lightest and most abundant element in the universe — powers stars.
  • Carbon can form over 10 million compounds, more than all other elements combined.
  • Oxygen makes up about 21% of Earth's atmosphere and 50% of Earth's crust (by mass).
  • Fluorine is the most reactive element — reacts with almost any substance, even glass.
  • Phosphorus was discovered from urine in 1669 by Hennig Brand while searching for the philosopher's stone.
  • Helium is the only element that cannot be frozen at atmospheric pressure — remains liquid down to absolute zero.

Chemical personality: the octet rule & reactivity

Halogens (Group 17) are highly electronegative and readily gain one electron to form stable anions (F⁻, Cl⁻). They are diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂).

Reactivity decreases down the group: fluorine is the strongest oxidizing agent.

Noble gases (Group 18) have full valence shells — extremely low chemical reactivity. Under extreme conditions, xenon and krypton form compounds (XeF₂, etc.).

Helium, neon, and argon are truly inert in most practical conditions.

Chalcogens (O, S, Se) typically gain two electrons to form anions (O²⁻, S²⁻) and form acidic oxides like SO₂ and CO₂, contributing to acid rain.

Abundance & vital statistics

Oxygen ≈ 46% crust
Carbon ≈ 0.02% crust
Hydrogen ≈ 0.14% crust
Nitrogen ≈ 78% atmosphere

Global production: Nitrogen (millions of tons via Haber process), Oxygen (500 Mt/year), Sulfur (80 Mt/year), Chlorine (70 Mt/year). Nonmetals dominate industrial chemistry — fertilizers, plastics, pharmaceuticals, and energy storage.

Life's backbone: The human body is ~65% oxygen, 18% carbon, 10% hydrogen, and 3% nitrogen. Nonmetals literally make up who we are.

Remarkable allotropes of nonmetals

Carbon: diamond (hardest mineral), graphite (lubricant), graphene (superconductor)
Oxygen: O₂ (essential for respiration) & O₃ (ozone layer protects from UV)
Phosphorus: white (toxic, pyrophoric) vs red (safe, matches)
