Dysprosium (Dy)

Atomic Number: 66

Basic Information

Symbol: Dy
Atomic Mass: 162.500 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁰ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Bright silvery metal, relatively soft
  • Highest magnetic strength of any element at low temperatures
  • Reacts with water and acids, tarnishes in air
  • Name from Greek "dysprositos" (hard to get at)
  • 7 naturally occurring isotopes

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1886 by French chemist Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran in Paris. He identified it spectroscopically and named it after the Greek "dysprositos" meaning "hard to get at" due to the difficulty of separating it from other rare earths.

It wasn't isolated in pure form until the 1950s with the development of ion-exchange techniques.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1886: Boisbaudran identifies dysprosium
  • 1906: Georges Urbain obtains relatively pure sample
  • 1950s: Ion-exchange purification makes it available
  • Modern: Essential for Neodymium magnets and nuclear control rods

Common Uses

NdFeB magnets (adds heat resistance)
Electric vehicle motor magnets
Nuclear reactor control rods
Terfenol-D magnetostrictive alloy
Solid-state laser dopant
Dysprosium iodide in metal halide lamps
Nanoparticle contrast agents (research)
Gamma ray imaging detectors

Interesting Facts

  • Dysprosium has the highest magnetic strength of any element at low temperatures – its magnetic moment is 10.6 µB, the highest known.
  • It is essential for high-performance neodymium magnets used in electric vehicles; adding dysprosium raises their operating temperature and prevents demagnetization.
  • The name "dysprositos" (hard to get at) is fitting – it took nearly 70 years after discovery to obtain it in pure form.
  • Dysprosium has the highest thermal neutron absorption cross-section of any non-radioactive element (except gadolinium), making it ideal for control rods.
  • Its isotopes include ¹⁶⁴Dy, which is used in dosimeters to measure radiation exposure.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is about 5.2 ppm, more common than silver.

Atomic Structure

66 protons, 96 neutrons (in 162Dy), 66 electrons

Try These Dysprosium Reactions

Dysprosium is a reactive lanthanide, slowly attacked by water and rapidly by acids. It forms pale yellow compounds in the +3 state.
