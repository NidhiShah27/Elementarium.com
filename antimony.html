Antimony (Sb)

Atomic Number: 51

Basic Information

Symbol: Sb
Atomic Mass: 121.760 u
Category: Metalloid
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p³

Key Properties

  • Silvery, lustrous, brittle metalloid
  • Expands when it solidifies (like water)
  • Poor conductor of heat and electricity
  • Resistant to acids
  • Known since ancient times as a metal

Discovery & History

Known since ancient times – antimony sulfide (stibnite) was used as eye makeup in Egypt. The element was first scientifically described by Nicolas Lémery in 1707.

The symbol Sb comes from Latin "stibium" meaning stibnite. The name antimony may come from Greek "anti-monos" (not alone) or Arabic "al-ithmid".

Historical Timeline:
  • ~3000 BCE: Used as eye cosmetic (Egypt)
  • 1707: Lémery publishes study of antimony
  • 19th c.: Used in alloys for type metal
  • 20th c.: Flame retardants and semiconductors

Common Uses

Flame retardants (Sb₂O₃)
Lead-acid batteries
Alloys (hardens lead, tin)
Semiconductor doping
Paints and pigments
Type metal (printing)
Antiprotozoal drugs
Glass decolorizer

Interesting Facts

  • Antimony was used as a laxative and cosmetic in ancient times – it may have killed Mozart (theories suggest antimony poisoning)
  • Like water, antimony expands when it solidifies – making it useful for casting detailed objects
  • The "Stibnite" mineral (Sb₂S₃) forms stunning metallic crystals that can be several feet long
  • Antimony trioxide is the most widely used flame retardant – it's added to plastics, textiles, and electronics
  • Ancient Egyptians used stibnite as kohl (eyeliner) – it was believed to have medicinal and magical properties
  • Antimony is one of the few elements that can act as both a metal and a nonmetal in compounds

Atomic Structure

51 protons, 71 neutrons, 51 electrons
