Transition Metals

Groups 3-12 Elements

Groups: 3 to 12

Category: Transition Metal

Elements: Scandium (Sc) to Zinc (Zn), Yttrium (Y) to Cadmium (Cd), Lanthanum (La) to Mercury (Hg), Actinium (Ac) to Copernicium (Cn)

General Electron Configuration: (n-1)d1-10 ns1-2

Transition metals are ductile, malleable, and conduct electricity and heat. They form colored compounds and can have various oxidation states.

They are typically hard and strong, with high melting and boiling points (except mercury, which is liquid at room temperature).

Key Properties:
  • Have partially filled d-orbitals in their ground state or common oxidation states.
  • Exhibit multiple oxidation states (e.g., Iron: +2, +3; Manganese: +2 to +7).
  • Form colored ions and compounds due to d-d electron transitions.
  • Good catalysts (e.g., Platinum, Nickel, Iron in industrial processes).
  • Form coordination complexes with ligands.
  • High densities and melting points (except Group 12 elements).
  • Many are paramagnetic (attracted to magnetic fields).
Common Uses:
  • Iron (Fe): Steel production, construction, magnets.
  • Copper (Cu): Electrical wiring, plumbing, coins.
  • Gold (Au) & Silver (Ag): Jewelry, electronics, currency.
  • Zinc (Zn): Galvanization, batteries, alloys.
  • Titanium (Ti): Aerospace, medical implants, pigments.
  • Platinum (Pt) & Palladium (Pd): Catalytic converters, jewelry.
  • Nickel (Ni): Stainless steel, batteries, coins.

Transition metals include many familiar elements like Iron, Copper, Gold, and Silver

Unique Characteristics:
  • Mercury (Hg): Only metal liquid at room temperature.
  • Chromium (Cr): Forms protective oxide layer (stainless steel).
  • Tungsten (W): Highest melting point of all metals (3422°C).
  • Gold (Au): Most malleable and ductile metal.
  • Iron (Fe): Most abundant transition metal in Earth's crust.
  • Copper (Cu): Excellent electrical conductor (second only to silver).
Notable Discoveries

Iron (Fe)

Ancient Times: Known since antiquity

Early Use: Used since at least 3000 BCE

Note: Iron Age began around 1200 BCE

Copper (Cu)

Ancient Times: Known since prehistoric times

Early Use: Used since 9000 BCE

Note: Copper Age began around 4500 BCE

Gold (Au) & Silver (Ag)

Ancient Times: Known since antiquity

Early Use: Used since 6000 BCE (Gold), 3000 BCE (Silver)

Note: Among first metals used by humans

Platinum (Pt)

Year: 1735

Discoverer: Antonio de Ulloa

Origin: Named from Spanish "platina" meaning "little silver"

Titanium (Ti)

Year: 1791

Discoverer: William Gregor

Method: Discovered in ilmenite sand

Named by: Martin Heinrich Klaproth in 1795

Mercury (Hg)

Ancient Times: Known since ancient China and India

Early Use: Used since 1500 BCE

Symbol: Hg from Greek "hydrargyrum" meaning "liquid silver"

Zinc (Zn)

Year: 1746

Discoverer: Andreas Sigismund Marggraf

Method: First isolated metallic zinc in Europe

Note: Known in India since 1000 BCE

Nickel (Ni)

Year: 1751

Discoverer: Axel Fredrik Cronstedt

Origin: Discovered in nickel ore; named from German "Kupfernickel"
