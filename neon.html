Neon (Ne)

Atomic Number: 10

Basic Information

Symbol: Ne
Atomic Mass: 20.1797 u
Category: Noble Gas
State at Room Temperature: Gas
Electron Configuration:
[He] 2s² 2p⁶

Key Properties

  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless noble gas
  • Emits bright orange-red light when electrified
  • Boils at -246°C, melts at -248.6°C
  • Completely inert – forms no stable compounds
  • Fifth most abundant element in the universe

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1898 by Sir William Ramsay and Morris Travers in London, England. They isolated it from liquid air and named it after the Greek "neos" (new).

The first neon sign was created by Georges Claude in 1910, starting the "neon lighting" industry.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1898: Ramsay and Travers discover neon
  • 1910: First commercial neon sign
  • 1912: First neon sign sold in Los Angeles
  • 20th c.: Neon becomes iconic for advertising

Common Uses

Neon signs (orange-red)
Plasma TVs and displays
Lightning arrestors
Cryogenic refrigeration
Helium-neon lasers
Vacuum leak detection
High-voltage indicators
Coolant in scientific research

Interesting Facts

  • Despite the name, "neon" signs actually use different gases for different colors – helium (yellow), argon (blue), krypton (white), and mercury vapor (blue)
  • The orange-red glow of real neon is unmistakable and cannot be exactly reproduced by other gases
  • Liquid neon is a more efficient refrigerant than liquid helium or hydrogen
  • Neon is rare on Earth but abundant in the universe – it's the fifth most abundant element in the cosmos
  • Unlike most elements, neon has no stable compounds – it's truly inert
  • Neon was discovered in just three weeks – Ramsay and Travers were on a roll, also discovering krypton and xenon in the same year!

Atomic Structure

10 protons, 10 neutrons, 10 electrons
