Lutetium (Lu)

Atomic Number: 71

Basic Information

Symbol: Lu
Atomic Mass: 174.9668 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, hardest and densest lanthanide
  • Last element in the lanthanide series
  • Named after Lutetia, ancient Roman name for Paris
  • Relatively stable in air, resists oxidation
  • Used in PET scan detectors (LSO crystals)

Discovery & History

Discovered independently in 1907 by French scientist Georges Urbain, Austrian chemist Carl Auer von Welsbach, and American chemist Charles James. Urbain named it after Lutetia, the ancient Roman name for Paris.

The discovery was controversial – Urbain and von Welsbach both claimed priority, but Urbain's name was eventually adopted.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1907: Urbain, von Welsbach, and James isolate lutetium
  • 1909: IUPAC adopts Urbain's name
  • 1950s: Pure lutetium produced
  • Modern: Essential for medical imaging (PET scans)

Common Uses

PET scan detectors (LSO: Lu₂SiO₅)
Beta source (¹⁷⁶Lu) for nuclear batteries
Lu:YAG laser crystals
High-refractive index glass
Catalyst in petroleum cracking
Phosphor activator
Cancer therapy research
Metallurgy additive

Interesting Facts

  • Lutetium is the last element in the lanthanide series and completes the filling of the 4f subshell (4f¹⁴ 5d¹ 6s²).
  • It is the hardest and densest lanthanide, with properties more similar to transition metals like lutetium.
  • Lutetium oxyorthosilicate (LSO) crystals are the gold standard for PET scan detectors – they are dense, fast, and have high light output.
  • Natural lutetium consists of 97.4% stable ¹⁷⁵Lu and 2.6% radioactive ¹⁷⁶Lu with a half-life of 38 billion years.
  • It is named after Lutetia, the ancient Roman settlement that became Paris – the only element named after the French capital.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is about 0.5 ppm, making it the least abundant lanthanide after promethium and thulium.

Atomic Structure

71 protons, 104 neutrons (in 175Lu), 71 electrons

Try These Lutetium Reactions

Lutetium is the least reactive lanthanide, tarnishing slowly in air and reacting slowly with water. It dissolves readily in acids.
