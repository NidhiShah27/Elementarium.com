Promethium (Pm)

Atomic Number: 61

Basic Information

Symbol: Pm
Atomic Mass: [145] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f⁵ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Only lanthanide with no stable isotopes
  • Soft, silvery-white metal
  • Glows in the dark due to radioactivity
  • Named after Prometheus from Greek mythology
  • Produced artificially in nuclear reactors

Discovery & History

First produced and identified in 1945 at Oak Ridge National Laboratory, Tennessee, by Jacob A. Marinsky, Lawrence E. Glendenin, and Charles D. Coryell. They isolated it from uranium fission products.

Named after Prometheus, the Titan in Greek mythology who stole fire from the gods and gave it to humanity – symbolizing both the power and danger of nuclear energy.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1945: First produced at Oak Ridge (fission products)
  • 1947: Name "promethium" proposed and accepted
  • 1960s: Used in atomic batteries for spacecraft
  • Modern: Limited use due to scarcity and radioactivity

Common Uses

Atomic batteries (pacemakers, spacecraft)
Luminous paint (glow-in-the-dark)
Thickness gauges
Scientific research
Radioisotope power sources
Nuclear battery prototypes
No biological applications (radioactive)
Calibration standards

Interesting Facts

  • Promethium is the only lanthanide and one of only two elements (with technetium) below bismuth that have no stable isotopes.
  • It glows pale blue or green in the dark due to its intense beta radiation.
  • 145Pm is the most stable isotope with a half-life of 17.7 years.
  • Trace amounts exist naturally in uranium ores as fission products, but it's essentially synthetic.
  • About 500 grams of promethium are produced annually worldwide – extremely rare.
  • Its name perfectly captures the "fire" of nuclear energy given to humanity, with all its promise and peril.

Atomic Structure

61 protons, 84 neutrons (in 145Pm), 61 electrons
