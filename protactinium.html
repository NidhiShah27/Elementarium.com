Protactinium (Pa)

Atomic Number: 91

Basic Information

Symbol: Pa
Atomic Mass: 231.03588 u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f² 6d¹ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Dense, silvery-gray radioactive metal
  • Name means "parent of actinium" (²³¹Pa decays to ²²⁷Ac)
  • One of the rarest and most expensive naturally occurring elements
  • Superconducting below 1.4 K
  • Exhibits multiple oxidation states (+4, +5) in aqueous solution

Discovery & History

First identified in 1913 by Kasimir Fajans and Oswald Helmuth Göhring, who named it "brevium" due to the short half-life of ²³⁴Pa. The longer-lived isotope ²³¹Pa was discovered in 1918 by Otto Hahn and Lise Meitner (Germany) and independently by Frederick Soddy and John Cranston (UK).

The name was changed to protactinium in 1918 because it decays to actinium. It was the last of the naturally occurring radioactive elements to be discovered.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1913: Fajans & Göhring discover ²³⁴Pa ("brevium")
  • 1918: Hahn/Meitner and Soddy/Cranston discover ²³¹Pa
  • 1927: Aristid von Grosse isolates Pa₂O₅
  • 1961: First preparation of pure protactinium metal (UKAEA)

Common Uses

Interesting Facts

  • Protactinium is one of the rarest elements in Earth's crust (about 0.1 parts per trillion), typically extracted from uranium ores.
  • ²³¹Pa has a half-life of 32,760 years – long enough to be isolated but still intensely radioactive.
  • It costs around $280 per gram, making it one of the most expensive natural elements.
  • The name "protactinium" means "before actinium" because ²³¹Pa decays to ²²⁷Ac.
  • Only a few hundred grams of protactinium have ever been produced worldwide.
  • It exhibits complex chemical behavior, with both +4 and +5 oxidation states stable in solution.

Atomic Structure

91 protons, 140 neutrons (in 231Pa), 91 electrons

Experiment Idea: Due to its extreme rarity and radioactivity, protactinium is handled in specialized labs. Its +5 state readily hydrolyzes, forming Pa₂O₅·xH₂O. In concentrated HCl, it forms anionic chloro complexes that can be extracted into organic solvents – a method used to separate it from uranium ores.
