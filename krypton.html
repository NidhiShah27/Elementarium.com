Krypton (Kr)

Atomic Number: 36

Basic Information

Symbol: Kr
Atomic Mass: 83.798 u
Category: Noble Gas
State at Room Temperature: Gas
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p⁶

Key Properties

  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless noble gas
  • Emits bright yellow-green light when electrified
  • Mostly inert – forms few compounds (with fluorine)
  • One of the rarest gases in Earth's atmosphere (1 ppm)
  • Boils at -153.4°C, melts at -157.4°C

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1898 by Sir William Ramsay and Morris Travers in London, England. They found it in the residue of evaporated liquid air.

The name comes from Greek "kryptos" meaning "hidden" – because it was so difficult to find.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1898: Ramsay and Travers discover krypton
  • 1960: Krypton-86 used to define the meter (until 1983)
  • 1965: First krypton compound (KrF₂) synthesized
  • 20th c.: Used in lighting and lasers

Common Uses

High-efficiency fluorescent lamps
Flash lamps for high-speed photography
Krypton lasers (white light)
Insulation in double-pane windows
Liquid krypton calorimeters (physics)
KrF excimer lasers (semiconductor manufacturing)
Satellite propulsion (xenon alternative)
Plasma globes and signage

Interesting Facts

  • Krypton was the first noble gas to have a compound synthesized (krypton difluoride, KrF₂) – proving they weren't completely inert
  • The orange-red glow of "neon" signs is often actually krypton – it produces a pale violet to white light, but with filters can create various colors
  • Krypton-86 was used from 1960 to 1983 to define the meter – 1 meter = 1,650,763.73 wavelengths of its orange-red spectral line
  • Krypton is 23 times heavier than helium and will collect in low areas if released
  • The name "krypton" comes from the Greek "kryptos" (hidden) – fitting for Superman's home planet!
  • Krypton is used in some photographic flash tubes because it produces a very intense, brief flash of light

Atomic Structure

36 protons, 48 neutrons, 36 electrons
