Barium (Ba)

Atomic Number: 56

Basic Information

Symbol: Ba
Atomic Mass: 137.327 u
Category: Alkaline Earth Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 6s²

Key Properties

  • Burns with brilliant apple-green flame
  • Highly reactive with water
  • Dense, silvery-white metal
  • Absorbs X-rays strongly
  • All water-soluble barium compounds are toxic

Discovery & History

Discovered by Carl Wilhelm Scheele in 1772 as an oxide. First isolated by Humphry Davy in 1808 through electrolysis of barium salts.

The name comes from Greek "barys" meaning heavy, due to its dense compounds (like barite, BaSO₄).

Historical Timeline:
  • 1600s: "Bologna stones" (barite) glow after exposure to light
  • 1772: Scheele identifies barium oxide
  • 1808: Davy isolates barium metal
  • 20th c.: Used in medical imaging and drilling fluids

Common Uses

Green fireworks and flares
Barium meal (medical imaging)
Drilling fluids (barite)
Pigments (lithopone, blanc fixe)
Getter in vacuum tubes
Nuclear reactors (absorbs neutrons)
Special glass and ceramics
Laboratory reagent

Interesting Facts

  • Barium compounds produce the most brilliant green color in fireworks – unmistakable and vivid
  • A "barium meal" (barium sulfate) is used to coat the digestive tract for X-rays – it's opaque to X-rays but safe because it's insoluble
  • The name comes from Greek "barys" (heavy) – some barium minerals are twice as dense as similar minerals
  • Barium metal is so reactive it must be stored under oil or in an inert atmosphere
  • Soluble barium compounds are highly toxic – they interfere with potassium channels in cells
  • "Bologna stones" (barite) from Italy were found to glow after exposure to light – an early form of phosphorescence

Atomic Structure

56 protons, 81 neutrons, 56 electrons
