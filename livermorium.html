Livermorium (Lv)

Atomic Number: 116

Basic Information

Symbol: Lv
Atomic Mass: [293] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Post-Transition Metal (predicted)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted)
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰ 7s² 7p⁴ (predicted)

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive
  • Named after Lawrence Livermore Lab
  • Predicted to be a volatile metal
  • Very short half-life (milliseconds)
  • Only a few atoms ever created

Discovery & History

Discovered in 2000 by a joint team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, and Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, USA. They bombarded curium-248 with calcium-48 ions.

Named after Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in California, which collaborated on the discovery of many superheavy elements.

Historical Timeline:
  • 2000: First atoms produced at Dubna
  • 2011: Confirmed by IUPAC
  • 2012: Officially named livermorium
  • 21st c.: Only a few dozen atoms ever created

Interesting Facts

  • Livermorium is named after Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory – the second element named after a lab (after dubnium)
  • Livermorium-293 has a half-life of about 60 milliseconds – barely enough time to detect it before it decays
  • Relativistic effects may make livermorium behave more like a noble gas than a metal
  • It's in the same group as oxygen and polonium – but its chemistry is purely theoretical
  • The discovery was a collaboration between Russian and American scientists – a rare example of Cold War cooperation in science
  • Only about 50 atoms of livermorium have ever been made – all have decayed in fractions of a second

Atomic Structure

116 protons, 177 neutrons (in 293Lv), 116 electrons
