Argon (Ar)

Atomic Number: 18

Basic Information

Symbol: Ar
Atomic Mass: 39.948 u
Category: Noble Gas
State at Room Temperature: Gas
Electron Configuration:
[Ne] 3s² 3p⁶

Key Properties

  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless noble gas
  • Emits lilac/violet light when electrified
  • Completely inert – forms no stable compounds
  • Third most abundant gas in Earth's atmosphere (0.93%)
  • Boils at -185.8°C, melts at -189.3°C

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1894 by Lord Rayleigh and Sir William Ramsay in Scotland. They noticed that nitrogen from air was denser than nitrogen from chemical sources.

The name comes from Greek "argos" meaning "lazy" or "inactive" – referring to its chemical inertness.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1785: Henry Cavendish suspects an unreactive gas in air
  • 1894: Rayleigh and Ramsay isolate argon
  • 1904: Nobel Prize for the discovery
  • 20th c.: Argon becomes vital for welding and light bulbs

Common Uses

Incandescent light bulbs (fill gas)
Welding (shielding gas)
Wine preservation (displaces oxygen)
Double-pane windows (insulation)
Fire extinguishers (inert gas)
Laboratory inert atmosphere
Argon lasers (blue-green)
Dry cleaning (argon plasma)

Interesting Facts

  • Argon is the most abundant noble gas in Earth's atmosphere – about 25 times more abundant than carbon dioxide
  • Blue argon lasers are used in eye surgery (LASIK) and to create holograms
  • The name "lazy" is fitting – argon doesn't react with anything under normal conditions
  • Liquid argon is used in neutrino detectors, like the massive detector at Fermilab
  • Argon-39 is used to date ice cores and groundwater up to 1 million years old
  • The purple glow of argon is familiar in "neon" signs – many signs labeled "neon" actually contain argon with mercury for blue color

Atomic Structure

18 protons, 22 neutrons, 18 electrons
