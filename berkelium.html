Berkelium (Bk)

Atomic Number: 97

Basic Information

Symbol: Bk
Atomic Mass: [247] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f⁹ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white radioactive metal, tarnishes slowly in air
  • Named after Berkeley, California, home of the University of California
  • One of the few transuranium elements with accessible +4 oxidation state
  • Exhibits brilliant green fluorescence in the +3 state
  • Extremely rare; only milligram quantities have ever been produced

Discovery & History

Discovered in December 1949 by Glenn T. Seaborg, Stanley G. Thompson, Albert Ghiorso, and Kenneth Street Jr. at the University of California, Berkeley. They bombarded americium-241 with alpha particles in the 60-inch cyclotron, producing berkelium-243.

Named after the city of Berkeley, following the tradition of naming elements after places (like americium and californium). The discovery was announced in 1950.

Interesting Facts

  • Berkelium completes the set of elements named after places (Americium after America, Berkelium after Berkeley, Californium after California).
  • Bk³⁺ ions in solution are yellow-green and exhibit brilliant green fluorescence under UV light.
  • ²⁴⁷Bk has a half-life of 1,380 years — long enough to study its chemistry, but only microscopic amounts exist.
  • Berkelium was used as the target to create element 117 (tennessine) by bombarding with calcium-48.
  • It's one of the few actinides where the +4 oxidation state is stable in aqueous solution (as Bk⁴⁺).
  • Total global production is measured in milligrams per year, primarily at Oak Ridge National Laboratory.

Atomic Structure

97 protons, 150 neutrons (in 247Bk), 97 electrons

Try These Berkelium Reactions

Berkelium exhibits both +3 and +4 oxidation states in solution — unusual among later actinides. The +3 state (yellow-green) is most stable, while +4 (orange) can be obtained using strong oxidants.

Experiment Idea: Berkelium(III) solutions show a characteristic yellow-green color and bright green fluorescence. Oxidation to Bk(IV) (orange) can be achieved using sodium bromate in nitric acid. The +4 state can be extracted into organic solvents like HDEHP, allowing separation from trivalent actinides. All work requires specialized hot cells due to intense radioactivity.
