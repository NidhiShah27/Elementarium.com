Rhenium (Re)

Atomic Number: 75

Basic Information

Symbol: Re
Atomic Mass: 186.207 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 7)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁵ 6s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, dense, lustrous refractory metal [citation:1]
  • 3rd highest melting point: 3186°C (after W & C) [citation:5]
  • Exceptional corrosion and wear resistance [citation:3]
  • Highest boiling point of any element: 5596°C [citation:7]
  • Superconducting below 1.7 K [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1925 by Walter Noddack, Ida Tacke, and Otto Berg in Germany. They detected it in platinum ores and columbite using X-ray spectroscopy. It was the last stable, non-radioactive element to be discovered [citation:4][citation:8].

The name comes from the Rhine River in Germany ("Rhenus" in Latin). Ida Tacke (later Noddack) was one of the few women scientists of her era and also proposed the idea of nuclear fission years before it was confirmed [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1925: Noddack, Tacke, Berg discover rhenium [citation:4]
  • 1928: Extract 1g from 660 kg of molybdenite [citation:1]
  • 1940s: Rhenium catalysts for high-octane gasoline [citation:3]
  • 21st c.: Superalloys for jet engines primary use [citation:8]

Common Uses

Ion implantation & semiconductors [citation:8]
Gyroscope components in aerospace [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Rhenium is one of the rarest elements in Earth's crust – average concentration about 1 part per billion. It's the last discovered stable element and was the "missing" element in the periodic table for decades [citation:2].
  • Rhenium has the third-highest melting point (3186°C) and the highest boiling point (5596°C) of any element. It retains its strength at temperatures that would melt most metals [citation:3].
  • Jet engines couldn't achieve today's fuel efficiency without rhenium. Adding rhenium to nickel-based superalloys allows turbine blades to operate at temperatures approaching 2000°C [citation:5].
  • The "rhenium effect" in petroleum refining: Rhenium-platinum catalysts revolutionized the production of lead-free, high-octane gasoline. The rhenium dramatically extends catalyst life and efficiency [citation:7].
  • Some high-end fountain pen nibs are tipped with rhenium-tungsten alloys because they're incredibly hard and wear-resistant, providing a smooth writing experience that lasts decades [citation:1].
  • Rhenium was the last element to be discovered in nature – all elements after it (atomic numbers 84 and above) were first produced artificially before being found in trace amounts [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

75 protons, 111 neutrons (in 186Re), 75 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): Rhenium's colorful oxides are a visual treat. Rhenium(VII) oxide (Re₂O₇) is a pale yellow solid that is highly hygroscopic. When dissolved in water, it forms perrhenic acid (HReO₄), which can be reduced to produce a series of colored rhenium complexes in lower oxidation states – from green Re(IV) to red Re(VI). The variety of colors reflects the complex d-orbital electron transitions available to this late transition metal [citation:1].
