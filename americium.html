Americium (Am)

Atomic Number: 95

Basic Information

Symbol: Am
Atomic Mass: [243] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f⁷ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, radioactive metal that tarnishes slowly in air
  • Named after the Americas, by analogy with europium
  • First element produced in macroscopic quantities by neutron irradiation
  • Glows faintly red in the dark due to intense alpha radiation
  • Most common household radioactive element (in smoke detectors)

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1944 by Glenn T. Seaborg, Ralph A. James, Leon O. Morgan, and Albert Ghiorso at the University of Chicago (Metallurgical Laboratory) during the Manhattan Project. They bombarded plutonium-239 with neutrons to create plutonium-240, which then captured another neutron and decayed to americium-241.

The discovery was kept secret until 1945. Americium-241 became commercially available in the 1960s and is now produced by neutron irradiation of plutonium in nuclear reactors.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1944: First synthesis at University of Chicago
  • 1945: Discovery announced on a children's radio quiz show
  • 1962: First smoke detectors using ²⁴¹Am commercialized
  • Modern: Millions of smoke detectors worldwide contain americium

Common Uses

Ionization smoke detectors (²⁴¹Am)
Industrial gauges (thickness, density)

Interesting Facts

  • Americium is named after the Americas, following the naming convention of its lanthanide analogue europium (named after Europe).
  • A typical smoke detector contains about 0.3 micrograms of ²⁴¹AmO₂, producing about 37 kBq of alpha activity.
  • ²⁴¹Am has a half-life of 432 years, decaying by alpha emission to ²³⁷Np.
  • It's the only synthetic element found in many household items (smoke detectors).
  • Americium-242m has one of the highest thermal neutron fission cross sections of any nuclide.
  • Global production is only a few hundred kilograms per year, mostly for smoke detectors and industrial uses.

Atomic Structure

95 protons, 148 neutrons (in 243Am), 95 electrons

Try These Americium Reactions

Americium chemistry is dominated by the +3 oxidation state, similar to its lanthanide analogue europium. The +4 and +5 states can be produced under strongly oxidizing conditions but are less stable.

Experiment Idea: In solution, Am³⁺ ions are pink or red (like its lanthanide analog Eu³⁺). Americium dioxide (AmO₂) is the common form in smoke detectors. Under strong oxidizing conditions (e.g., peroxydisulfate), Am(III) can be oxidized to Am(V) as AmO₂⁺, which has a distinct yellow-green color. Because of its high specific activity, all work requires gloveboxes and shielding.
