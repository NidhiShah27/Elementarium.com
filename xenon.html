Xenon (Xe)

Atomic Number: 54

Basic Information

Symbol: Xe
Atomic Mass: 131.293 u
Category: Noble Gas
State at Room Temperature: Gas
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p⁶

Key Properties

  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless noble gas
  • Emits bright blue light when electrified
  • One of the few noble gases that forms compounds
  • Extremely rare in Earth's atmosphere (0.09 ppm)
  • Boils at -108.1°C, melts at -111.8°C

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1898 by Sir William Ramsay and Morris Travers in London, England. They found it in the residue of evaporated liquid air.

The name comes from Greek "xenos" meaning "stranger" – because it was so rare and unexpected.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1898: Ramsay and Travers discover xenon
  • 1962: First xenon compound (XePtF₆) synthesized by Neil Bartlett
  • 1990s: Xenon becomes an anesthetic
  • 21st c.: Used in ion thrusters for spacecraft

Common Uses

High-intensity flash lamps
High-performance headlights (HID)
Xenon lasers (medical)
Electron microscopes (coating)
Ion propulsion (spacecraft)
Anesthetic (inhalation)
Neuroprotection (stroke research)
NMR spectroscopy (hyperpolarized Xe)

Interesting Facts

  • Xenon was the first noble gas to have a compound synthesized (XePtF₆) in 1962, shattering the belief that noble gases were completely inert
  • Xenon flash lamps produce brilliant white light and are used in high-speed photography, strobe lights, and camera flashes
  • Xenon is an excellent anesthetic – it's non-toxic and doesn't cause side effects, but it's too expensive for routine use
  • NASA's Dawn spacecraft used xenon ion thrusters to travel to Vesta and Ceres – it's ideal because it's heavy and easy to ionize
  • Xenon-135 is a powerful neutron absorber and affects the operation of nuclear reactors (the "xenon pit")
  • Hyperpolarized xenon is used in MRI to image lungs – it's inhaled and provides detailed images of air spaces

Atomic Structure

54 protons, 77 neutrons, 54 electrons
