Tantalum (Ta)

Atomic Number: 73

Basic Information

Symbol: Ta
Atomic Mass: 180.94788 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 5)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d³ 6s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Hard, blue-gray, lustrous transition metal [citation:1]
  • Extremely high melting point: 3017°C (5th highest) [citation:5]
  • Virtually immune to corrosion (resists all acids except HF) [citation:3]
  • Forms a protective oxide layer (Ta₂O₅) [citation:7]
  • Excellent conductor of heat and electricity [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1802 by Anders Gustaf Ekeberg in Sweden. He named it after Tantalus from Greek mythology, referring to the "tantalyzing" problem of dissolving the oxide in acids. In 1824, Jöns Jacob Berzelius produced impure tantalum metal [citation:4][citation:8].

For decades, tantalum and niobium were thought to be the same element. In 1866, Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac proved they were distinct by separating them based on their fluoride salts' solubility [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1802: Ekeberg discovers tantalum in Finnish minerals [citation:4]
  • 1824: Berzelius produces impure tantalum metal [citation:1]
  • 1903: Bolton produces ductile pure tantalum for lamp filaments [citation:3]
  • 1940s: Tantalum capacitors revolutionize electronics [citation:8]

Common Uses

Tantalum capacitors (phones, computers) [citation:3]
Surgical implants (biocompatible) [citation:2]
Superalloys for jet engine blades [citation:5]
Chemical processing equipment [citation:7]
High-refractive-index glass for lenses [citation:1]
X-ray tubes & radiation shielding [citation:4]
Luxury watches (corrosion-resistant) [citation:8]
Vacuum furnace components [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Tantalum is one of the "conflict minerals" – its mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo has been linked to funding armed conflicts. The Dodd-Frank Act now requires companies to disclose their tantalum sources [citation:2].
  • Tantalum carbide (TaC) combined with hafnium carbide (HfC) forms the highest-melting-point material known (approx. 4,263 K or 3,990°C) [citation:3].
  • Tantalum is so biocompatible that it's used for cranial plates and "tantalum screws" that can be left in the body permanently. Bone tissue actually grows into porous tantalum implants, creating a strong bond [citation:5].
  • Your smartphone contains hundreds of tantalum capacitors – they store and release energy quickly, enabling the flash to fire and circuits to stabilize [citation:7].
  • The mythical Tantalus was punished by the gods – he stood in water that receded when he tried to drink, and fruit that pulled away when he reached. Ekeberg named the element because its oxide refused to "drink up" (dissolve) in acids [citation:1].
  • Tantalum was used for incandescent lamp filaments before tungsten replaced it – it could be drawn into fine wires and had a high melting point [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

73 protons, 108 neutrons (in 181Ta), 73 electrons

Try These Tantalum Reactions

Tantalum exhibits the +5 oxidation state almost exclusively. It is famously resistant to chemical attack, but reacts with fused alkalis and some nonmetals at high temperatures [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): Tantalum's legendary corrosion resistance can be demonstrated by comparing its behavior with titanium or stainless steel. A piece of tantalum immersed in hot aqua regia (which dissolves gold and platinum) will remain completely unchanged. However, if you add a few drops of hydrofluoric acid (HF), the oxide layer dissolves and the metal rapidly reacts – a dramatic illustration of why HF is used in tantalum processing and why it's the only common acid that attacks it [citation:1].
