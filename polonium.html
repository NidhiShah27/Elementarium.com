Polonium (Po)

Atomic Number: 84

Basic Information

Symbol: Po
Atomic Mass: [209] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Metalloid
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p⁴

Key Properties

  • Highly radioactive – glows blue
  • Discovered by Marie Curie (named after Poland)
  • Alpha emitter – dangerous if ingested
  • Low melting point (254°C)
  • Extremely rare in nature (uranium ores)

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1898 by Marie and Pierre Curie in Paris, France. They isolated it from pitchblende (uranium ore) and named it after Marie's homeland, Poland.

It was the first element discovered by the Curies and helped establish the theory of radioactivity.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1898: Curies discover polonium
  • 1902: Isolated in pure form
  • 1940s: Produced artificially in reactors
  • 2006: Polonium-210 used in poisoning of Alexander Litvinenko

Common Uses

Static eliminators (industrial)
Anti-static brushes
Neutron sources (with beryllium)
No consumer applications (too dangerous)
Heat sources for space probes
Scientific research
Historic use in early nuclear physics
Calibration of radiation detectors

Interesting Facts

  • Polonium is named after Poland – Marie Curie's homeland, which was not an independent country at the time
  • Polonium-210 is 250,000 times more toxic than cyanide by weight – a microgram can be lethal
  • It glows blue because its radiation excites surrounding air molecules – the glow is not from the metal itself
  • Polonium occurs naturally in tiny amounts in uranium ores (about 100 micrograms per ton)
  • The Curies named it polonium before Poland regained independence – a patriotic gesture
  • In 2006, Alexander Litvinenko was poisoned with polonium-210 in London – it was detected by its gamma emissions

Atomic Structure

84 protons, 125 neutrons (in 209Po), 84 electrons
