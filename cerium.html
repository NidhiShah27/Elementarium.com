Cerium (Ce)

Atomic Number: 58

Basic Information

Symbol: Ce
Atomic Mass: 140.116 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹ 5d¹ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Iron-gray, ductile, rare earth metal
  • Most abundant rare earth element
  • Pyrophoric; filings ignite in air
  • Named after the dwarf planet Ceres
  • Exhibits +4 oxidation state in solution

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1803 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth in Germany, and independently by Jöns Jacob Berzelius and Wilhelm Hisinger in Sweden, in the mineral cerite.

The oxide "ceria" was named after the recently discovered asteroid Ceres, following the naming pattern of "yttria" from Ytterby.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1803: Isolated as oxide (ceria)
  • 1875: First relatively pure metal produced
  • 1903: Used in gas mantles (Welsbach mantle)
  • Modern: Key in catalytic converters and polishing powders

Common Uses

Catalytic converters
Lighter flints (mischmetal)
Glass polishing powder
Gas mantles
Self-cleaning ovens
UV blocking in sunglasses
Arc lamp electrodes
Oxidation catalyst

Interesting Facts

  • Cerium is the only rare earth element that exhibits a +4 oxidation state in aqueous solution (Ce⁴⁺), making it useful as an oxidizer.
  • Cerium(IV) oxide (ceria) switches between Ce₂O₃ and CeO₂, enabling oxygen storage in catalytic converters.
  • It tarnishes quickly in air; a cm-sized piece can completely disintegrate over months.
  • Natural cerium is a mix of four isotopes: 136Ce, 138Ce, 140Ce (88.5%), and 142Ce.
  • Mischmetal (containing ~50% cerium) produces sparks when scraped – that's why lighter flints work.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is 68 ppm, similar to copper and more common than lead.

Atomic Structure

58 protons, 82 neutrons (in 140Ce), 58 electrons

Try These Cerium Reactions

Cerium is highly reactive, especially in its finely divided form. It combines with oxygen, halogens, and acids. Its +4 oxide is a strong oxidizer.
