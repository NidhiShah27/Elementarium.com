Radon (Rn)

Atomic Number: 86

Basic Information

Symbol: Rn
Atomic Mass: [222] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Noble Gas
State at Room Temperature: Gas
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p⁶

Key Properties

  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless noble gas
  • Glows yellow/red when cooled (phosphorescence)
  • Radioactive – all isotopes decay
  • Dense – heaviest known gas (9.7× air)
  • Forms compounds only with fluorine (RnF₂)

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1900 by Friedrich Ernst Dorn in Germany. He noticed that radium compounds emitted a radioactive gas, which he called "radium emanation."

The name comes from "radium" + "-on" suffix for noble gases. Ramsay and Gray isolated it in 1908 and named it "niton" (from Latin "nitens" – shining), but later renamed to radon.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1900: Dorn discovers radon as a gas from radium
  • 1908: Ramsay and Gray isolate pure radon
  • 1923: IUPAC officially names it radon
  • 1980s: Health concerns about indoor radon recognized

Common Uses

Earthquake prediction (groundwater monitoring)
Scientific research (noble gas compounds)
Hydrology (tracing groundwater)
Calibration of radiation detectors
Radon testing (health & safety)
Atmospheric tracer studies
Historic use in cancer therapy (radon seeds)
No commercial uses (too dangerous)

Interesting Facts

  • Radon is the heaviest known gas – it's about 9.7 times denser than air. A balloon filled with radon would sink rapidly!
  • Radon glows yellow at low temperatures (due to radioactivity), turning orange-red as it gets colder
  • Radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer (after smoking) – it accumulates in basements from uranium in soil
  • Radon is the only radioactive noble gas and has no stable isotopes – all are radioactive
  • Marie Curie's laboratory notebooks are still radioactive today due to radium and radon contamination
  • In the early 1900s, "radon spas" were fashionable – people paid to breathe radioactive air, thinking it was healthy!

Atomic Structure

86 protons, 136 neutrons (in 222Rn), 86 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): Radon is too dangerous for any home experiment. However, you can buy radon test kits to check your home's air – they use activated charcoal to adsorb radon, which is then measured in a lab. This is the most common "interaction" with radon for most people.
