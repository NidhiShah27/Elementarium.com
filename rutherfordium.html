Rutherfordium (Rf)

Atomic Number: 104

Basic Information

Symbol: Rf
Atomic Mass: [267] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 4)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2][citation:5]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d² 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 2100°C (predicted) [citation:2][citation:8]
  • Density: ~23 g/cm³ (estimated, among highest) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of hafnium (Group 4) [citation:1][citation:2]
  • Forms volatile tetrachloride (RfCl₄), similar to Zr and Hf [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First reported in 1964 by a team led by Georgy Flerov at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Dubna, Russia. They bombarded plutonium-242 with neon-22 ions and detected a spontaneous fission activity [citation:1][citation:8].

In 1969, a team led by Albert Ghiorso at Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory (LBL) conclusively synthesized rutherfordium by bombarding californium-249 with carbon-12 ions, producing 257Rf and identifying its alpha decay [citation:1][citation:8].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1964: Dubna team reports element 104, proposes name "kurchatovium" [citation:4]
  • 1969: Berkeley team confirms synthesis, proposes name "rutherfordium" [citation:8]
  • 1992: IUPAC concludes both teams contributed; credit shared [citation:1]
  • 1997: IUPAC officially names element 104 rutherfordium [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Rutherfordium is the first "transactinide" element – the first element beyond the actinide series (lawrencium, element 103). Its chemistry confirms the continuation of the periodic table into unexplored territory [citation:1][citation:8].
  • The naming dispute between Soviet and American scientists was one of the most intense in element history – the Russians wanted "kurchatovium" (Ku) after Igor Kurchatov, while Americans insisted on "rutherfordium" after Ernest Rutherford. It wasn't resolved until 1997 [citation:4].
  • Chemical experiments with rutherfordium are incredibly challenging – only a few atoms at a time can be produced, and they must be studied within seconds before they decay. Using fast gas chromatography, scientists have shown that RfCl₄ is more volatile than HfCl₄, confirming its group 4 identity [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 267Rf, has a half-life of about 48 minutes – long enough for chemical studies, but still only a handful of atoms have ever been made [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects are predicted to influence rutherfordium's chemistry – electrons near the heavy nucleus move so fast that their mass increases, potentially altering bond lengths and reactivity compared to lighter group 4 elements [citation:2].
  • The name "unnilquadium" (Unq) was used temporarily by IUPAC based on Latin/Greek roots for 1-0-4. This systematic naming system was used for undiscovered or disputed elements until official names were assigned [citation:4][citation:9].

Atomic Structure

104 protons, 163 neutrons (in 267Rf), 104 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic rutherfordium chemistry experiment uses gas-phase chromatography to compare RfCl₄ with HfCl₄ and ZrCl₄. Atoms of 261Rf (half-life ~65 seconds) are produced in a nuclear reaction, quickly converted to chlorides by reaction with ZrCl₄ gas, and passed through a chromatography column. By measuring where they deposit (temperature gradient), scientists determine volatility – RfCl₄ is found to be more volatile than HfCl₄, confirming relativistic effects and group 4 placement [citation:1].
