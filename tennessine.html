Tennessine (Ts)

Atomic Number: 117

Basic Information

Symbol: Ts
Atomic Mass: [294] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Halogen (predicted)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted)
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰ 7s² 7p⁵ (predicted)

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive
  • Named after Tennessee, USA
  • Predicted to be metallic, not a typical halogen
  • Very short half-life (milliseconds)
  • Only a few atoms ever created

Discovery & History

Discovered in 2010 by a joint team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, and Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee, USA. They bombarded berkelium-249 with calcium-48 ions.

Named after Tennessee, home of Oak Ridge National Laboratory, which produced the berkelium target.

Historical Timeline:
  • 2010: First atoms produced at Dubna
  • 2015: Confirmed by IUPAC
  • 2016: Officially named tennessine
  • 21st c.: Only a few dozen atoms ever created

Interesting Facts

  • Tennessine is the second element named after a U.S. state (after californium) – honoring Tennessee's contributions to nuclear science
  • Relativistic effects may make tennessine behave more like a metal than a halogen – it might not form salts like other halogens
  • The berkelium target used to create tennessine was produced at Oak Ridge – it took years to create enough berkelium-249
  • Tennessine-294 has a half-life of about 50 milliseconds – barely enough time to detect it before it decays
  • It completes the 7th period of the periodic table – the last halogen discovered
  • Only about 15 atoms of tennessine have ever been made – all have decayed in fractions of a second

Atomic Structure

117 protons, 177 neutrons (in 294Ts), 117 electrons
