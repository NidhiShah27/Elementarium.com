Vanadium (V)

Atomic Number: 23

Basic Information

Symbol: V
Atomic Mass: 50.9415 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 5)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d³ 4s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Medium-hard, ductile, silvery-grey metal [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1910°C, Boiling point: 3407°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance (forms protective oxide) [citation:3]
  • Multiple oxidation states, leading to colorful compounds [citation:7]
  • Not found free in nature; occurs in ~65 different minerals [citation:2]

Discovery & History

First discovered in 1801 by Andrés Manuel del Río in Mexico. He found it in a lead mineral and named it "erythronium" because its salts turned red when heated [citation:4][citation:8]. However, French chemists incorrectly claimed it was impure chromium [citation:1].

Rediscovered in 1830 by Nils Gabriel Sefström in Sweden, who named it vanadium after Vanadís, the Norse goddess of beauty and fertility, because of its beautiful multicolored compounds [citation:4].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1801: Del Río discovers "erythronium" [citation:4]
  • 1830: Sefström rediscovers and names vanadium [citation:1]
  • 1867: Henry Enfield Roscoe produces pure vanadium metal [citation:8]
  • 1905: Vanadium steel first used in Ford cars [citation:3]

Common Uses

High-strength steel alloys (springs, tools) [citation:3]
Titanium alloys (Ti-6Al-4V for aerospace) [citation:2]
Vanadium redox flow batteries (grid storage) [citation:5]
Catalyst for sulfuric acid production [citation:7]
Nuclear applications (low neutron capture) [citation:1]
Pigments and ceramics (vanadium glazes) [citation:4]
Superconductors (V₃Ga, V₃Si) [citation:8]
Armor plating and tools [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Vanadium is named after Vanadís, the Norse goddess of beauty, because its compounds come in a rainbow of colors – yellow, green, blue, violet, and red, depending on oxidation state [citation:4].
  • The first industrial use of vanadium was in the Ford Model T. Henry Ford was convinced by French racing cars to use vanadium steel, which was stronger and lighter than conventional steel [citation:3].
  • Vanadium redox flow batteries are a promising technology for grid-scale energy storage. They can store enormous amounts of energy and be recharged simply by replacing the electrolyte [citation:5].
  • Vanadium is essential to some living organisms. Certain sea squirts (ascidians) concentrate vanadium in their blood to a concentration over a million times higher than in seawater – though its function is still unknown [citation:2].
  • Vanadium dioxide (VO₂) undergoes a dramatic phase change at 68°C, switching from an insulating to a conducting state. This property is being researched for "smart windows" that block infrared heat on hot days [citation:7].
  • Despite its abundance in the Earth's crust (about 100 ppm), vanadium is difficult to extract and was not produced in pure form until 1867, over 60 years after its discovery [citation:8].

Atomic Structure

23 protons, 28 neutrons (in 51V), 23 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The "vanadium rainbow" is a classic demonstration of oxidation states. Start with ammonium vanadate (NH₄VO₃) in acidic solution (yellow VO₂⁺, V⁵⁺). Reduce with zinc metal: first blue (VO²⁺, V⁴⁺), then green (V³⁺), and finally violet (V²⁺). Each reduction step produces a distinct color, showing the progression of oxidation states. Re-oxidize by shaking in air to see the sequence reverse – a beautiful display of vanadium's colorful chemistry [citation:1].
