Caesium (Cs)

Atomic Number: 55

Basic Information

Symbol: Cs
Atomic Mass: 132.90545 u
Category: Alkali Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid (melts at 28.5°C)
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 6s¹

Key Properties

  • Most reactive of all stable alkali metals
  • Melts in your hand (just above room temp)
  • Explodes on contact with water
  • Golden-colored metal (unlike silvery others)
  • Used in the most accurate atomic clocks

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1860 by Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff in Germany using spectroscopy – the first element discovered by this method.

The name comes from Latin "caesius" meaning sky blue, for the bright blue lines in its spectrum.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1860: Bunsen & Kirchhoff discover caesium
  • 1882: First isolated by Carl Setterberg
  • 1967: SI second defined by caesium clock
  • Today: Essential for GPS and timekeeping

Common Uses

Atomic clocks (GPS, internet)
Drilling fluids (caesium formate)
Laboratory research
Photoelectric cells
Cancer treatment (Cs-137)
Ion propulsion (experimental)
Getter in vacuum tubes
Biochemical ultracentrifugation

Interesting Facts

  • The official definition of the second is based on caesium-133: 9,192,631,770 cycles of its microwave radiation
  • Caesium melts at 28.5°C – it would liquefy in your hand on a warm day!
  • It's the most reactive metal – it ignites spontaneously in air and explodes in water
  • Caesium is one of only three metals that are not silvery-white (it's gold-colored)
  • Caesium-137 is a dangerous radioactive byproduct of nuclear fallout (from Chernobyl and Fukushima)
  • Plants absorb caesium like potassium – it can enter the food chain after nuclear accidents

Atomic Structure

55 protons, 78 neutrons, 55 electrons
