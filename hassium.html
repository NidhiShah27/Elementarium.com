Hassium (Hs)

Atomic Number: 108

Basic Information

Symbol: Hs
Atomic Mass: [269] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 8)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d⁶ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~2400°C (predicted, similar to osmium) [citation:8]
  • Density: ~41 g/cm³ (estimated, highest of all elements) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of osmium (Group 8) [citation:1]
  • Forms volatile tetroxide (HsO₄), similar to OsO₄ [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First synthesized in 1984 by a team led by Peter Armbruster and Gottfried Münzenberg at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. They bombarded lead-208 with iron-58 ions, producing 265Hs and identifying its alpha decay [citation:1][citation:8].

The reaction used was a "cold fusion" approach, where the compound nucleus has relatively low excitation energy, increasing the chance of survival [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1984: GSI team conclusively synthesizes and identifies element 108 [citation:1]
  • 1992: IUPAC credits GSI with discovery [citation:1]
  • 1994: IUPAC recommends name "hahnium" for element 108 (later changed) [citation:4]
  • 1997: IUPAC officially names element 108 hassium (after Hesse, Germany) [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Hassium is named after the Latin name for the German state of Hesse ("Hassia"), where the GSI laboratory is located. It's one of the few elements named after a German state rather than a person or place [citation:4].
  • With a predicted density of about 41 g/cm³, hassium is expected to be the densest element of all – even denser than osmium (22.59 g/cm³). This is due to relativistic effects contracting the atomic radius [citation:5].
  • Hassium was the first superheavy element where chemical studies were performed using its tetroxide, HsO₄. In 2001, scientists successfully synthesized HsO₄ and studied its volatility, finding it behaved similarly to OsO₄ – confirming its place in Group 8 [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 277Hs, has a half-life of about 11 minutes – long enough for chemical studies. This isotope is produced in the decay chain of 285Fl (flerovium) [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects in hassium are predicted to stabilize the +8 oxidation state, similar to osmium. However, the 6d orbitals are significantly expanded, which may affect the volatility and reactivity of HsO₄ compared to OsO₄ [citation:2].
  • The synthesis of hassium is extremely difficult – the fusion of lead-208 and iron-58 has a very low cross-section. In their original experiment, the GSI team observed only three atoms of 265Hs after two weeks of beam time [citation:1].

Atomic Structure

108 protons, 161 neutrons (in 269Hs), 108 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic hassium chemistry experiment uses gas-phase chromatography to compare HsO₄ with OsO₄. Atoms of 269Hs (half-life ~10 seconds) are produced in a nuclear reaction, rapidly oxidized in a hot gas stream containing O₂, and passed through a chromatography column. By measuring deposition temperatures, scientists found HsO₄ is slightly less volatile than OsO₄ – confirming relativistic effects and group 8 placement. This was the first chemical study of an element heavier than 107, and it beautifully demonstrated the power of single-atom chemistry [citation:1].
