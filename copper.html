Copper (Cu)

Atomic Number: 29

Basic Information

Symbol: Cu
Atomic Mass: 63.546 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 11)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Reddish-orange, soft and malleable metal [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1085°C, Boiling point: 2562°C [citation:5]
  • Highest electrical conductivity of any metal (except silver) [citation:3]
  • Develops green patina (verdigris) when weathered [citation:7]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance and antimicrobial [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Copper has been known since prehistoric times – one of the first metals used by humans. Artifacts date to around 9000 BCE in the Middle East. The name comes from Latin "cuprum," derived from the island of Cyprus [citation:4][citation:8].

The Bronze Age began when copper was alloyed with tin (around 3500 BCE). Ancient civilizations in Mesopotamia, Egypt, and China used copper for tools, ornaments, and coinage [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • ~9000 BCE: Oldest copper artifacts (Middle East) [citation:4]
  • ~3500 BCE: Smelting developed; Bronze Age begins [citation:1]
  • Roman era: Extensive mining in Cyprus; named "aes Cyprium" [citation:3]
  • 19th c.: Electrical revolution makes copper vital [citation:8]

Interesting Facts

  • Copper is 100% recyclable without any loss of quality – nearly 80% of all copper ever mined is still in use today. This makes it one of the most sustainable metals [citation:2].
  • The Statue of Liberty is made of copper (about 80 tons) and gets its green color from a protective patina layer (basic copper carbonate) that shields the metal underneath [citation:4].
  • Copper has natural antimicrobial properties – it's used for hospital touch surfaces (doorknobs, handrails) to reduce the spread of germs. Copper alloys kill bacteria within hours [citation:3].
  • Copper is essential for human health – it's a key component of enzymes involved in iron metabolism, nerve function, and connective tissue formation. Copper deficiency can cause anemia [citation:5].
  • If you could draw all the copper wire in an average modern car, it would stretch over 1 mile (1.6 km). Electric vehicles contain even more copper [citation:7].
  • Ancient Egyptians used copper compounds for eye makeup and medicinal ointments as early as 3000 BCE. Copper sulfate was used as an antiseptic and fungicide [citation:8].

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic "silver mirror" reaction with copper is actually the formation of a copper mirror. But even more striking is the reaction of copper with ammonia. Copper(II) sulfate solution is pale blue. Add ammonia, and it forms the deep blue-violet complex [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺. If you then add a reducing agent like glucose, it can form copper(I) oxide – a yellow or red precipitate. This demonstrates copper's two oxidation states and its ability to form colorful coordination complexes [citation:1].
