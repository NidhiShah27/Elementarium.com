Actinium (Ac)

Atomic Number: 89

Basic Information

Symbol: Ac
Atomic Mass: [227] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 6d¹ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Intensely radioactive, silvery-white metal
  • First element in the actinide series
  • Named from Greek 'aktinos' meaning beam or ray
  • Glows blue in the dark due to ionizing radiation
  • About 150 times more radioactive than radium

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1899 by French chemist André-Louis Debierne. He extracted it from pitchblende and named it after the Greek word "aktis" (beam). Independently rediscovered in 1902 by Friedrich Oskar Giesel, who called it "emanium".

Debierne was a colleague of Marie Curie, and actinium's discovery followed closely after polonium and radium. Its intense radioactivity made it significant for early nuclear studies.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1899: Debierne discovers actinium in Paris
  • 1902: Giesel independently isolates it
  • 1940s: First microscopic samples produced
  • Modern: Used in neutron sources and cancer therapy

Interesting Facts

  • Actinium gives its name to the entire actinide series – elements 89 to 103.
  • It glows pale blue in the dark due to its intense radioactivity exciting surrounding air molecules.
  • Found in trace amounts in uranium ores (about 0.15 mg per ton of pitchblende).
  • ²²⁷Ac has a half-life of 21.7 years and decays primarily via beta emission.
  • It is 150 times more radioactive than radium, making it extremely hazardous to handle.
  • Only about 100 grams of actinium exist in the entire Earth's crust at any time.

Atomic Structure

89 protons, 138 neutrons (in 227Ac), 89 electrons
