Thallium (Tl)

Atomic Number: 81

Basic Information

Symbol: Tl
Atomic Mass: 204.383 u
Category: Post-Transition Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p¹

Key Properties

  • Burns with brilliant green flame
  • Very soft – can be cut with a knife
  • Highly toxic – more dangerous than lead
  • Conducts electricity well
  • Named for its green spectral line (Greek "thallos" – green twig)

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1861 by Sir William Crookes in England using spectroscopy. He observed a bright green line in the spectrum of selenium compounds.

Independently discovered by Claude-Auguste Lamy in 1862, who first isolated the metal.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1861: Crookes discovers thallium spectroscopically
  • 1862: Lamy isolates pure thallium
  • 1920s: Used in rat poisons and insecticides
  • 1970s: Use restricted due to toxicity

Common Uses

Optical lenses (thallium bromide-iodide)
Infrared detectors
Low-melting-point glass
Nuclear medicine (Tl-201)
High-temperature superconductors
Rodenticides (historical, now banned)
Alloys (bearings, switches)
Fireworks (green color)

Interesting Facts

  • Thallium is extremely toxic – it was called "the poisoner's poison" because it's tasteless, odorless, and symptoms mimic other illnesses
  • Thallium compounds produce a brilliant green flame – used in fireworks and flares
  • The name comes from the Greek "thallos" meaning green twig – for its bright green spectral line
  • Thallium-201 is used in nuclear medicine for cardiac stress tests – it's taken up by healthy heart tissue
  • Like lead, thallium is soft and can be cut with a knife – fresh surfaces have a metallic luster that quickly tarnishes
  • Thallium bromide-iodide (KRS-5) crystals are used in infrared optics because they're transparent to IR light

Atomic Structure

81 protons, 123 neutrons, 81 electrons
