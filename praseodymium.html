Praseodymium (Pr)

Atomic Number: 59

Basic Information

Symbol: Pr
Atomic Mass: 140.90766 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f³ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Soft, silvery-yellowish metal
  • Salts are pale green
  • One naturally occurring stable isotope (141Pr)
  • Pyrophoric in finely divided form
  • Paramagnetic at room temperature

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1885 by Austrian chemist Carl Auer von Welsbach. He separated the previously thought element "didymium" into praseodymium and neodymium.

Name comes from Greek prasios (green) + didymos (twin), referring to the green salts of its oxide and its twin relationship with neodymium.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1841: Mosander extracts "didymium" (mixture)
  • 1885: Auer von Welsbach splits didymium into Pr & Nd
  • 1931: First relatively pure praseodymium metal produced
  • Modern: Key component in superalloys, magnets, and optics

Common Uses

Ceramic pigments (praseodymium yellow)
Welder's goggles
Alloy with magnesium
Mischmetal for lighter flints
Permanent magnets
Fiber optic amplifiers
UV-blocking glass
Solid-state lasers

Interesting Facts

  • Praseodymium gives the yellow color to "didymium" glass, used in welder's goggles to absorb the bright yellow sodium flare.
  • Its name literally means "green twin" – green because its salts are green, twin because it was born from didymium.
  • 141Pr is the only natural isotope; it is stable, but 38 radioisotopes have been synthesized.
  • When scratched, praseodymium chips can ignite – it's part of pyrophoric mischmetal.
  • Praseodymium oxide (Pr₆O₁₁) has a complex structure with mixed +3/+4 oxidation states.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is ~9.5 ppm, about as common as lithium.

Atomic Structure

59 protons, 82 neutrons (in 141Pr), 59 electrons

Try These Praseodymium Reactions

Praseodymium is quite electropositive. It reacts with oxygen, halogens, and acids, forming mostly green Pr(III) compounds.

Experiment Idea: Praseodymium metal reacts slowly with cold water but rapidly with hot water, producing hydrogen gas. Finely divided metal can ignite in air. Its compounds are characteristically green.
