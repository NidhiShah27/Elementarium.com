Molybdenum (Mo)

Atomic Number: 42

Basic Information

Symbol: Mo
Atomic Mass: 95.95 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 6)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d⁵ 5s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, very hard refractory metal [citation:1]
  • Extremely high melting point: 2623°C (6th highest) [citation:5]
  • Excellent high-temperature strength [citation:3]
  • Low thermal expansion, high thermal conductivity [citation:7]
  • Essential trace element for most living organisms [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1778 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele in Sweden. He identified molybdenum as a distinct element in the mineral molybdenite (MoS₂), but only produced the oxide. Pure molybdenum metal was first isolated by Peter Jacob Hjelm in 1781 [citation:4][citation:8].

The name comes from the Greek word "molybdos" meaning lead, because its ores were often confused with lead ores. For centuries, molybdenite was thought to be a lead ore or graphite [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • Ancient: Molybdenite used as lubricant (confused with graphite) [citation:4]
  • 1778: Scheele identifies molybdenum as new element [citation:1]
  • 1781: Hjelm isolates impure molybdenum metal [citation:3]
  • 1910s: Molybdenum steel vital for WWI armor [citation:8]

Common Uses

High-strength steel alloys [citation:3]
Jet engine turbine blades [citation:2]
High-temperature furnace components [citation:5]
Lubricant additive (MoS₂) [citation:7]
Catalyst in petroleum refining [citation:1]
Electrodes in thin-film transistors [citation:4]
Heating elements in furnaces [citation:8]
Corrosion-resistant pipes [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Molybdenum is the only second-row transition metal essential for life. It's a critical component of nitrogenase, the enzyme that bacteria use to fix nitrogen from the air into ammonia – a process that sustains all life on Earth [citation:2].
  • Molybdenum has one of the highest melting points of all elements (2623°C) – only tantalum, tungsten, rhenium, osmium, and carbon are higher. It retains its strength at white-hot temperatures [citation:3].
  • The space shuttle's main engines used molybdenum alloys in their turbines and nozzles because they withstand the extreme temperatures of liquid hydrogen combustion [citation:5].
  • Molybdenum disulfide (MoS₂) is an excellent dry lubricant used in space applications where oil would evaporate or freeze. It's also used in automotive engine oils to reduce friction [citation:7].
  • The oceans contain molybdenum – it's the most abundant transition metal dissolved in seawater, though at very low concentrations (10 parts per billion) [citation:1].
  • Molybdenum was crucial to the "Haber-Bosch process" that produces ammonia fertilizer – modern iron-molybdenum catalysts enable the reaction that feeds billions of people today [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

42 protons, 54 neutrons (in 96Mo), 42 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The "blue water" reaction: When molybdenum(VI) oxide is reduced in solution, it forms "molybdenum blue" – a striking deep blue color that results from mixed-valence molybdenum oxide clusters. This classic demonstration shows the complexity of molybdenum chemistry and its multiple oxidation states. In analytical chemistry, the "molybdenum blue" method is used to detect phosphates in water. You can create it by reducing an acidic molybdate solution with a mild reducing agent like tin(II) chloride or ascorbic acid [citation:1].
