Plutonium (Pu)

Atomic Number: 94

Basic Information

Symbol: Pu
Atomic Mass: [244] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f⁶ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white metal that tarnishes rapidly, glowing red in the dark
  • Named after the dwarf planet Pluto (after Uranus and Neptune)
  • First element produced in quantity by nuclear reactors
  • Six allotropes with wildly different densities (pyrophoric in some forms)
  • Fissile isotope ²³⁹Pu is the primary material for nuclear weapons

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1940–41 by Glenn T. Seaborg, Edwin McMillan, Joseph W. Kennedy, and Arthur C. Wahl at UC Berkeley. They bombarded uranium-238 with deuterons to create neptunium-238, which beta-decayed to plutonium-238.

The discovery was kept secret until after WWII. Plutonium-239 was produced in industrial quantities at the Hanford Site and used in the "Trinity" test and the "Fat Man" bomb dropped on Nagasaki.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1940: First synthesis of ²³⁸Pu at Berkeley
  • 1944: Hanford Site begins large-scale production
  • 1945: Trinity test (July 16); Nagasaki bomb (August 9)
  • 1951: Seaborg wins Nobel Prize for transuranium work

Common Uses

Nuclear weapons (²³⁹Pu)
Nuclear reactor fuel (MOX fuel)
Radioisotope thermoelectric generators (²³⁸Pu for space probes)
Cardiac pacemaker batteries (²³⁸Pu, historical)
Neutron sources (Pu-Be)
Research in nuclear physics
Component of nuclear waste
Smoke detectors (very small amounts of ²³⁹Pu, historical)

Interesting Facts

  • Plutonium-238 emits mostly alpha particles and generates significant heat – a 1 kg sphere glows red hot from its own decay.
  • It has six allotropes at ambient pressure; the δ-phase is almost as malleable as aluminum.
  • A sphere of plutonium-239 the size of a softball weighs about 5 kg and has the explosive power of 20 kilotons of TNT.
  • Trace amounts of ²³⁹Pu are naturally produced in uranium-rich ores by neutron capture, but it's essentially a synthetic element.
  • Plutonium expands when it melts (like water), which is unusual for a metal.
  • The name follows the sequence Uranus → Neptune → Pluto, completing the planet names for actinides.

Atomic Structure

94 protons, 150 neutrons (in 244Pu), 94 electrons

Try These Plutonium Reactions

Plutonium chemistry is complex, with oxidation states from +3 to +7. The +4 state (Pu⁴⁺) is most stable in aqueous solution, but all states interconvert easily. Plutonium metal is highly reactive and pyrophoric in finely divided form.

Experiment Idea: In a glovebox, plutonium(III) solutions are blue-violet; Pu(IV) is tan or orange-brown; Pu(V) is light pink (as PuO₂⁺); Pu(VI) is pink-orange (as PuO₂²⁺). The complex aqueous chemistry makes plutonium one of the most fascinating elements to study. Because of its radioactivity and toxicity, all work requires specialized facilities and extreme caution.
