Indium (In)

Atomic Number: 49

Basic Information

Symbol: In
Atomic Mass: 114.818 u
Category: Post-Transition Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p¹

Key Properties

  • Very soft metal – can be cut with a knife
  • Cries when bent (emits a high-pitched sound)
  • Wide liquid range (156°C to 2072°C)
  • Forms a thin oxide layer that wets glass
  • Named for its indigo-blue spectral line

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1863 by Ferdinand Reich and Hieronymous Theodor Richter in Germany. They were looking for thallium in zinc ores but found a brilliant indigo line in the spectrum.

Named after the indigo color of its spectral line. Richter isolated the metal in 1864.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1863: Reich and Richter discover indium
  • 1864: Richter isolates pure indium
  • 1930s: Used in bearings and jewelry
  • 21st c.: Essential for touchscreens and solar cells

Common Uses

Touchscreen coatings (ITO)
Solar cells (CIGS)
Low-melting-point alloys
Semiconductor doping
Lead-free solders
Vacuum seals and gaskets
Dental alloys
Neutron detectors

Interesting Facts

  • Indium "cries" when bent – it emits a high-pitched squeaking sound due to crystal twinning
  • It's one of the softest metals – you can scratch it with a fingernail and cut it with a knife
  • Your smartphone touchscreen uses indium tin oxide (ITO) – it's transparent yet conductive
  • The name comes from its indigo spectral line – one of the few elements named for a color
  • Indium is used in the "Galinstan" alloy (gallium-indium-tin) that's liquid at room temperature
  • When molten, indium wets glass and forms a strong bond – used in cryogenic seals and vacuum systems

Atomic Structure

49 protons, 66 neutrons, 49 electrons
