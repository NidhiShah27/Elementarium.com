Tin (Sn)

Atomic Number: 50

Basic Information

Symbol: Sn
Atomic Mass: 118.710 u
Category: Post-Transition Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p²

Key Properties

  • "Tin cry" – audible crackling when bent
  • Tin pest – crumbles at low temperatures
  • Resists corrosion (used for food cans)
  • Forms many important alloys (bronze, solder)
  • One of the earliest metals used by humans

Discovery & History

Known since ancient times – tin has been used for over 5,000 years. The Bronze Age began when copper was alloyed with tin (~3500 BCE).

The name comes from the Anglo-Saxon "tin" and the Latin "stannum" (hence the symbol Sn).

Historical Timeline:
  • ~3500 BCE: Bronze (copper-tin alloy) ushers in Bronze Age
  • Roman era: Tin used for coins and vessels
  • 19th c.: Tin cans revolutionize food preservation
  • 20th c.: Essential for electronics (solder)

Common Uses

Tin-plated steel (food cans)
Solder (electronics, plumbing)
Bronze (with copper)
Float glass manufacturing
Pewter (tin alloy)
Lithium-ion batteries
Organ pipes and statues
Chemical reagent

Interesting Facts

  • Tin emits a crackling "cry" when bent – this is caused by crystal twinning, a sound like tearing tin foil
  • "Tin pest" is a real phenomenon – below 13°C, white tin slowly transforms to gray tin and crumbles into powder
  • Napoleon's soldiers in Russia supposedly had their tin buttons disintegrate in the cold – contributing to their defeat!
  • Float glass (window glass) is made by floating molten glass on molten tin – it creates perfectly flat surfaces
  • "Tin cans" are actually steel with a thin tin coating – the tin prevents rust
  • Tin has more stable isotopes than any other element – 10 stable isotopes!

Atomic Structure

50 protons, 69 neutrons, 50 electrons
