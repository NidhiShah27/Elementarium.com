Roentgenium (Rg)

Atomic Number: 111

Basic Information

Symbol: Rg
Atomic Mass: [282] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 11)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d⁹ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~2100°C (predicted, similar to gold) [citation:8]
  • Density: ~29 g/cm³ (estimated, very high) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of gold (Group 11) [citation:1]
  • Predicted to be a noble metal, even more resistant to corrosion than gold [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First synthesized in 1994 by a team led by Sigurd Hofmann at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. They bombarded bismuth-209 with nickel-64 ions, producing three atoms of 272Rg and identifying their alpha decay [citation:1][citation:8].

This was the third new element discovered at GSI in 1994, following darmstadtium (110) and roentgenium (111). The reaction was a "cold fusion" approach [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1994: GSI team produces and identifies element 111 [citation:1]
  • 2001: IUPAC credits GSI with discovery [citation:1]
  • 2004: IUPAC officially names element 111 roentgenium (after Wilhelm Röntgen) [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Roentgenium is named after Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen, the German physicist who discovered X-rays in 1895. This was the first Nobel Prize in Physics (1901), making it a fitting namesake [citation:4].
  • The original 1994 experiment producing 272Rg created just three atoms after several weeks of bombardment. Each atom was identified by its characteristic alpha decay chain, a remarkable feat of nuclear chemistry [citation:1].
  • Chemical studies on roentgenium are extremely challenging but have been attempted. Due to its position under gold, it is predicted to be even more noble than gold – the "most noble" metal in Group 11 [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 282Rg, has a half-life of about 2 minutes. It is produced in the decay chain of 286Nh (nihonium) and was first observed in 2009 [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects in roentgenium are predicted to be among the strongest in the periodic table. The 7s electrons are so strongly contracted and stabilized that roentgenium may have a different ground state electron configuration (6d⁸7s¹) than gold (5d¹⁰6s¹), dramatically affecting its chemistry [citation:2].
  • Roentgenium is predicted to be a noble metal, even more resistant to oxidation than gold. However, unlike gold, it may form a stable +5 oxidation state due to the availability of 6d orbitals, a unique property among Group 11 elements [citation:1].

Atomic Structure

111 protons, 171 neutrons (in 282Rg), 111 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): A classic roentgenium chemistry experiment uses gas-phase chromatography to study adsorption on a gold surface. Atoms of 282Rg (half-life 2 minutes) are produced in a nuclear reaction, thermalized, and passed through a chromatography column coated with gold. By measuring where they deposit (temperature gradient), scientists can determine the adsorption enthalpy and compare it with gold. Results show Rg is even more strongly adsorbed than gold, confirming relativistic effects and its place in Group 11 as the "most noble" metal [citation:1].
