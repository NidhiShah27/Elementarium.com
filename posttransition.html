Post‑Transition Metals

Groups 13–15 • the “poor metals”

What are Post‑Transition Metals?

Position: found immediately to the right of the transition metals (groups 13–15, periods 4–7)
Electron configuration: typically have filled d subshells (d¹⁰) and one to three p‑electrons
Typical traits:
  • Softer and lower melting than transition metals
  • Still metallic but often more covalent character
  • Exhibit variable oxidation states (commonly +3, +2, +1)
  • Tend to form amphoteric oxides
  • Many are used in alloys and electronics
  • Some are toxic (e.g. lead, thallium)

They are sometimes called “poor metals” because their metallic properties are less pronounced than those of true transition metals.

Key Properties

  • Melting points: generally low – gallium melts in your hand (29.8 °C)
  • Density: increase down the group (e.g. lead is very dense)
  • Electrical conductivity: good, but lower than transition metals
  • Amphoteric oxides: e.g. Al₂O₃, SnO₂, PbO react with both acids and bases
  • Common oxidation states: Al (+3), Ga (+3), In (+3), Sn (+2,+4), Tl (+1,+3), Pb (+2,+4), Bi (+3)
  • “Inert pair effect”: heavier elements favour lower oxidation state (+2 for Sn,Pb; +1 for Tl)
Did you know? Aluminium is the most abundant metal in Earth’s crust, yet it was once considered a precious metal (more expensive than gold in the 1850s).

Interesting facts

  • Gallium melts at 29.8 °C – it liquefies in your hand, but boils at ~2400 °C!
  • Bismuth forms stunning iridescent crystals due to an oxide layer.
  • Lead was used in ancient plumbing (the word ‘plumbing’ comes from plumbum).
  • Indium is so soft you can scratch it with a fingernail.
  • Napoleon III once served his guests with aluminium cutlery while commoners used gold – it was that rare.
  • Thallium salts were once used as rat poison (now banned in many countries).

Amphoteric oxides & the inert pair effect

Al₂O₃ (alumina) reacts with both acids and bases:

Al₂O₃ + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂O
Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH + 3H₂O → 2Na[Al(OH)₄]

Inert pair effect: heavier post‑transition metals prefer lower oxidation states.

Tl mostly +1 (not +3) • Pb mostly +2 (not +4) • Bi mostly +3 (not +5)

Abundance & production

Aluminium 8.2% crust
Lead ≈ 0.001%
Tin ≈ 0.0002%
Gallium ≈ 0.0019% (but dispersed)

Annual global production: aluminium ~70 million tonnes, lead ~12 Mt, tin ~0.3 Mt. Gallium and indium are produced mainly as by‑products of bauxite or zinc refining.
