Iridium (Ir)

Atomic Number: 77

Basic Information

Symbol: Ir
Atomic Mass: 192.217 u
Category: Transition Metal (Platinum Group)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁷ 6s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Dense, silvery-white, extremely hard and brittle [citation:1]
  • Most corrosion-resistant metal known [citation:5]
  • 2nd highest melting point of metals: 2446°C [citation:3]
  • 2nd densest element (22.56 g/cm³) after osmium [citation:7]
  • Extremely high modulus of elasticity [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1803 by Smithson Tennant in London, England, simultaneously with osmium. He found it in the black residue left after dissolving platinum ore in aqua regia. The name comes from the Greek "iris" (rainbow) [citation:4][citation:8].

The famous "Iridium Point" was the original international prototype kilogram – a 90% platinum / 10% iridium cylinder that defined the kilogram from 1889 to 2019 [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1803: Tennant discovers iridium in platinum residues [citation:4]
  • 1889: Iridium-platinum alloy for kilogram prototype [citation:1]
  • 1970s: Iridium critical for spark plugs & crucibles [citation:3]
  • 1980: Alvarez proposes iridium layer as dinosaur impact evidence [citation:8]

Common Uses

High-temperature crucibles & spark plugs [citation:3]
Hardening platinum for jewelry & tools [citation:2]
Fountain pen nib tipping (with osmium) [citation:5]
Compass bearings & watch balance wheels [citation:7]
Standard weights & measures (meter bars) [citation:1]
Catalyst for acetic acid (Cativa process) [citation:4]
Gamma-ray detectors & X-ray optics [citation:8]
Satellite & deep-sea components [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Iridium is the most corrosion-resistant element ever discovered. It is completely unaffected by any single acid, including boiling aqua regia (which dissolves gold and platinum). It only reacts with molten bases like sodium hydroxide or with chlorine at high temperatures [citation:2].
  • The Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction layer (the "K-Pg boundary") contains a thin layer of iridium-rich clay found worldwide. This discovery by Luis and Walter Alvarez in 1980 provided the first strong evidence that an asteroid impact killed the dinosaurs [citation:3].
  • The original standard kilogram, the "International Prototype Kilogram" (Le Grand K), was made of 90% platinum and 10% iridium. It was the last physical artifact used to define a base unit until 2019 [citation:5].
  • Iridium spark plugs last much longer than standard platinum plugs because iridium is six times harder and has a higher melting point. The fine iridium tip allows for better ignition and efficiency [citation:7].
  • Iridium forms beautiful, colorful salts – from yellow to red to green – which is how it got its name from the Greek goddess Iris, personification of the rainbow [citation:1].
  • Iridium is extremely rare in Earth's crust – about 1,000 times rarer than gold. Most of the iridium on Earth sank to the core during planetary formation. What little remains in the crust arrived via asteroid impacts [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

77 protons, 115 neutrons (in 192Ir), 77 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The "Cativa process" is a modern industrial showcase of iridium catalysis. It produces acetic acid (vinegar) from methanol and carbon monoxide. Unlike the older rhodium-based Monsanto process, the iridium catalyst is more stable, more efficient, and uses less water. The iridium cycles between Ir(I) and Ir(III) states, and the key intermediate, [Ir(CO)₂I₂]⁻, is a classic example of a square-planar d⁸ complex. This process now produces millions of tons of acetic acid annually [citation:1].
