Titanium (Ti)

Atomic Number: 22

Basic Information

Symbol: Ti
Atomic Mass: 47.867 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 4)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d² 4s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-grey, lustrous, strong yet lightweight metal [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1668°C, Boiling point: 3287°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance (forms TiO₂ passive layer) [citation:3]
  • High strength-to-weight ratio (strong as steel, 45% lighter) [citation:7]
  • Biocompatible and non-toxic [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1791 by William Gregor in Cornwall, England. He found a new metal in ilmenite sand and named it "menachanite" [citation:4][citation:8]. In 1795, Martin Heinrich Klaproth independently rediscovered it in rutile and named it titanium after the Titans of Greek mythology [citation:1].

Pure titanium metal was not produced until 1910 by Matthew Hunter. The Kroll process (1940s) made commercial production possible [citation:6].

Common Uses

Aerospace (airframes, jet engines) [citation:3]
Medical implants (hip replacements, dental) [citation:2]
Jewelry and watches [citation:5]
Bicycle frames, golf clubs [citation:7]
Naval ships, submarines (corrosion-resistant) [citation:1]
Chemical processing equipment [citation:4]
White pigment (TiO₂ in paint) [citation:8]
Sunscreen (TiO₂ nanoparticles) [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Titanium is the only element that burns in pure nitrogen gas. At high temperatures, it reacts with nitrogen to form titanium nitride, a hard ceramic used for coating tools [citation:3].
  • Despite being as strong as steel, titanium is 45% lighter. This makes it ideal for aerospace applications – the SR-71 Blackbird was made mostly of titanium to withstand high-speed friction [citation:5].
  • Titanium dioxide (TiO₂) is the most widely used white pigment in the world, found in paints, plastics, paper, and even food (as the whitener in toothpaste and icing) [citation:8].
  • Titanium is the ninth most abundant element in the Earth's crust, but it's expensive to produce because the Kroll process is energy-intensive and batch-based [citation:4].
  • Titanium is biocompatible – bone will actually grow onto titanium implants, making it perfect for medical uses. This property is called osseointegration [citation:2].
  • The titanium dioxide in sunscreen works as a physical blocker, reflecting UV light. Unlike chemical sunscreens, it's safe for sensitive skin and doesn't degrade in sunlight [citation:6].

Atomic Structure

22 protons, 26 neutrons (in 48Ti), 22 electrons

Try These Titanium Reactions

Titanium exhibits the +4 oxidation state primarily, but also +3 in some compounds. It is highly reactive at high temperatures but protected by a passive oxide layer at room temperature [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The "titanium volcano" demonstration shows the reactivity of titanium at high temperatures. When titanium powder is heated in air, it burns with an intense white flame, forming titanium dioxide. More dramatically, if heated in nitrogen, it forms golden titanium nitride. In the lab, the reaction of titanium tetrachloride (TiCl₄) with water produces dense white fumes of TiO₂ and HCl – a classic demonstration of the compound used in smoke screens and the production of titanium dioxide pigment [citation:1].
