Platinum (Pt)

Atomic Number: 78

Basic Information

Symbol: Pt
Atomic Mass: 195.084 u
Category: Transition Metal (Platinum Group)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁹ 6s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Dense, malleable, ductile, silvery-white precious metal [citation:1]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance – unaffected by air or single acids [citation:5]
  • One of the rarest elements in Earth's crust (0.005 ppm) [citation:3]
  • Outstanding catalytic properties [citation:7]
  • High melting point: 1768°C, density: 21.45 g/cm³ [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Platinum has been used since ancient times by pre-Columbian South Americans. It first appeared in European writings in 1557. The name comes from the Spanish "platina," meaning "little silver" [citation:4][citation:8].

In 1741, Charles Wood brought samples to England, sparking scientific interest. By the 1780s, chemists like Chabaneau and Wollaston developed methods to produce pure, workable platinum [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • ~100 BCE: Pre-Columbian cultures craft platinum artifacts [citation:4]
  • 1741: Wood introduces platinum to European scientists [citation:1]
  • 1800s: Wollaston develops powder metallurgy [citation:3]
  • 1970s: Catalytic converters revolutionize emission control [citation:8]

Interesting Facts

  • Platinum is one of the rarest metals on Earth – all the platinum ever mined would fit in a cube just 25 feet (7.6 meters) on each side. It's 30 times rarer than gold [citation:2].
  • About 40% of all platinum used annually goes into catalytic converters, where it converts harmful exhaust gases into less toxic substances. A single catalytic converter contains 3-7 grams of platinum [citation:3].
  • The "Platinum Age" of Spain (1730-1820) saw platinum smuggled from South America and used to create counterfeit gold coins – its density is close to gold, and it could be disguised by surface treatment [citation:5].
  • Cisplatin, a platinum-based drug, revolutionized cancer treatment in the 1970s. It binds to DNA in cancer cells and prevents replication, and is still used today for testicular, ovarian, and lung cancers [citation:7].
  • Platinum jewelry is hypoallergenic and doesn't tarnish – it's 95% pure in most jewelry alloys, unlike 18K gold which is only 75% pure. This purity makes it heavier and more durable [citation:1].
  • The Ostwald process for nitric acid production (essential for fertilizers) uses platinum-rhodium gauze catalysts. Without platinum, modern agriculture would be impossible [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic demonstration of platinum's catalytic power is the "lighting a match with platinum" experiment. A few drops of methanol on a platinum wire or gauze will ignite spontaneously due to catalytic oxidation. More dramatically, a hydrogen gas stream directed at platinum gauze will ignite without a spark – the platinum catalyzes the reaction between hydrogen and oxygen, releasing enough heat to glow red-hot and sustain combustion. This principle is used in catalytic heaters and hydrogen sensors [citation:1].
