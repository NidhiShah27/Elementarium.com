Hafnium (Hf)

Atomic Number: 72

Basic Information

Symbol: Hf
Atomic Mass: 178.49 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 4)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d² 6s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Lustrous, silvery-gray, ductile metal [citation:1]
  • Exceptionally high melting point: 2233°C [citation:5]
  • Corrosion-resistant (forms protective oxide) [citation:3]
  • Largest neutron absorption of any structural metal [citation:7]
  • Pyrophoric as fine powder (ignites spontaneously) [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1923 by Dirk Coster and George de Hevesy in Copenhagen, Denmark. They used X-ray spectroscopy to examine zirconium ores and identified the new element. It was named after "Hafnia," the Latin name for Copenhagen [citation:4][citation:8].

Its existence was predicted by Mendeleev in 1869, but it remained undiscovered because hafnium is chemically almost identical to zirconium – they are the most difficult pair of elements to separate [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1869: Mendeleev predicts element 72 (eka-zirconium) [citation:4]
  • 1923: Coster and Hevesy discover hafnium [citation:1]
  • 1925: First pure hafnium produced [citation:3]
  • 1950s: Nuclear reactor control rods create demand [citation:8]

Common Uses

Nuclear reactor control rods [citation:3]
High-k dielectrics in microchips (HfO₂) [citation:2]
Superalloys for aerospace & rocket nozzles [citation:5]
Plasma welding torches (electrodes) [citation:7]
Ultra-high temperature ceramics (HfC, HfB₂) [citation:1]
Gas-filled lamp filaments [citation:4]
Getter in vacuum tubes [citation:8]
Catalysts for polymerization [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Hafnium and zirconium are the most chemically similar pair of elements in the periodic table – their ionic radii differ by only 0.01 Å due to lanthanide contraction. This made separating them one of chemistry's greatest challenges [citation:2].
  • Hafnium carbide (HfC) has the highest melting point of any binary compound (3,890°C), while the mixed carbide Ta₄HfC₅ melts at 4,263 K – the highest of any known material [citation:3].
  • Hafnium's amazing neutron-absorbing ability makes it essential for nuclear submarines – a single set of control rods can cost over a million dollars. It absorbs neutrons without swelling or corroding [citation:5].
  • Hafnium oxide (HfO₂) revolutionized the semiconductor industry in 2007 when Intel introduced it as a high-k dielectric, allowing chips to become smaller and more power-efficient [citation:7].
  • The nuclear isomer 178m2Hf (half-life 31 years) was the subject of controversial research in the early 2000s regarding its potential use as a gamma-ray weapon, though this was never realized [citation:1].
  • Hafnium is found in all zirconium ores, typically at 1-5% concentration. The mineral hafnon (HfSiO₄) is the rare hafnium analog of zircon [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

72 protons, 106 neutrons (in 178Hf), 72 electrons

Try These Hafnium Reactions

Hafnium almost exclusively exhibits the +4 oxidation state. It is remarkably resistant to corrosion, but reacts with halogens and nonmetals at high temperatures [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): Hafnium's chemical similarity to zirconium is so extreme that traditional separation methods fail – but the Kroll process, developed in the 1940s, uses fractional distillation or solvent extraction to separate HfCl₄ from ZrCl₄. In a teaching lab, the "hafnium mystery" can be demonstrated: both elements form identical white precipitates with many reagents, but only hafnium salts can be ignited to produce a brilliant white light (pyrotechnic effect) due to its high density and oxidation energy [citation:1].
