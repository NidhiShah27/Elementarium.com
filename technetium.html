Technetium (Tc)

Atomic Number: 43

Basic Information

Symbol: Tc
Atomic Mass: [98] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 7)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d⁵ 5s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-gray, radioactive metal [citation:1]
  • High melting point: 2157°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance (similar to rhenium) [citation:3]
  • First artificially produced element (no stable isotopes) [citation:7]
  • Becomes a superconductor at 11.2 K [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Technetium was the first element to be artificially produced. It was discovered in 1937 by Carlo Perrier and Emilio Segrè in Italy. They bombarded molybdenum with deuterons in a cyclotron and isolated technetium-97 from the irradiated molybdenum foil [citation:4][citation:8].

The name comes from the Greek "technetos" meaning "artificial," because it was the first element created by humans. For decades, scientists had searched for element 43 in nature [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1925: Noddack claims discovery of "masurium" (disproven) [citation:4]
  • 1937: Perrier & Segrè produce and identify technetium [citation:1]
  • 1952: Detected in S-type red giant stars [citation:3]
  • 1960s: Tc-99m becomes widely used in medical imaging [citation:8]

Interesting Facts

  • Technetium is the lightest element that has no stable isotopes. All its isotopes are radioactive, with half-lives ranging from seconds to millions of years. It's why element 43 was missing from the periodic table for so long [citation:2].
  • Technetium does occur naturally – but only in the spectra of certain red giant stars (S-type stars). Its presence there proves that nuclear reactions inside stars create heavy elements (the s-process) [citation:4].
  • Technetium-99m is a medical marvel. With a half-life of 6 hours and emitting only gamma rays (no beta particles), it's perfect for medical imaging. It's used in over 30 million procedures annually worldwide to image hearts, brains, bones, and tumors [citation:3].
  • The pertechnetate ion (TcO₄⁻) is an excellent corrosion inhibitor. Just 5 parts per million in water protects steel from rust – a property unique among radioactive elements [citation:5].
  • In 1952, Paul Merrill detected technetium in the atmosphere of an S-type star – the first proof that elements are synthesized in stars. This discovery confirmed theories of stellar nucleosynthesis [citation:7].
  • Technetium-99 is a long-lived fission product (half-life 211,000 years) found in nuclear waste. Managing it is a challenge, but it can also be recycled for medical and industrial uses [citation:8].

43 protons, 55 neutrons (in Tc-98), 43 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The technetium-99m generator (or "moly cow") is a beautiful example of practical radiochemistry. Molybdenum-99 (produced in reactors) decays to technetium-99m. In a hospital, saline solution is passed through a column containing the Mo-99; the Tc-99m (as pertechnetate) is selectively eluted while the Mo-99 stays behind. This "milking" process provides fresh, short-lived Tc-99m for medical imaging – a perfect demonstration of parent-daughter radioactive equilibrium and separation chemistry [citation:1].
