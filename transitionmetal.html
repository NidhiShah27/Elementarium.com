Transition Metals

Groups 3–12 • The d‑block elements

What are Transition Metals?

Position: Groups 3 through 12, periods 4–7 (and some in period 6‑7 as part of the d‑block)
Electron configuration: partially filled d subshell (or readily form cations with incomplete d‑subshell)
Typical traits:
  • Hard, shiny, good conductors of heat & electricity
  • High melting points (except group 12)
  • Multiple oxidation states
  • Form coloured compounds
  • Excellent catalysts
  • Often paramagnetic

The term "transition" refers to the elements' position in the periodic table – they represent a transition from the highly electropositive group 1&2 metals to the less reactive metals and nonmetals of the p‑block.

Key Properties

  • Coloured ions: due to d‑d electron transitions (e.g. blue Cu²⁺, green Ni²⁺, purple Cr³⁺)
  • Variable oxidation states: e.g. manganese from +2 to +7
  • Magnetic behaviour: many are paramagnetic (unpaired d‑electrons); iron, cobalt, nickel are ferromagnetic
  • Catalytic activity: iron in Haber process, platinum in catalytic converters, nickel in hydrogenation
  • High density & strength: tungsten (W) has highest melting point of all metals
  • Coinage metals: copper, silver, gold (often found in native form)
Did you know? Many transition metals are essential for life: iron (haemoglobin), cobalt (vitamin B₁₂), copper, zinc, manganese …

Interesting facts

  • Emerald gets its green colour from chromium or vanadium.
  • Tungsten has the highest melting point (3422 °C) of all metals – used in light bulb filaments.
  • Mercury (group 12) is the only metal liquid at room temperature.
  • Gadolinium (a lanthanide, often studied with transition metals) is used in MRI contrast agents.
  • Gold is so malleable that one gram can be hammered into a sheet of one square metre.
  • Many transition metal compounds are used as catalysts in industrial processes (Haber, Ostwald, contact process).
