Einsteinium (Es)

Atomic Number: 99

Basic Information

Symbol: Es
Atomic Mass: [252] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹¹ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Highly radioactive, silvery-white metal, glows blue in the dark
  • Named in honor of Albert Einstein, father of modern physics
  • First discovered in the debris of the "Ivy Mike" hydrogen bomb test
  • Intense alpha and gamma emitter, self-heats due to radioactivity
  • Only available in microgram quantities; extremely difficult to study

Discovery & History

Discovered in December 1952 by Albert Ghiorso and colleagues at the University of California, Berkeley, along with teams from Argonne and Los Alamos National Laboratories. They analyzed coral and filter paper from the first hydrogen bomb test (Ivy Mike, November 1952) and found isotopes of einsteinium and fermium.

Named after Albert Einstein, who had died in 1955. The discovery was initially classified and not revealed until 1955 due to nuclear test secrecy.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1952: First produced in "Ivy Mike" thermonuclear explosion
  • 1955: Discovery announced publicly
  • 1961: First macroscopic (microgram) quantities produced
  • Modern: Produced in high-flux reactors for research

Common Uses

Synthesis of heavier elements (mendelevium, nobelium)
Basic research in nuclear chemistry
Study of actinide contraction and bonding
No commercial applications (extremely rare)
Calibration of radiation detectors
Study of nuclear decay properties
Target material for superheavy element searches
Tracer experiments in chemistry

Interesting Facts

  • Einsteinium is the first element named after a real person (Albert Einstein).
  • It was discovered in the radioactive fallout of a hydrogen bomb — about 10,000 atoms of ²⁵³Es were found in the debris.
  • ²⁵⁴Es has a half-life of only 275 days, making it difficult to accumulate.
  • Only about 1-2 milligrams of einsteinium have ever been produced worldwide.
  • Einsteinium-253 emits so much energy that a visible amount would glow red-hot and vaporize itself.
  • It's produced in tiny quantities by intense neutron irradiation of plutonium or curium in high-flux reactors like the HFIR at Oak Ridge.

Atomic Structure

99 protons, 153 neutrons (in 252Es), 99 electrons

Experiment Idea: Einsteinium(III) solutions are pale green and exhibit simple absorption spectra. Due to the intense radioactivity (especially gamma from ²⁵⁴Es), experiments must be performed remotely in hot cells. The divalent state Es²⁺ can be prepared by reduction with Sm²⁺ or Zn(Hg) and is stable enough for study by co-precipitation with SrSO₄.
