Ytterbium (Yb)

Atomic Number: 70

Basic Information

Symbol: Yb
Atomic Mass: 173.054 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Bright silvery, soft, malleable metal
  • Exists in +2 and +3 oxidation states
  • Reacts slowly with water, tarnishes in air
  • Named after the village Ytterby, Sweden
  • 7 naturally occurring isotopes

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1878 by Swiss chemist Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac. He found a new component in erbia, which he called ytterbia (after Ytterby). In 1907, Georges Urbain separated ytterbia into two elements: lutetium and ytterbium.

Named after Ytterby, the Swedish village that also gave names to yttrium, terbium, and erbium.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1878: Marignac discovers ytterbium
  • 1907: Urbain separates lutetium from ytterbium
  • 1953: First pure ytterbium produced
  • Modern: Essential for atomic clocks and fiber lasers

Common Uses

Atomic clocks (Yb lattice clocks)
Yb:fiber lasers (high-power, industrial)
Doping in laser crystals (Yb:YAG)
Gamma ray source (¹⁶⁹Yb)
Stress sensors in materials
Stainless steel alloying
Semiconductor research
Cancer therapy research

Interesting Facts

  • Ytterbium is one of four elements named after Ytterby, Sweden – the small quarry that has more elements named after it than any other place (yttrium, terbium, erbium, ytterbium).
  • Ytterbium atomic clocks are among the most accurate timekeeping devices ever made, with precision of one second in 10 billion years.
  • It can exist as Yb²⁺, which behaves like an alkaline earth metal, forming compounds similar to calcium and strontium.
  • Yb-169 (half-life 32 days) is used in portable X-ray and gamma-ray sources for medical and industrial imaging.
  • Ytterbium fiber lasers can achieve extremely high efficiency and power, used in cutting and welding metals.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is about 3 ppm, similar to tin and silver.

Atomic Structure

70 protons, 103 neutrons (in 173Yb), 70 electrons
