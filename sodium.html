Sodium (Na)

Atomic Number: 11

Basic Information

Symbol: Na
Atomic Mass: 22.98977 u
Category: Alkali Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ne] 3s¹

Key Properties

  • Highly reactive, especially with water
  • Soft, silvery-white metal (can be cut with a knife)
  • Excellent conductor of electricity
  • Low density – floats on water (but reacts violently!)
  • Sixth most abundant element in Earth's crust

Discovery & History

First isolated by Humphry Davy in 1807 in England through electrolysis of molten sodium hydroxide (caustic soda).

The name comes from the English word "soda" and the Latin "natrium" (sodium carbonate).

Historical Timeline:
  • 1807: Davy isolates sodium
  • 1860s: Sodium compounds used in soap-making
  • 1920s: Sodium vapor lamps developed
  • 20th c.: Essential for industrial chemistry

Common Uses

Sodium vapor lamps (yellow light)
Table salt (NaCl)
Baking soda (NaHCO₃)
Chemical reagent
Soap and detergent production
Coolant in nuclear reactors
Sodium azide in airbags
Glass manufacturing

Interesting Facts

  • Sodium reacts explosively with water – the reaction produces hydrogen gas and enough heat to ignite it
  • The yellow glow of sodium vapor lamps is almost monochromatic – it's the same color as the sodium D-line
  • Sodium ions are essential for nerve impulses in the human body
  • Liquid sodium is used as a coolant in some nuclear reactors because it conducts heat extremely well
  • Despite being a metal, sodium is so soft you can cut it with a butter knife
  • The most common sodium compound, table salt, has been used for thousands of years to preserve food

Atomic Structure

11 protons, 12 neutrons, 11 electrons
