Radium (Ra)

Atomic Number: 88

Basic Information

Symbol: Ra
Atomic Mass: [226] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Alkaline Earth Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 7s²

Key Properties

  • Highly radioactive – glows blue-green
  • Burns with crimson-red flame
  • Reacts violently with water
  • Extremely toxic and carcinogenic
  • Discovered by Marie and Pierre Curie

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1898 by Marie and Pierre Curie in Paris, France. They isolated it from uranium ore (pitchblende) after years of processing tons of ore.

The name comes from Latin "radius" meaning ray, because of its intense radioactivity.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1898: Curies discover radium
  • 1902: Marie Curie isolates pure radium
  • 1910s: "Radium craze" – used in paints, medicine, cosmetics
  • 1920s: Health effects recognized (Radium Girls tragedy)

Common Uses

Luminous watch dials (historical)
Cancer therapy (radium-223)
Scientific research
No modern commercial uses (too dangerous)
Neutron sources (with beryllium)
Early radiation therapy
Historic glow-in-the-dark paint
Radiometric dating

Interesting Facts

  • Marie Curie's notebooks are still radioactive today and will be for over 1,500 years – they're kept in lead-lined boxes
  • Radium glows blue-green due to its own radioactivity exciting surrounding air molecules – it's not the radium itself glowing
  • The "Radium Girls" were factory workers who painted watch dials with radium paint and ingested it by licking their brushes – many died painful deaths
  • Radium-226 has a half-life of 1,600 years – it decays to radon gas, which is also radioactive
  • Like other alkaline earth metals, radium would burn with a crimson-red flame (if you could get enough)
  • Radium is 1 million times more radioactive than the same mass of uranium

Atomic Structure

88 protons, 138 neutrons (in 226Ra), 88 electrons
