Iron (Fe)

Atomic Number: 26

Basic Information

Symbol: Fe
Atomic Mass: 55.845 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 8)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d⁶ 4s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-grey, lustrous, ductile and malleable metal [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1538°C, Boiling point: 2862°C [citation:5]
  • Reacts with oxygen and water (rusts), but can be alloyed to resist [citation:3]
  • Ferromagnetic (strongly magnetic) up to 770°C (Curie point) [citation:7]
  • Most abundant element by mass in Earth (34% of total mass) [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Iron has been known since prehistoric times – it's one of the first metals used by humans. The earliest iron artifacts (beads) date to around 4000 BCE in Egypt and Mesopotamia [citation:4][citation:8].

The Iron Age began around 1200 BCE when smelting techniques became widespread. The symbol Fe comes from the Latin "ferrum," meaning iron [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • ~4000 BCE: Earliest iron artifacts (meteoric iron) [citation:4]
  • ~1200 BCE: Iron Age begins with smelting technology [citation:1]
  • ~500 BCE: Wootz steel (India) – first high-quality steel [citation:3]
  • 1856: Bessemer process enables mass steel production [citation:8]

Interesting Facts

  • The Earth's core is composed mainly of iron (about 85%) with some nickel. This molten iron generates the Earth's magnetic field through the dynamo effect, protecting us from solar radiation [citation:2].
  • The Iron Pillar of Delhi (India), built around 400 CE, has stood for over 1600 years without rusting. Its corrosion resistance is due to a protective film formed by phosphorus in the iron [citation:4].
  • Iron is essential for life – it's at the core of hemoglobin, the molecule that carries oxygen in our blood. That's why iron deficiency causes anemia and fatigue [citation:7].
  • Iron is the final element produced by nuclear fusion in massive stars. Fusion of elements lighter than iron releases energy, but fusing iron consumes energy – leading to stellar collapse and supernovae [citation:5].
  • Stainless steel contains at least 10.5% chromium, which forms a protective oxide layer. Other alloying elements like nickel and molybdenum enhance corrosion resistance and strength [citation:3].
  • Meteoric iron (from meteorites) was the first source of iron for ancient peoples, prized for its nickel content and used for tools and weapons before smelting was developed [citation:8].

Atomic Structure

26 protons, 30 neutrons (in 56Fe), 26 electrons

Try These Iron Reactions

Iron exhibits two common oxidation states: +2 (ferrous) and +3 (ferric). Its chemistry is dominated by the formation of colorful complexes and the familiar process of rusting [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Fe H₂O

Fe H₂SO₄
Fe + Acid (FeSO₄ + H₂)
Fe Cr
Fe + Chromium (stainless steel)
Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic "iron clock" or "blue bottle" experiment demonstrates iron's redox chemistry. Dissolve iron(III) chloride and potassium ferricyanide – the solution is yellow. Add sodium thiosulfate and shake: Fe³⁺ is reduced to Fe²⁺, forming deep blue "Prussian blue" (Fe₄[Fe(CN)₆]₃). Let it stand, and oxygen slowly re-oxidizes it back to yellow – a beautiful cycle showing iron's two oxidation states and its role in electron transfer [citation:1].
