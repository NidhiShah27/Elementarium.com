Post-Transition Metals

Poor Metals / Other Metals

Groups: 13-16 (below the staircase line)

Category: Post-Transition Metal

Elements: Aluminum (Al), Gallium (Ga), Indium (In), Thallium (Tl), Tin (Sn), Lead (Pb), Bismuth (Bi), Polonium (Po)

General Properties: Soft or brittle, poor mechanical strength, lower melting points than transition metals

Post-transition metals are located between transition metals and metalloids. They have lower melting and boiling points than transition metals and are generally softer.

Key Properties:
  • Generally have higher electronegativity than transition metals.
  • Softer and have lower mechanical strength than transition metals.
  • Lower melting points compared to transition metals.
  • Form covalent bonds in compounds (especially in lower oxidation states).
  • Some exhibit amphoteric behavior (react with both acids and bases).
  • Often form colored ions, though less intensely than transition metals.
  • Many are toxic (Lead, Thallium, Polonium).
Common Uses:
  • Aluminum (Al): Aircraft, cans, foil, construction.
  • Tin (Sn): Tin cans, solder, bronze alloy.
  • Lead (Pb): Batteries, radiation shielding, historically in pipes and paint.
  • Gallium (Ga): Semiconductors, LEDs, low-melting alloys.
  • Bismuth (Bi): Pharmaceuticals (Pepto-Bismol), cosmetics, low-toxicity shot.
  • Indium (In): Touch screens, LCD displays, solar panels.
  • Thallium (Tl): Historically as rat poison, now limited uses.
  • Polonium (Po): Static eliminators, atomic batteries (highly radioactive).

Post-transition metals include Aluminum, Tin, Lead, and Bismuth

Unique Characteristics:
  • Gallium (Ga): Melts in your hand (29.76°C).
  • Bismuth (Bi): Least toxic heavy metal with beautiful rainbow-colored oxidation.
  • Aluminum (Al): Most abundant metal in Earth's crust (8.2%).
  • Lead (Pb): Very dense (11.34 g/cm³) and corrosion-resistant.
  • Tin (Sn): Exhibits "tin cry" - cracking sound when bent.
  • Polonium (Po): One of the most radioactive elements.
Historical Significance:
  • Tin and Lead: Known since antiquity (Bronze Age, Roman Empire).
  • Aluminum: Once more valuable than gold; now ubiquitous.
  • Bismuth: Often confused with lead and tin until 18th century.
