Metalloids

The semimetals • stepping stones between metals and nonmetals

What are Metalloids?

Position: along the zig‑zag line from boron to astatine (groups 13–17, periods 2–6)
Electron configuration: intermediate – typically have 3 to 6 valence electrons (p‑block)
Typical traits:
  • Exhibit properties of both metals and nonmetals
  • Semiconductors (moderate electrical conductivity)
  • Brittle, often metallic lustre
  • Form amphoteric or weakly acidic oxides
  • Can act as dopants in semiconductor industry
  • Alloys often improve strength or corrosion resistance

The term “metalloid” comes from the Greek metallon (metal) and eidos (resembling) – they look like metals but behave chemically like nonmetals in many ways.

Key Properties

  • Semiconductivity: silicon and germanium are the foundations of modern electronics
  • Melting points: high (boron >2000 °C) to moderate (antimony 630 °C)
  • Amphoteric oxides: e.g. SiO₂ reacts with HF, but also with strong bases to form silicates
  • Electrical conductivity: increases with temperature (opposite of metals)
  • Common oxidation states: B (+3), Si (+4), Ge (+4), As (±3,+5), Sb (+3,+5), Te (±2,+4,+6)
  • Alloying behaviour: antimony hardens lead; arsenic improves battery grids
Did you know? Silicon is the second most abundant element in Earth's crust (about 28%) – it’s the main ingredient in sand, quartz, and computer chips.

The Metalloid Elements

Six elements are universally recognised as metalloids. Boron, silicon, germanium, arsenic, antimony, tellurium – and sometimes polonium and astatine are included.

Interesting facts

  • Boron is essential for plant growth – boron deficiency causes brittle stems.
  • Silicon makes up about 60% of the mass of the Moon’s crust.
  • Arsenic was called the “inheritance powder” in the Middle Ages because of its discreet use in poisonings.
  • Germanium was used in the first transistors – its name honours Germany.
  • Antimony trioxide is one of the most widely used flame retardants in plastics.
  • Tellurium is named after the Latin tellus (earth) – it’s extremely rare, yet used in next‑gen solar cells.

Semiconductor behaviour & the zig‑zag

Silicon & germanium are the classic semiconductors. Their conductivity can be tuned by doping with tiny amounts of arsenic or boron.

n‑type (arsenic) adds extra electrons • p‑type (boron) creates “holes”.

The metalloid staircase separates metals (left) from nonmetals (right). Elements along this line (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te) show mixed properties.

Abundance & production

Silicon ≈ 28% crust
Boron ≈ 0.001%
Arsenic ≈ 0.0002%
Germanium ≈ 0.00015%

Annual production: silicon >8 million tonnes (mostly ferrosilicon for metallurgy), boron ~4 Mt (as borates), antimony ~150,000 t, germanium ~130 t. Metalloids are vital for high‑tech industries despite modest tonnages.
