Gold (Au)

Atomic Number: 79

Basic Information

Symbol: Au
Atomic Mass: 196.96657 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 11)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Bright, slightly reddish-yellow, soft, and highly malleable [citation:1]
  • Noble metal – extremely corrosion-resistant [citation:5]
  • Most ductile metal – can be drawn into miles of wire [citation:3]
  • Excellent conductor of electricity and heat [citation:7]
  • Chemically inert, dissolves only in aqua regia & cyanide [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Gold has been known and treasured since prehistoric times – one of the first metals used by humans. Its symbol Au comes from the Latin "aurum," meaning "shining dawn." Gold artifacts date to the 5th millennium BCE [citation:4][citation:8].

The quest for gold drove exploration, conquest, and trade for millennia – from Midas and the Pharaohs to the Spanish conquest of the Americas. The California Gold Rush (1848-1855) transformed the United States [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • ~4600 BCE: Oldest gold artifacts (Varna Necropolis) [citation:4]
  • ~1500 BCE: Egyptian gold jewelry & death masks [citation:1]
  • 564 BCE: First gold coins minted in Lydia [citation:3]
  • 1848: California Gold Rush [citation:8]

Interesting Facts

  • All the gold ever mined throughout human history would fit into a cube just 22 meters on each side (about 200,000 tons). This is because gold is extremely dense and almost all of it is still in circulation [citation:2].
  • Gold is so malleable that a single ounce (28 grams) can be beaten into a sheet covering 100 square feet – thin enough to be translucent. This gold leaf has been used for centuries to decorate buildings, art, and food [citation:3].
  • Gold is one of the best electrical conductors and never corrodes, making it essential for critical electronic components. Your smartphone contains about 50 milligrams of gold in its circuit boards and connectors [citation:5].
  • Gold nanoparticles (colloidal gold) have been used in medicine since ancient times. Today, they're used in rapid pregnancy tests, cancer photothermal therapy, and targeted drug delivery [citation:7].
  • "Fool's gold" (iron pyrite) has fooled prospectors for centuries – it looks like gold but is much harder, less dense, and forms cubic crystals. Real gold is usually found in irregular shapes and is extremely soft [citation:1].
  • Edible gold leaf (E175) is used in gourmet foods and drinks – it has no taste but adds a luxurious appearance. Goldschlager liqueur contains tiny gold flakes [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

Try These Gold Reactions

Gold exhibits oxidation states from -1 to +5, with +1 and +3 being most common. It's famously unreactive, but forms spectacular compounds [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Au Cu

Au Ag
Au + Silver (electrum)
Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic "gold cyanide process" is the basis of modern gold mining. Gold is unreactive, but in the presence of oxygen and cyanide ions, it forms the soluble complex [Au(CN)₂]⁻. This reaction allowed miners to extract gold from low-grade ores. In a teaching lab, the "purple of Cassius" is a beautiful demonstration – a colloidal gold precipitate formed by reducing gold chloride with tin(II) chloride, producing a stunning ruby-red to purple color that's been used in glass staining for centuries [citation:1].
