Osmium (Os)

Atomic Number: 76

Basic Information

Symbol: Os
Atomic Mass: 190.23 u
Category: Transition Metal (Platinum Group)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁶ 6s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Bluish-white, lustrous, extremely hard and brittle [citation:1]
  • Densest naturally occurring element: 22.59 g/cm³ [citation:5]
  • 4th highest melting point: 3033°C [citation:3]
  • Exceptional corrosion resistance – unaffected by acids [citation:7]
  • Forms toxic, volatile tetroxide (OsO₄) when heated [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1803 by Smithson Tennant in London, England. While studying platinum ore residues dissolved in aqua regia, he isolated two new elements – osmium and iridium. The name comes from the Greek "osme" meaning "smell" [citation:4][citation:8].

Tennant was a brilliant chemist who also discovered that diamond is pure carbon. His discovery of osmium and iridium filled the missing gaps in the platinum group metals [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1803: Tennant discovers osmium and iridium [citation:4]
  • 1814: Berzelius confirms and names the elements [citation:1]
  • 1898: Osmium used in lamp filaments (later replaced) [citation:3]
  • 20th c.: Osmium alloys for pen tips & contacts [citation:8]

Common Uses

Hardening platinum alloys for contacts [citation:3]
Staining tissues for electron microscopy [citation:2]
Fountain pen nib tipping (with iridium) [citation:5]
Electrical contacts & high-wear parts [citation:7]
Catalyst for certain reactions (OsO₄) [citation:1]
Jewelry (very rare, luxury watches) [citation:4]
Compass bearings & clock pivots [citation:8]
Spacecraft components [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Osmium is the densest naturally occurring element – a cubic foot of osmium would weigh about 1,400 pounds (almost twice as heavy as lead). For decades, it was considered the densest element [citation:2].
  • Osmium tetroxide (OsO₄) is extremely toxic and volatile – it vaporizes at room temperature and can cause blindness and lung damage. Yet it's also one of the most valuable reagents in organic chemistry, used to make pharmaceuticals [citation:3].
  • Despite being the densest element, osmium is surprisingly brittle. You can't hammer it into sheets like gold – it will shatter. This limits its practical applications [citation:5].
  • Osmium was used in early light bulb filaments before tungsten replaced it. Its high melting point made it suitable, but its volatility and brittleness made it impractical [citation:7].
  • In electron microscopy, osmium tetroxide is essential for staining lipids in cell membranes. It binds to fatty acids and provides contrast, revealing the intricate structure of cells [citation:1].
  • Osmium can form a blue-black oxide layer when heated, and some jewelers have experimented with "blue osmium" rings – but the cost and difficulty make them incredibly rare and expensive [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

76 protons, 114 neutrons (in 190Os), 76 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic "osmium tetroxide" reaction is both beautiful and dangerous. When a fine powder of osmium metal is gently heated in air, it produces yellow crystals of OsO₄ that sublime readily. In organic chemistry labs, it's used in catalytic amounts with co-oxidants for the Upjohn dihydroxylation – a key step in synthesizing complex pharmaceuticals. Its ability to add two hydroxyl groups across a double bond with perfect stereochemistry is unmatched, even by enzymes [citation:1].
