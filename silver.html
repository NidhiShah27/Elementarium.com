Silver (Ag)

Atomic Number: 47

Basic Information

Symbol: Ag
Atomic Mass: 107.8682 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 11)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Brilliant white, lustrous, and highly reflective metal [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 961.8°C, Boiling point: 2162°C [citation:5]
  • Highest electrical conductivity of any metal [citation:3]
  • Excellent ductility and malleability [citation:7]
  • Tarnishes when exposed to sulfur compounds (forms Ag₂S) [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Silver has been known since prehistoric times – one of the first five metals discovered and used by humans (along with gold, copper, lead, and iron). Artifacts date to around 4000 BCE in Anatolia [citation:4][citation:8].

The name comes from the Anglo-Saxon "seolfor," and its symbol Ag comes from the Latin "argentum" (shiny, white). Ancient civilizations mined silver extensively – the Laurium mines in Greece provided silver for the Athenian empire [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • ~4000 BCE: Earliest silver artifacts (Anatolia, Iran) [citation:4]
  • ~1200 BCE: Silver used as currency (Lydian coins) [citation:1]
  • Medieval: Silver mining boom in Europe [citation:3]
  • 19th c.: Photographic film (silver halides) revolutionizes imaging [citation:8]

Interesting Facts

  • Silver is the best electrical conductor of any element – even better than copper. That's why it's used in high-end audio connectors, satellite components, and critical electrical switches [citation:2].
  • "Argentium silver" is a modern alloy containing 93.5% silver and 1% germanium – it's tarnish-resistant and brighter than traditional sterling silver (which is 92.5% silver with copper) [citation:3].
  • Ancient peoples didn't know why, but they observed that water or wine stored in silver vessels stayed fresh longer. We now know this is due to the oligodynamic effect – silver ions kill bacteria and microorganisms [citation:5].
  • Silver reflects 95% of visible light – more than any other metal. This is why telescope mirrors, searchlights, and high-quality mirrors are coated with silver (though they need a protective layer to prevent tarnish) [citation:7].
  • The term "sterling silver" for 92.5% pure silver originated in England in the 13th century. The extra 7.5% copper adds hardness without losing too much of silver's beauty [citation:1].
  • Silver nanoparticles are now used in clothing, bandages, and deodorants for their antibacterial properties – they prevent odor-causing bacteria from growing on fabrics and skin [citation:4].

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic "silver mirror" reaction is a beautiful demonstration of silver chemistry. In a clean glass vessel, Tollens' reagent ([Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺) is mixed with an aldehyde (like glucose). The silver ions are reduced, plating a brilliant mirror of pure metallic silver onto the glass. This reaction was once used to make mirrors and is still used to demonstrate oxidation-reduction chemistry. In the lab, you can also demonstrate the light sensitivity of silver halides – AgCl turns purple-gray when exposed to sunlight [citation:1].
