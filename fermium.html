Fermium (Fm)

Atomic Number: 100

Basic Information

Symbol: Fm
Atomic Mass: [257] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹² 7s²

Key Properties

  • Highly radioactive synthetic element, silvery-white in appearance
  • Named in honor of Enrico Fermi, pioneer of nuclear physics
  • Last element discovered in the debris of the "Ivy Mike" hydrogen bomb test
  • Intense alpha emitter; isotopes with mass >256 undergo spontaneous fission
  • Only available in nanogram quantities; chemical studies extremely challenging

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1952 by Albert Ghiorso and colleagues at the University of California, Berkeley, along with teams from Argonne and Los Alamos National Laboratories. Like einsteinium, fermium was found in radioactive debris from the first hydrogen bomb test (Ivy Mike, November 1952).

Named after Enrico Fermi, who had died in 1954. The discovery was classified until 1955 due to nuclear test secrecy. Fermium-255 was the first isotope identified.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1952: First produced in "Ivy Mike" thermonuclear explosion
  • 1955: Discovery announced publicly
  • 1960s: Produced in high-flux reactors for research
  • Modern: Used as target for synthesizing superheavy elements

Common Uses

Synthesis of superheavy elements (target material)
Basic research in nuclear chemistry and physics
Study of spontaneous fission properties
No commercial applications (extremely rare)
Study of nuclear decay systematics
Calibration of alpha and fission detectors
Investigation of actinide metallic bonding
Tracer experiments in solution chemistry

Interesting Facts

  • Fermium is the heaviest element that can be formed by neutron capture in nuclear reactors; heavier elements require accelerators.
  • It was discovered in the same hydrogen bomb test as einsteinium, but fermium has higher atomic number and mass.
  • ²⁵⁷Fm has a half-life of 100.5 days — long enough for chemical studies, but only picogram quantities are available.
  • All isotopes with mass number above 256 have significant spontaneous fission branches, limiting their study.
  • Only about 10^-12 grams of fermium are produced annually for research worldwide.
  • Fermium is named after Enrico Fermi, who built the first nuclear reactor and won the Nobel Prize in 1938.

Atomic Structure

100 protons, 157 neutrons (in 257Fm), 100 electrons

Try These Fermium Reactions

Fermium chemistry is dominated by the +3 oxidation state, similar to its lanthanide homolog erbium. The +2 state can be produced under reducing conditions and is relatively stable due to the half-filled 5f¹² configuration.
