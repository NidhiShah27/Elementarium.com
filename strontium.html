Strontium (Sr)

Atomic Number: 38

Basic Information

Symbol: Sr
Atomic Mass: 87.62 u
Category: Alkaline Earth Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 5s²

Key Properties

  • Burns with brilliant crimson-red flame
  • Reacts vigorously with water
  • Softer than calcium, silvery metal
  • Tarnishes rapidly in air
  • Named after the village of Strontian, Scotland

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1790 by Adair Crawford in Scotland, who recognized a new mineral (strontianite) from lead mines near Strontian. First isolated by Humphry Davy in 1808 via electrolysis.

Named after the village of Strontian in the Scottish Highlands, where the mineral was found.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1790: Crawford identifies new mineral
  • 1808: Davy isolates strontium metal
  • 1887: Strontium used in sugar refining
  • 20th c.: Used in cathode ray tubes and fireworks

Common Uses

Red fireworks and flares
Cathode ray tubes (old TVs)
Toothpaste for sensitive teeth (strontium chloride)
Ceramic magnets (ferrites)
Strontium-89 (bone cancer treatment)
Research in atomic clocks
Glass for color televisions
Sugar beet refining

Interesting Facts

  • Strontium compounds produce the most intense red color in fireworks – unmistakable and brilliant
  • Strontium-90 is a dangerous radioactive fallout product from nuclear tests – it's chemically similar to calcium and can accumulate in bones
  • Strontium atoms are used in some of the world's most accurate atomic clocks (optical lattice clocks)
  • The village of Strontian in Scotland (population ~350) has the unique honor of having an element named after it
  • Strontium ranelate is used to treat osteoporosis – it stimulates bone formation
  • Strontium is softer than calcium and can be cut with a knife

Atomic Structure

38 protons, 50 neutrons, 38 electrons
