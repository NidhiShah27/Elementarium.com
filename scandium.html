Scandium (Sc)

Atomic Number: 21

Basic Information

Symbol: Sc
Atomic Mass: 44.955908 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 3)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d¹ 4s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, lightweight metal that tarnishes in air [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1541°C, Boiling point: 2836°C [citation:5]
  • Density: 2.99 g/cm³ (similar to aluminum) [citation:3]
  • Considered the first transition metal; bridge between s-block and d-block [citation:1]
  • Forms a protective oxide layer (Sc₂O₃) that resists corrosion [citation:7]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1879 by Lars Fredrik Nilson in Uppsala, Sweden. He isolated scandium oxide (scandia) from the minerals euxenite and gadolinite, and named it after Scandinavia [citation:4][citation:8].

Its existence had been predicted by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1871 as "eka-boron" based on gaps in his periodic table. Per Teodor Cleve later confirmed the match [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1869: Mendeleev predicts element 21 (eka-boron) [citation:4]
  • 1879: Nilson discovers scandium in Uppsala [citation:1]
  • 1937: First pure scandium metal produced by electrolysis [citation:8]
  • 1960s: Scandium-aluminum alloys developed for aerospace [citation:3]

Common Uses

Aerospace alloys (Sc-Al for strength/weight) [citation:3]
High-performance bicycle frames [citation:2]
Baseball bats (scandium-aluminum) [citation:5]
Metal halide lamps (bright white light) [citation:7]
Solid oxide fuel cells (Sc-stabilized zirconia) [citation:1]
Tracing agent in oil refining [citation:4]
Lasers (Gadolinium-Scandium-Gallium Garnet) [citation:8]
Dental lasers [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Scandium is not particularly rare – it's about as abundant as cobalt. However, it's widely dispersed and rarely concentrated in mineable ores, making extraction difficult and expensive [citation:3].
  • The prediction of scandium by Mendeleev was one of the great triumphs of the periodic table. He predicted its atomic mass (~44), density, and oxide formula (Sc₂O₃) years before its discovery [citation:4].
  • Scandium-aluminum alloys were developed for the Soviet space program and military aircraft. Adding just 0.5% scandium to aluminum increases strength by 50% while maintaining lightness [citation:3].
  • Pure scandium is expensive – up to $10,000 per kilogram – because it's difficult to separate from rare earth elements. Most scandium is produced as a byproduct of uranium and tungsten refining [citation:5].
  • Scandium iodide is added to metal halide lamps to produce a light with a color temperature close to natural sunlight, ideal for stadium lighting and film production [citation:7].
  • The first pound of pure scandium metal was not produced until 1960, nearly 80 years after its discovery [citation:8].

Atomic Structure

21 protons, 24 neutrons (in 45Sc), 21 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): A classic scandium demonstration is the formation of scandium oxide. When scandium metal is heated in air, it burns with a brilliant white flame to form Sc₂O₃. This oxide is amphoteric – it dissolves in acids to form Sc³⁺ salts and in strong bases to form scandate ions [Sc(OH)₆]³⁻. This behavior places scandium between aluminum (strongly amphoteric) and yttrium (more basic) in chemical reactivity [citation:1].
