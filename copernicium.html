Copernicium (Cn)

Atomic Number: 112

Basic Information

Symbol: Cn
Atomic Mass: [285] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 12)
State at Room Temperature: Liquid (predicted) or Gas? [citation:5]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~10°C? (predicted, may be volatile liquid) [citation:5]
  • Density: ~23 g/cm³ (estimated, similar to mercury) [citation:5]
  • Heavier homolog of mercury (Group 12); may be a noble liquid [citation:1]
  • Predicted to be surprisingly volatile and inert, like a noble gas [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First synthesized in 1996 by a team led by Sigurd Hofmann at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. They bombarded lead-208 with zinc-70 ions, producing a single atom of 277Cn [citation:1][citation:8].

The discovery was confirmed in 2000 by RIKEN (Japan) and later at GSI. It was the first element discovered that completed the 6d series [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:

Interesting Facts

  • Copernicium is named after Nicolaus Copernicus, the Polish astronomer who proposed that the Earth orbits the Sun. It was the first element named after an astronomer [citation:4].
  • The original 1996 experiment producing 277Cn created just one atom after two weeks of bombardment. Its alpha decay chain was identified, confirming the discovery [citation:1].
  • Chemical studies on copernicium have been performed using gas-phase chromatography. Surprisingly, it behaves like a noble gas – it's even more volatile than mercury and does not stick to gold surfaces at room temperature [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 285Cn, has a half-life of about 29 seconds. It is produced in the decay chain of 289Fl (flerovium) and 293Lv (livermorium) [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects in copernicium are so strong that they fundamentally change its chemical behavior. The 7s electrons are so tightly bound that copernicium behaves like a noble gas, with very low reactivity and high volatility [citation:2].
  • Copernicium is predicted to be the only metal that is a gas at room temperature (though this is debated). Its boiling point is estimated to be around 60°C, meaning it would boil on a hot day [citation:5].

Atomic Structure

112 protons, 173 neutrons (in 285Cn), 112 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic copernicium chemistry experiment uses thermochromatography to study adsorption on a gold surface. Atoms of 285Cn (half-life 29 seconds) are produced in a nuclear reaction, thermalized, and passed through a chromatography column coated with gold. Surprisingly, copernicium does not adsorb on gold at room temperature – it passes through like a noble gas. This demonstrates the extreme relativistic effect where the 7s electrons are so tightly bound that copernicium is inert, confirming its unique place in the periodic table as a "volatile metal" [citation:1].
