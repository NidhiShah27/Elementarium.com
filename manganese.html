Manganese (Mn)

Atomic Number: 25

Basic Information

Symbol: Mn
Atomic Mass: 54.938044 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 7)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d⁵ 4s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Hard, brittle, silvery-grey metal, tarnishes easily [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1246°C, Boiling point: 2061°C [citation:5]
  • Reacts slowly with water, dissolves in dilute acids [citation:3]
  • Exists in many oxidation states (+2 to +7), most of any element [citation:7]
  • Essential trace element for all known life [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Manganese dioxide (pyrolusite) has been used since ancient times for glassmaking. In the 1700s, it was confused with magnetic iron ore. In 1774, Johan Gottlieb Gahn in Sweden first isolated pure manganese metal by reducing the dioxide with carbon [citation:4][citation:8].

The name derives from the Latin "magnes" (magnet), due to confusion with magnetic iron ores, and from the Italian "manganese" for the mineral [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • Antiquity: MnO₂ used to decolorize glass (Egypt, Rome) [citation:4]
  • 1774: Gahn isolates pure manganese [citation:1]
  • 1856: Bessemer process requires manganese to remove sulfur from steel [citation:3]
  • 20th c.: Essential for dry cell batteries and steel production [citation:8]

Common Uses

Steel production (90% of Mn) – deoxidizer, desulfurizer [citation:3]
Dry cell batteries (MnO₂ cathode) [citation:2]
Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄) – disinfectant, oxidizer [citation:5]
Aluminum alloys (increase strength) [citation:7]
Pigments (manganese violet, umber) [citation:1]
Water treatment and bleaching [citation:4]
Fertilizer additive (essential nutrient) [citation:8]
Organic synthesis oxidizer [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Manganese has the widest range of oxidation states of any element – from -3 to +7. This makes its chemistry incredibly rich and colorful, from pale pink Mn²⁺ to deep purple MnO₄⁻ (permanganate) [citation:2].
  • Ancient glassmakers used manganese dioxide (pyrolusite) to decolorize glass – it oxidizes the green tint from iron impurities. They also used it to make purple glass [citation:4].
  • Manganese is essential for photosynthesis – it's at the heart of the oxygen-evolving complex in plants, where it catalyzes the splitting of water into oxygen [citation:7].
  • Manganese nodules on the ocean floor contain vast amounts of manganese (along with iron, nickel, copper, and cobalt). These potato-sized lumps could be a future source of these metals [citation:5].
  • Steel cannot be made without manganese – it removes sulfur and oxygen during production, preventing brittleness. About 90% of all manganese mined goes into steel [citation:3].
  • Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄) is a powerful disinfectant and is still used in some water treatment plants. Its deep purple color is so intense that a single crystal can color liters of water [citation:8].

Atomic Structure

25 protons, 30 neutrons (in 55Mn), 25 electrons

Try These Manganese Reactions

Manganese's multiple oxidation states create a beautiful series of color changes. The +7 state (permanganate) is a powerful oxidizing agent, while +2 (manganous) is the most stable [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Mn O
Mn + Oxygen (MnO₂)
Mn Cl
Mn + Chlorine (MnCl₂)
Mn Fe
Mn + Iron (ferromanganese)
Mn KMnO₄
Mn + Permanganate (redox)
Mn SO₄²⁻
Mn + Sulfate (MnSO₄)
Mn H₂O
Mn + Water (slow reaction)
Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The "manganese rainbow" is a classic oxidation state demonstration. Start with deep purple potassium permanganate (KMnO₄, Mn⁷⁺). Reduce it stepwise with sodium sulfite or oxalic acid: first to green Mn⁶⁺ (manganate), then to brown Mn⁴⁺ (MnO₂), and finally to pale pink Mn²⁺ (manganous ion). Each step produces a distinct color change, showcasing manganese's remarkable range of oxidation states and its value as a redox indicator [citation:1].
