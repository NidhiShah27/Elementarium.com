Halogens

The salt-formers • most reactive nonmetals on Earth

What are Halogens?

Position: Group 17 (VIIA) of the periodic table — fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine, astatine
Electron configuration: ns² np⁵ — seven valence electrons, one electron short of a full octet
Typical traits:
  • Extremely reactive — never found free in nature (except as diatomic molecules)
  • High electronegativity — fluorine is the most electronegative element (4.0)
  • Diatomic molecules: F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ (At₂ is theoretical)
  • Form salts when combined with metals (e.g., NaCl, KI)
  • Colored vapors: F₂ (pale yellow), Cl₂ (greenish-yellow), Br₂ (red-brown), I₂ (violet)
  • Oxidizing agents — readily gain one electron to form halide ions (X⁻)

The name "halogen" comes from Greek hals (salt) and gennan (to form) — literally "salt-producers".

Key Properties

  • Physical states: F₂ & Cl₂ (gases), Br₂ (liquid), I₂ (solid) at room temperature
  • Electronegativity trend: decreases down the group: F (4.0) > Cl (3.2) > Br (3.0) > I (2.7) > At (2.2)
  • Reactivity trend: decreases down the group — fluorine is the most reactive nonmetal
  • Melting/Boiling points: increase with atomic number (F₂ low, I₂ high)
  • Oxidizing strength: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂ (fluorine can oxidize water to oxygen)
  • Industrial importance: disinfectants, pharmaceuticals, plastics (PTFE, PVC), flame retardants
Did you know? Fluorine is so reactive that it reacts with noble gases (xenon, krypton) and even with asbestos and glass under certain conditions.

Fascinating facts

  • Fluorine was isolated in 1886 after decades of failed attempts — many chemists were poisoned or injured.
  • Chlorine was the first chemical weapon used in WWI (1915) — now it's essential for safe drinking water worldwide.
  • Bromine is the only nonmetal that is liquid at room temperature (mercury is the only metal liquid).
  • Iodine deficiency is the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability globally — iodized salt saves millions.
  • Astatine is the rarest naturally occurring element — less than one gram exists in Earth's crust at any time.
  • Reactivity trend: Fluorine can even react with xenon and krypton, forming XeF₂, XeF₄, and KrF₂.

Displacement reactions & reactivity series

Halogen displacement rule: A more reactive halogen will displace a less reactive halogen from its halide solution.

Cl₂ + 2NaBr → 2NaCl + Br₂ (chlorine displaces bromine)

Br₂ + 2KI → 2KBr + I₂ (bromine displaces iodine)

Fluorine is too reactive for aqueous displacement — reacts violently with water.

Oxidizing strength decreases down the group:

F₂ (strongest) Cl₂ Br₂ I₂ (weakest)

Fluorine can even oxidize oxygen in some compounds — unparalleled reactivity.

Halogen acids: HF (weak acid, dangerous), HCl (strong acid, stomach acid), HBr, HI (strong acids). Hydrogen fluoride is unique — etches glass.

Production methods: Chlorine via electrolysis of brine (Chlor-alkali process). Bromine extracted from seawater or brine wells. Iodine from caliche ore or underground brines. Fluorine obtained by electrolysis of KF·HF mixture.

Environmental note: Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) — once used as refrigerants — were phased out due to ozone layer depletion. Modern halogens are managed with strict regulations.

Important halogen compounds

PVC: (C₂H₃Cl)ₙ — pipes, cables, flooring
Levothyroxine: iodine-containing thyroid medication
Bleach: NaClO (sodium hypochlorite) — disinfectant
Halons: brominated fire suppressants (phased out)
NaF, SnF₂: fluoride in toothpaste for enamel protection
