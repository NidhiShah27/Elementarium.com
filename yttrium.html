Yttrium (Y)

Atomic Number: 39

Basic Information

Symbol: Y
Atomic Mass: 88.90584 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 3)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹ 5s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-metallic, lustrous, and crystalline [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1526°C, Boiling point: 3336°C [citation:5]
  • Stable in air due to protective oxide layer [citation:3]
  • Finely divided yttrium is pyrophoric (ignites in air) [citation:7]
  • Often classified with rare earth elements [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1794 by Johan Gadolin in Finland, in a mineral from the village of Ytterby, Sweden. The element is named after Ytterby, the same Swedish quarry that yielded four elements (yttrium, ytterbium, terbium, erbium) [citation:4][citation:8].

Friedrich Wöhler is credited with first isolating the impure metal in 1828. Yttrium is often classified as a "rare earth" element because it shares chemical properties with the lanthanides [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1794: Gadolin discovers yttrium oxide (yttria) [citation:4]
  • 1828: Wöhler produces impure yttrium metal [citation:1]
  • 1843: Mosander splits yttria into yttrium, terbium, erbium [citation:3]
  • 20th c.: Vital for color TV phosphors, lasers, superconductors [citation:8]

Common Uses

Red phosphors in color TV & LED screens [citation:3]
High-temperature superconductors (YBCO) [citation:2]
Yttrium-aluminum garnet (YAG) lasers [citation:5]
Microwave filters & radar technology [citation:7]
Stabilizer in zirconia ceramics (cubic zirconia) [citation:1]
Nuclear technology (fuel cladding) [citation:4]
Electrolyte in solid oxide fuel cells [citation:8]
Catalyst for ethylene polymerization [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Yttrium is one of four elements named after the Swedish village of Ytterby (along with ytterbium, terbium, erbium) – the most elements named after a single location. This makes it a favorite among element trivia enthusiasts [citation:4].
  • Yttrium-aluminum garnet (YAG) doped with neodymium (Nd:YAG) is one of the most widely used solid-state laser crystals, found in medical, industrial, and military lasers. It's used for everything from eye surgery to cutting metal [citation:2].
  • Yttrium barium copper oxide (YBCO) was the first material to exhibit superconductivity above the boiling point of liquid nitrogen (77 K), revolutionizing practical applications of superconductors. It can levitate magnets in a dramatic demonstration [citation:3].
  • Yttrium is used to make cubic zirconia – the synthetic gemstone that looks like diamond. Adding yttrium stabilizes the zirconia crystal structure, creating a hard, brilliant material used in jewelry [citation:5].
  • Yttrium-90, a radioactive isotope, is used in targeted cancer therapy (radioembolization) for liver tumors. It's delivered directly to the tumor site via microspheres [citation:7].
  • Yttrium is surprisingly abundant – it's about twice as common in the Earth's crust as lead and more abundant than silver. Despite this, it's difficult to extract and refine [citation:8].

Atomic Structure

39 protons, 50 neutrons (in 89Y), 39 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): Yttrium oxide (Y₂O₃) is a white powder that can be dissolved in nitric acid to form yttrium nitrate (Y(NO₃)₃). When combined with europium, it creates the famous red phosphor used in old color TV screens. While a full synthesis is complex, the chemistry demonstrates how rare earth elements produce brilliant, pure colors – a property that revolutionized display technology. In the lab, you can observe the fluorescence of europium-doped yttrium oxide under UV light – it glows bright red [citation:1].
