Aluminium (Al)

Atomic Number: 13

Basic Information

Symbol: Al
Atomic Mass: 26.981538 u
Category: Post-Transition Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ne] 3s² 3p¹

Key Properties

  • Lightweight but strong (density 2.7 g/cm³)
  • Forms protective oxide layer (corrosion-resistant)
  • Excellent conductor of electricity
  • 100% recyclable without quality loss
  • Most abundant metal in Earth's crust

Discovery & History

First isolated by Hans Christian Ørsted in 1825 in Denmark. Friedrich Wöhler produced the first pure sample in 1827.

The name comes from Latin "alumen" (alum). Once so rare and expensive that Napoleon III served it on special occasions.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1825: Ørsted produces impure aluminium
  • 1854: First commercial production (very expensive)
  • 1886: Hall-Héroult process makes it affordable
  • 20th c.: Becomes the most widely used non-ferrous metal

Common Uses

Aircraft and aerospace
Automotive parts (engines, wheels)
Beverage cans and packaging
Window frames and construction
Power lines (high-tension cables)
Electronics and smartphones
Kitchen foil and cookware
Bicycle frames

Interesting Facts

  • Aluminium was once more valuable than gold – the Washington Monument has a 2.8 kg aluminium capstone, the largest piece at the time (1884)
  • Recycling aluminium saves 95% of the energy needed to make it from ore
  • The protective oxide layer forms instantly in air – it's only 4 nm thick but incredibly effective
  • Aluminium is the third most abundant element in Earth's crust (8% by weight) – only oxygen and silicon are more common
  • Though it conducts electricity well, aluminium wiring was phased out in homes because it expands/contracts more than copper
  • An aluminium soda can weighs about 15 grams – the same weight as 20 paperclips

Atomic Structure

13 protons, 14 neutrons, 13 electrons
