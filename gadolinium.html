Gadolinium (Gd)

Atomic Number: 64

Basic Information

Symbol: Gd
Atomic Mass: 157.25 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f⁷ 5d¹ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Strongly paramagnetic (Curie point 20°C)
  • Silvery-white, ductile metal
  • Highest neutron absorption cross-section of any element
  • Named after mineral gadolinite
  • 7 unpaired electrons – highest magnetic moment

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1880 by Swiss chemist Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac. He detected its spectral lines in samples of gadolinite and samarskite.

Named after the mineral gadolinite, which itself was named after Finnish chemist and geologist Johan Gadolin.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1880: Marignac detects gadolinium spectroscopically
  • 1886: Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran isolates the metal
  • 1960s: MRI contrast agents developed
  • Modern: Essential for MRI and nuclear reactors

Common Uses

MRI contrast agent (Gd chelates)
Nuclear reactor control rods
Magnetostrictive alloys (Terfenol-D)
Solid-state laser dopant
Green phosphor in CRT displays
Neutron radiography screens
Electron microscopy substrates
Magnetic refrigeration prototypes

Interesting Facts

  • Gadolinium has the highest thermal neutron capture cross-section of any known element (49,000 barns for ¹⁵⁷Gd) – perfect for nuclear reactor control rods.
  • It is ferromagnetic below 20°C (room temperature in cold climates!), but paramagnetic above – the only lanthanide with Curie point near room temp.
  • Gadolinium-based contrast agents are used in about 1/3 of all MRI scans, enhancing image clarity by altering the magnetic properties of water.
  • With 7 unpaired 4f electrons, Gd³⁺ has the highest possible magnetic moment for a lanthanide ion.
  • Terfenol-D (Tb₀.₃Dy₀.₇Fe₂) with gadolinium additives exhibits the largest magnetostriction of any alloy.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is about 6.2 ppm, similar to niobium.

Atomic Structure

64 protons, 93 neutrons (in 157Gd), 64 electrons
