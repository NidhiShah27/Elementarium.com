Lanthanum (La)

Atomic Number: 57

Basic Information

Symbol: La
Atomic Mass: 138.90547 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 5d¹ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Soft, ductile, silvery-white metal
  • Tarnishes rapidly in air
  • Highly reactive, reacts with water
  • Namesake of the lanthanide series
  • One of the most reactive rare earth metals

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1839 by Swedish chemist Carl Gustaf Mosander. He partially decomposed a sample of cerium nitrate and extracted a new oxide he called "lantana".

The name comes from the Greek "lanthanein" meaning "to lie hidden", as it was hidden in cerium minerals.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1839: First isolated as oxide by Mosander
  • 1923: First relatively pure lanthanum produced
  • 1950s: Ion-exchange purification developed
  • Modern: Widely used in optics and batteries

Common Uses

Camera lenses
Nickel-metal hydride batteries
Carbon arc lamps
Fluid catalytic cracking
Electron microscopy cathodes
Hydrogen storage alloys
Laboratory reagent
Phosphate binder in medicine

Interesting Facts

  • Lanthanum is the first element in the lanthanide series, though its electron configuration is unusual ([Xe]5d¹6s² instead of 4f¹).
  • It oxidizes so easily that a sample the size of a pea will completely corrode within a day in moist air.
  • Lanthanum carbonate is used as a medication to reduce phosphate levels in patients with kidney failure.
  • The stable isotope 139La makes up 99.91% of natural lanthanum.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is about 32 ppm, making it more common than lead.
  • It's a key ingredient in mischmetal used in lighter flints.

Atomic Structure

57 protons, 82 neutrons (in 139La), 57 electrons
