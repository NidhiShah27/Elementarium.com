Nickel (Ni)

Atomic Number: 28

Basic Information

Symbol: Ni
Atomic Mass: 58.6934 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 10)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d⁸ 4s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, lustrous metal with a golden tinge [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1455°C, Boiling point: 2913°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance (oxidizes slowly) [citation:3]
  • Ferromagnetic (magnetic at room temperature) [citation:7]
  • Tough, malleable, and ductile [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Isolated and identified by Axel Fredrik Cronstedt in 1751 in Sweden. He extracted it from a mineral called "kupfernickel" (Old Nick's copper) and named it nickel after the mischievous sprite [citation:4][citation:8].

Miners called the ore "kupfernickel" because it looked like copper ore but yielded no copper – they blamed it on a mischievous sprite. Cronstedt proved it contained a new metal [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • ~3500 BCE: Nickel-containing meteoric iron used for tools [citation:4]
  • 1751: Cronstedt isolates nickel [citation:1]
  • 1889: Nickel strengthens steel (stainless steel developed) [citation:3]
  • 20th c.: Vital for superalloys, batteries, and coinage [citation:8]

Common Uses

Stainless steel (kitchenware, sinks) [citation:3]
Rechargeable batteries (NiCd, NiMH) [citation:2]
Coinage (nickels, euros) [citation:5]
Superalloys for jet engines [citation:7]
Magnets (Alnico alloys) [citation:1]
Electrical contacts & thermocouples [citation:4]
Electroplating (protective coating) [citation:8]
Catalysts (hydrogenation) [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Nickel is one of the few elements that can be found in metallic form on Earth, but it's much more common in meteorites. Some iron meteorites contain up to 20% nickel – ancient peoples used this meteoric iron for tools and weapons [citation:2].
  • The US five-cent coin called "nickel" is actually only 25% nickel and 75% copper. The Canadian nickel, however, is mostly nickel (with some copper) [citation:4].
  • Nickel plating is used to coat other metals to prevent corrosion – it's the shiny layer on many bathroom fixtures, musical instruments, and automotive trim. Nickel-chrome plating gives a durable, bright finish [citation:3].
  • Nickel is essential for the high-temperature performance of superalloys in gas turbines and rocket engines. It maintains strength at temperatures where other metals would soften [citation:5].
  • Some plants (like the Alyssum genus) hyperaccumulate nickel from the soil and are used for phytomining – they can contain up to 20% nickel in their sap, allowing nickel to be "farmed" from poor soils [citation:7].
  • Ancient Chinese "white copper" (pak-tong) was actually a nickel-copper alloy, known in the West as "German silver" centuries later. It was prized for its silvery appearance and corrosion resistance [citation:8].

Atomic Structure

28 protons, 31 neutrons (in 59Ni), 28 electrons

Try These Nickel Reactions

Nickel primarily exhibits the +2 oxidation state. It forms a variety of coordination complexes, especially green Ni²⁺ compounds, and is a key component in many alloys [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Ni + Copper (Monel alloy)
Experiment Idea (Conceptual): Nickel compounds are often green. Nickel(II) chloride (NiCl₂) is a beautiful green crystalline solid. In aqueous solution, it forms the green [Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺ complex. When you add ammonia, it forms the blue-violet [Ni(NH₃)₆]²⁺ complex – a classic coordination chemistry demonstration showing ligand exchange. The color change is striking and illustrates how the coordination environment affects the d-orbital splitting and thus the color of transition metal complexes [citation:1].
