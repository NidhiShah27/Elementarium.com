Beryllium (Be)

Atomic Number: 4

Basic Information

Symbol: Be
Atomic Mass: 9.012183 u
Category: Alkaline Earth Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[He] 2s²

Key Properties

  • Lightweight but extremely strong and stiff
  • High melting point (1287°C)
  • Excellent heat conductor
  • Transparent to X-rays
  • Highly toxic if inhaled (berylliosis)

Discovery & History

Discovered by Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin in 1798 in France as the oxide in beryl and emerald. First isolated independently by Friedrich Wöhler and Antoine Bussy in 1828.

The name comes from the mineral beryl (Greek "beryllos"). Originally called "glucinum" because its salts taste sweet.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1798: Vauquelin discovers beryllium oxide
  • 1828: Wöhler and Bussy isolate pure metal
  • 1920s: First commercial use in copper alloys
  • 1950s: Nuclear and aerospace applications

Common Uses

Aerospace components (light, stiff)
Nuclear reactors (neutron reflector)
X-ray tube windows
Beryllium-copper alloys (tools, springs)
Scientific instruments
High-end audio components
Gemstones (emerald, aquamarine)
Missile guidance systems

Interesting Facts

  • Emeralds and aquamarines are gemstone varieties of beryl (Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈) – color comes from trace impurities
  • Beryllium is transparent to X-rays, making it ideal for X-ray windows – it's 17 times more transparent than aluminum
  • Beryllium compounds are extremely toxic – they can cause chronic beryllium disease, a serious lung condition
  • It's lighter than aluminum but 6 times stiffer – a unique combination for aerospace
  • Beryllium is used as a neutron reflector in nuclear weapons to increase efficiency
  • Its salts taste sweet – hence the old name "glucinum" – but don't taste them, they're deadly!

Atomic Structure

4 protons, 5 neutrons, 4 electrons
