Sulfur (S)

Atomic Number: 16

Basic Information

Symbol: S
Atomic Mass: 32.06 u
Category: Nonmetal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ne] 3s² 3p⁴

Key Properties

  • Bright yellow, brittle solid at room temperature
  • Burns with a blue flame producing SO₂ (choking odor)
  • Famous for its "rotten egg" smell in compounds (H₂S)
  • Melting point: 115.2°C, boils at 444.6°C
  • Found in nature as pure element (near volcanoes)

Discovery & History

Known since ancient times – referred to in the Bible as "brimstone" (burning stone). Used by Egyptians for bleaching and by Greeks for fumigation.

The name comes from Latin "sulfurium" and Sanskrit "sulvere." Antoine Lavoisier recognized it as an element in 1777.

Historical Timeline:
  • Ancient: Used in religious ceremonies and medicines
  • 1777: Lavoisier lists sulfur as an element
  • 1867: Frasch process developed for mining sulfur
  • 20th c.: Vital for vulcanizing rubber and fertilizers

Common Uses

Vulcanizing rubber (tires)
Sulfuric acid production
Fungicides and pesticides
Food preservative (sulfites)
Matches and gunpowder
Skin treatments (acne, psoriasis)
Lithium-sulfur batteries
Water purification

Interesting Facts

  • The "rotten egg" smell is hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) – highly toxic and smells at very low concentrations
  • Sulfur has over 30 allotropes (different crystal forms) – more than any other element
  • Volcanic emissions are rich in sulfur compounds – giving volcanoes their characteristic smell
  • Sulfur is essential for life – found in amino acids (cysteine, methionine)
  • Vulcanization (adding sulfur to rubber) was discovered by Charles Goodyear in 1839
  • Sulfuric acid is the most produced industrial chemical in the world

Atomic Structure

16 protons, 16 neutrons, 16 electrons
