Tellurium (Te)

Atomic Number: 52

Basic Information

Symbol: Te
Atomic Mass: 127.60 u
Category: Metalloid
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p⁴

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, brittle metalloid
  • Semiconducting properties
  • Reacts with gold to form gold telluride
  • Causes "tellurium breath" – garlic odor
  • Named after the Earth (Latin "tellus")

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1782 by Franz-Joseph Müller von Reichenstein in Romania. He isolated it from gold ore and initially thought it was antimony. Martin Klaproth confirmed it as a new element in 1798 and named it tellurium.

The name comes from Latin "tellus" meaning Earth, following the pattern of naming after celestial bodies (uranium, selenium).

Historical Timeline:
  • 1782: Müller discovers tellurium
  • 1798: Klaproth confirms and names it
  • 19th c.: Used in alloys and pigments
  • 20th c.: Essential for solar cells and thermoelectrics

Common Uses

Cadmium telluride solar cells
Thermoelectric devices (cooling)
Phase-change memory (DVDs)
Alloys (improves machinability)
Rubber vulcanization
Colored glass and ceramics
Semiconductor doping
Gold telluride (calaverite) ore

Interesting Facts

  • Tellurium causes "tellurium breath" – even tiny amounts (micrograms) produce a strong garlic odor that can last for weeks
  • It's named after the Earth (tellus) – its sister element selenium was named after the moon (selene)
  • Gold telluride (calaverite) is a beautiful mineral that was a major source of gold in the Australian gold rush
  • Cadmium telluride (CdTe) solar panels are the second most common photovoltaic technology after silicon
  • Tellurium is one of the rarest stable elements – as rare as platinum in Earth's crust
  • Some bacteria can metabolize tellurium compounds, producing elemental tellurium crystals

Atomic Structure

52 protons, 76 neutrons, 52 electrons
