Bismuth (Bi)

Atomic Number: 83

Basic Information

Symbol: Bi
Atomic Mass: 208.98040 u
Category: Post-Transition Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p³

Key Properties

  • Forms beautiful iridescent crystals
  • Expands when it freezes (like water)
  • Least toxic heavy metal (safe for medicine)
  • Diamagnetic – repelled by magnets
  • Naturally occurring with a pinkish hue

Discovery & History

Known since ancient times, but often confused with lead and tin. First identified as a distinct metal by Claude François Geoffroy in 1753.

The name comes from the German "Bisemutum" or "Weisse Masse" (white mass), possibly from Arabic "bi ismid" meaning having the properties of antimony.

Historical Timeline:
  • Medieval: Used in alloys for printing type
  • 1753: Geoffroy proves it's distinct from lead
  • 1830s: First used in cosmetics
  • 20th c.: Pharmaceuticals and electronics

Common Uses

Pepto-Bismol (bismuth subsalicylate)
Rainbow crystals for collectors
Low-melting-point alloys (Wood's metal)
Nail polish and cosmetics
Nuclear reactor coolant
Lead-free hunting shot
Catalyst in organic synthesis
Solders and fusible plugs

Interesting Facts

  • Bismuth crystals are artificially grown – the rainbow colors come from an oxide layer, not the metal itself
  • Pepto-Bismol gets its pink color and stomach-soothing properties from bismuth subsalicylate
  • Like water, bismuth expands when it freezes – one of the few substances with this property
  • It's the heaviest stable element – all heavier elements are radioactive
  • Bismuth is so diamagnetic that it can levitate between strong magnets
  • For over 100 years, bismuth was thought to be stable, but in 2003 it was discovered to be very slightly radioactive (half-life 20 billion billion years)

Atomic Structure

83 protons, 126 neutrons, 83 electrons
