Fluorine (F)

Atomic Number: 9

Basic Information

Symbol: F
Atomic Mass: 18.998403 u
Category: Halogen
State at Room Temperature: Gas
Electron Configuration:
[He] 2s² 2p⁵

Key Properties

  • Most reactive and electronegative element
  • Extremely toxic and corrosive
  • Pale yellow gas at room temperature
  • Forms compounds with almost all elements
  • 13th most abundant element in Earth's crust

Discovery & History

Isolated in 1886 by Henri Moissan in France after 74 years of attempts by other chemists (many died). He electrolyzed potassium bifluoride (KHF₂) in liquid HF.

The name comes from Latin "fluere" meaning to flow, because fluorite (CaF₂) was used as a flux in metal smelting.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1810: Ampère proposes existence of fluorine
  • 1886: Moissan finally isolates fluorine
  • 1906: Moissan receives Nobel Prize
  • 1930s: Fluorocarbons developed for refrigeration

Common Uses

Toothpaste (fluoride)
Refrigerants (Freon)
Teflon (non-stick coating)
Uranium enrichment (UF₆)
Fluorinated drugs (Prozac, Lipitor)
Lithium-ion battery electrolytes
Aluminum production (cryolite)
Etching glass

Interesting Facts

  • Fluorine is so reactive it can cause glass and even water to burn – it reacts explosively with almost everything
  • Many early fluorine researchers died from poisoning – it was called the "chemist's graveyard"
  • Fluoride in toothpaste prevents cavities by strengthening tooth enamel (forming fluorapatite)
  • Teflon (PTFE) was discovered accidentally by DuPont in 1938 – it's now used everywhere from cookware to space suits
  • Fluorine is the 13th most abundant element in Earth's crust, but very reactive so rarely found pure
  • Uranium hexafluoride (UF₆) is the only uranium compound that is gaseous at room temperature – essential for enrichment

Atomic Structure

9 protons, 10 neutrons, 9 electrons
