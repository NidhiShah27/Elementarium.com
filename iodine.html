Iodine (I)

Atomic Number: 53

Basic Information

Symbol: I
Atomic Mass: 126.90447 u
Category: Halogen
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p⁵

Key Properties

  • Dark purple, lustrous solid
  • Sublimes to purple vapor when heated
  • Essential nutrient (thyroid hormone)
  • Least reactive halogen
  • Named from Greek "iodes" meaning violet

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1811 by Bernard Courtois in France while extracting sodium and potassium compounds from seaweed ash. He accidentally added too much sulfuric acid and saw purple vapors.

The name comes from Greek "iodes" meaning violet-colored, because of its distinctive purple vapor.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1811: Courtois discovers iodine
  • 1813: Gay-Lussac names it iodine
  • 1820: First medical use (goiter treatment)
  • 20th c.: Iodized salt prevents deficiency

Common Uses

Antiseptic (tincture of iodine)
Iodized salt
Thyroid medication
X-ray contrast agents
Water purification tablets
Polarizing film for LCDs
Dyes and pigments
Nutritional supplement

Interesting Facts

  • Iodine is essential for human health – deficiency causes goiter (enlarged thyroid) and intellectual disability
  • Iodine sublimes at room temperature – solid iodine turns directly into purple vapor without melting
  • The name comes from the Greek word for violet, describing its beautiful purple vapor
  • The "iodine clock reaction" is a classic chemistry demonstration – a colorless solution suddenly turns dark blue
  • Iodine-131 is a radioactive isotope used to treat thyroid cancer – it's taken up by thyroid tissue
  • Seaweed (kelp) concentrates iodine from seawater and was the original commercial source

Atomic Structure

53 protons, 74 neutrons, 53 electrons
