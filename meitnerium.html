Meitnerium (Mt)

Atomic Number: 109

Basic Information

Symbol: Mt
Atomic Mass: [278] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 9)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d⁷ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~2400°C (predicted, similar to iridium) [citation:8]
  • Density: ~37 g/cm³ (estimated, very high) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of iridium (Group 9) [citation:1]
  • Predicted to form volatile compounds like MtO₃Cl, similar to Ir [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First synthesized in 1982 by a team led by Peter Armbruster and Gottfried Münzenberg at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. They bombarded bismuth-209 with iron-58 ions, producing a single atom of 266Mt and identifying its alpha decay [citation:1][citation:8].

This was the first time element 109 was created. The reaction used was a "cold fusion" approach, and the experiment took over a week to produce just one atom [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1982: GSI team produces a single atom of element 109 [citation:1]
  • 1984: Confirmation with additional atoms [citation:8]
  • 1992: IUPAC credits GSI with discovery [citation:1]
  • 1997: IUPAC officially names element 109 meitnerium (after Lise Meitner) [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Meitnerium is named after Lise Meitner, the Austrian-Swedish physicist who co-discovered nuclear fission. She was one of the most significant women in physics history, and this element honors her contributions [citation:4].
  • The original 1982 experiment producing 266Mt created just one atom after more than a week of continuous bombardment. The atom was identified by its characteristic alpha decay chain, a testament to the precision of nuclear chemistry [citation:1].
  • No chemical experiments have yet been performed on meitnerium due to its short half-lives and low production rates. However, theoretical calculations predict it should behave like a typical Group 9 element, forming compounds analogous to iridium [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 278Mt, has a half-life of about 8 seconds. It is produced in the decay chain of 286Mc (moscovium) and was discovered in 2009 [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects in meitnerium are predicted to be significant. The 7s electrons are contracted and stabilized, while the 6d orbitals are expanded. This may affect oxidation states and complex formation compared to iridium [citation:2].
  • Meitnerium is one of the few elements named after a woman. Others include curium (Marie Curie) and roentgenium (Wilhelm Röntgen, male). Lise Meitner's legacy is particularly notable as she was overlooked for the Nobel Prize awarded to Otto Hahn for fission [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

109 protons, 169 neutrons (in 278Mt), 109 electrons
