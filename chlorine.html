Chlorine (Cl)

Atomic Number: 17

Basic Information

Symbol: Cl
Atomic Mass: 35.45 u
Category: Halogen
State at Room Temperature: Gas
Electron Configuration:
[Ne] 3s² 3p⁵

Key Properties

  • Greenish-yellow gas with pungent odor
  • Highly toxic and reactive
  • Used as disinfectant (water treatment)
  • Second most reactive halogen
  • Essential for life as chloride ion

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1774 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele in Sweden, who thought it contained oxygen. Named and identified as an element by Humphry Davy in 1810.

The name comes from Greek "chloros" meaning greenish-yellow, referring to its color.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1774: Scheele produces chlorine gas
  • 1810: Davy confirms it's an element
  • 1915: Used as chemical weapon (WWI)
  • 20th c.: Becomes essential for water purification

Common Uses

Water disinfection
Swimming pool sanitizer
PVC plastic production
Bleaching paper and textiles
Pharmaceuticals
Solvents (chloroform, methylene chloride)
Chemical warfare (historical)
Table salt (NaCl)

Interesting Facts

  • Chlorine gas was the first chemical weapon used in warfare (WWI, 1915) – it caused panic and suffocation
  • Chlorine is added to drinking water worldwide – it has saved millions of lives from waterborne diseases
  • The element gets its name from its color – "chloros" is Greek for greenish-yellow
  • Chlorine has two stable isotopes: Cl-35 (76%) and Cl-37 (24%) – their ratio is used in hydrology studies
  • Common salt (NaCl) is the source of most chlorine – it's produced by electrolysis of brine
  • Chlorine is essential for making PVC – the third most widely used plastic in the world

Atomic Structure

17 protons, 18 neutrons, 17 electrons
