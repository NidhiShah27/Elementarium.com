Oganesson (Og)

Atomic Number: 118

Basic Information

Symbol: Og
Atomic Mass: [294] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Noble Gas (predicted)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted)
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰ 7s² 7p⁶ (predicted)

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive (does not occur naturally)
  • Heaviest element ever synthesized
  • Last element in Period 7 (noble gas)
  • May not behave like a typical noble gas (relativistic effects)
  • Half-life: < 1 millisecond (very unstable)

Discovery & History

Discovered in 2002 by a joint team of Russian and American scientists at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, and Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, USA.

Named in honor of Yuri Oganessian, a pioneer in superheavy element research. It's only the second element named after a living person (after seaborgium).

Historical Timeline:
  • 2002: First atom of oganesson produced
  • 2006: Confirmed by IUPAC
  • 2016: Officially named oganesson
  • 21st c.: Only a few atoms ever created

Interesting Facts

  • Oganesson is the heaviest element ever created – only a few atoms have ever been produced, and they exist for less than a millisecond
  • Relativistic effects are so extreme in oganesson that its electron shells may be smeared out like a gas – it might not be a gas at all, but a solid at room temperature!
  • Despite being a noble gas, oganesson is predicted to be reactive enough to form compounds (like OgF₄) – shattering the noble gas trend
  • It's named after Yuri Oganessian, who led the team that discovered many superheavy elements – he was alive to see the honor
  • The most stable isotope, 294Og, has a half-life of about 0.7 milliseconds – barely enough time to detect it before it decays
  • Oganesson completes the 7th period of the periodic table – it's the last element currently known

Atomic Structure

118 protons, 176 neutrons (in 294Og), 118 electrons
