Neodymium (Nd)

Atomic Number: 60

Basic Information

Symbol: Nd
Atomic Mass: 144.242 u
Category: Lanthanide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f⁴ 6s²

Key Properties

  • Bright silvery metal, tarnishes in air
  • Forms the strongest permanent magnets (Nd₂Fe₁₄B)
  • Salts are purple-red
  • Pyrophoric; chips can ignite in air
  • Name from Greek "neos" (new) + "didymos" (twin)

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1885 by Austrian chemist Carl Auer von Welsbach. He separated the previously thought element "didymium" into praseodymium and neodymium.

Name comes from Greek neos (new) + didymos (twin), reflecting that it was the "new twin" extracted from didymium alongside praseodymium.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1841: Mosander extracts "didymium" (mixture)
  • 1885: Auer von Welsbach splits didymium into Pr & Nd
  • 1925: First relatively pure neodymium metal produced
  • Modern: Essential for super-strong magnets and lasers

Common Uses

Nd₂Fe₁₄B permanent magnets
Motors in electric vehicles
Hard disk drive actuators
Wind turbine generators
Nd:YAG and Nd:glass lasers
Didymium glass for welding goggles
Purple glass and ceramic glazes
Contrast agent in microscopy

Interesting Facts

  • Neodymium magnets are so strong that a tiny cube can lift 1000x its own weight – but they are brittle and corrode easily.
  • The Nd:YAG laser is the workhorse of industrial lasers, used for engraving, welding, and even eye surgery.
  • Its salts are a beautiful purple-red, used to color glass for decorative vases and art pieces.
  • Natural neodymium is a mix of seven isotopes; 142Nd, 143Nd, and 144Nd are the most abundant.
  • Like other lanthanides, it is pyrophoric: neodymium chips can ignite spontaneously in air.
  • Abundance in Earth's crust is about 38 ppm, similar to copper and more common than lead.

Atomic Structure

60 protons, 84 neutrons (in 144Nd), 60 electrons
