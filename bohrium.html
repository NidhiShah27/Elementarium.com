Bohrium (Bh)

Atomic Number: 107

Basic Information

Symbol: Bh
Atomic Mass: [270] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 7)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d⁵ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~2400°C (predicted, similar to rhenium) [citation:8]
  • Density: ~37 g/cm³ (estimated, extremely high) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of rhenium (Group 7) [citation:1]
  • Forms volatile oxychloride (BhO₃Cl), similar to Re and Tc [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First unambiguously synthesized in 1981 by a team led by Peter Armbruster and Gottfried Münzenberg at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. They bombarded bismuth-209 with chromium-54 ions, producing 262Bh and identifying its alpha decay [citation:1][citation:8].

Earlier attempts in 1976 by a Soviet team at Dubna produced a spontaneous fission activity, but their results were not conclusive enough for official recognition [citation:1][citation:8].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1976: Dubna team reports element 107 but evidence inconclusive [citation:4]
  • 1981: GSI team conclusively synthesizes and identifies element 107 [citation:1]
  • 1992: IUPAC credits GSI with discovery [citation:1]
  • 1997: IUPAC officially names element 107 bohrium (after Niels Bohr) [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Bohrium is named after the Danish physicist Niels Bohr, who made fundamental contributions to understanding atomic structure and quantum theory. It was the first element named after a physicist (rather than a chemist) [citation:4].
  • The GSI team originally proposed "nielsbohrium" (Ns) to honor Niels Bohr, but IUPAC shortened it to "bohrium" in 1997. This followed a tradition of using only the surname for element names (like einsteinium, curium) [citation:4].
  • Bohrium is one of the few superheavy elements where chemical studies have been successfully performed. In 2000, scientists studied the volatility of BhO₃Cl (bohrium oxychloride) using just a few atoms and found it behaved similarly to its lighter homologs technetium and rhenium [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 270Bh, has a half-life of about 61 seconds. This relatively long half-life (for a superheavy element) was discovered in 2007 and allows for chemical studies [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects in bohrium are predicted to stabilize the +7 oxidation state, similar to rhenium. However, the 6d orbitals are significantly expanded, which may affect complex formation and bond lengths compared to Re [citation:2].
  • The synthesis of bohrium is extremely difficult – the fusion of bismuth-209 and chromium-54 is one of the most "cold fusion" reactions used to create superheavy elements. The cross-section (probability) is incredibly low, requiring weeks of beam time to produce a few atoms [citation:1].

Atomic Structure

107 protons, 163 neutrons (in 270Bh), 107 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic bohrium chemistry experiment uses isothermal gas chromatography to compare BhO₃Cl with TcO₃Cl and ReO₃Cl. Atoms of 267Bh (half-life ~17 seconds) are produced in a nuclear reaction, rapidly converted to oxychlorides in a hot gas stream containing O₂ and Cl₂, and passed through a chromatography column. By measuring deposition temperatures, scientists found BhO₃Cl is less volatile than TcO₃Cl but similar to ReO₃Cl – confirming relativistic effects and group 7 placement, while showing the influence of the 5f electron shell on bonding. This was one of the first chemical studies of an element heavier than 106 [citation:1].
