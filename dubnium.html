Dubnium (Db)

Atomic Number: 105

Basic Information

Symbol: Db
Atomic Mass: [268] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 5)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d³ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~2200°C (predicted) [citation:8]
  • Density: ~29 g/cm³ (estimated, extremely high) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of tantalum (Group 5) [citation:1]
  • Forms volatile pentachloride (DbCl₅), similar to Nb and Ta [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First reported in 1968 by a team led by Georgy Flerov at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Dubna, Russia. They bombarded americium-243 with neon-22 ions and detected a spontaneous fission activity they attributed to element 105 [citation:1][citation:8].

In 1970, a team led by Albert Ghiorso at Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory (LBL) conclusively synthesized dubnium by bombarding californium-249 with nitrogen-15 ions, producing 260Db and identifying its alpha decay to lawrencium [citation:1][citation:8].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1968: Dubna team reports element 105, proposes name "nielsbohrium" [citation:4]
  • 1970: Berkeley team confirms synthesis, proposes name "hahnium" (Ha) [citation:8]
  • 1992: IUPAC concludes both teams contributed; credit shared [citation:1]
  • 1997: IUPAC officially names element 105 dubnium (after Dubna) [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Dubnium is named after Dubna, Russia, the site of the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research. This was a compromise after a long naming dispute – Americans had proposed "hahnium" (after Otto Hahn) for this element [citation:4].
  • The naming controversy for element 105 was particularly heated. For over 25 years, American textbooks called it "hahnium" (Ha), while Russian and European sources used "nielsbohrium" or "dubnium." The 1997 compromise named element 105 dubnium and element 107 bohrium [citation:4].
  • Early chemical studies of dubnium (using just a few atoms) showed it behaves like a Group 5 element, but with interesting relativistic effects. The compound DbBr₅ was found to be more volatile than its lighter homolog TaBr₅, confirming predictions [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 268Db, has a half-life of about 16 hours – the longest-lived of any element beyond lawrencium. This was discovered in 2003 and allows for chemical studies [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects are predicted to stabilize the +5 oxidation state of dubnium, but also make the +3 state more accessible than for tantalum. This unique behavior is an active area of superheavy element chemistry [citation:2].
  • The first atom-at-a-time chemistry on dubnium was performed in 1988 using the isotope 262Db (half-life 34 seconds). Scientists studied its chloride and bromide complexes, comparing them to niobium and tantalum [citation:1].

Atomic Structure

105 protons, 163 neutrons (in 268Db), 105 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic dubnium chemistry experiment uses isothermal gas chromatography to compare DbCl₅ with NbCl₅ and TaCl₅. Atoms of 262Db (half-life 34 seconds) are produced in a nuclear reaction, rapidly chlorinated to form DbCl₅, and passed through a chromatography column with a temperature gradient. By measuring deposition temperatures, scientists found DbCl₅ is more volatile than TaCl₅ but similar to NbCl₅ – confirming relativistic effects and group 5 placement, while also showing the influence of the 5f electron shell [citation:1].
