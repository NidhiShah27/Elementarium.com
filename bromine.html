Bromine (Br)

Atomic Number: 35

Basic Information

Symbol: Br
Atomic Mass: 79.904 u
Category: Halogen
State at Room Temperature: Liquid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p⁵

Key Properties

  • Only nonmetallic element liquid at room temperature
  • Reddish-brown, fuming liquid with pungent odor
  • Highly toxic and corrosive
  • Less reactive than chlorine but more than iodine
  • Named from Greek "bromos" meaning stench

Discovery & History

Discovered independently by two chemists in 1825-26: Carl Jacob Löwig (Germany) and Antoine Jérôme Balard (France). Balard published first and is credited.

The name comes from Greek "bromos" meaning stench, because of its strong, irritating smell.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1825: Löwig isolates bromine
  • 1826: Balard publishes discovery
  • 1840s: Used in daguerreotype photography
  • 20th c.: Flame retardants and pharmaceuticals

Common Uses

Flame retardants
Pharmaceuticals (sedatives)
Photography chemicals
Water purification
Brominated gasoline (historical)
Drilling fluids
Pesticides and fumigants
Dyes and pigments

Interesting Facts

  • Bromine is one of only two elements that are liquid at room temperature (the other is mercury)
  • Its name means "stench" in Greek – and rightly so, as bromine vapor has an overpowering, irritating smell
  • Bromine was discovered almost simultaneously by two chemists – Balard's professor recommended he name it "muride", but Balard chose "bromine"
  • Silver bromide (AgBr) is light-sensitive and was the basis of photographic film for over 150 years
  • Brominated flame retardants are added to electronics and furniture to prevent fires – but some are now banned due to toxicity
  • The Dead Sea is the world's largest source of bromine – it contains about 5 grams of bromine per ton of water

Atomic Structure

35 protons, 45 neutrons, 35 electrons
