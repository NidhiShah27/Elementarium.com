Cadmium (Cd)

Atomic Number: 48

Basic Information

Symbol: Cd
Atomic Mass: 112.414 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 12)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Soft, bluish-white, and ductile metal [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 321°C, Boiling point: 767°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance (protective oxide) [citation:3]
  • Toxic to humans (bioaccumulates in kidneys) [citation:7]
  • Good electrical and thermal conductivity [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1817 by Friedrich Stromeyer in Germany. He found it as an impurity in zinc carbonate (calamine) and isolated it by roasting and reduction. The name comes from the Latin "cadmia" and Greek "kadmeia" – ancient names for zinc ores [citation:4][citation:8].

Around the same time, Hermann and Roloff also independently discovered cadmium in zinc ores. Stromeyer's priority was established when he published his findings in 1818 [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1817: Stromeyer discovers cadmium in zinc carbonate [citation:4]
  • 1818: Publishes his findings; cadmium named [citation:1]
  • 20th c.: Widely used for electroplating, pigments, Ni-Cd batteries [citation:3]
  • 21st c.: Use restricted due to toxicity; replaced in many applications [citation:8]

Common Uses

Nickel-cadmium (Ni-Cd) batteries [citation:3]
Cadmium pigments (yellows, reds) [citation:2]
Corrosion-resistant electroplating [citation:5]
Nuclear reactor control rods [citation:7]
Cadmium telluride (CdTe) solar cells [citation:1]
Stabilizer in PVC plastics [citation:4]
Quantum dots for imaging [citation:8]
Fusible alloys (low-melting solders) [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Cadmium is highly toxic – it accumulates in the body and can cause kidney damage and bone disease. The infamous "itai-itai" disease in Japan was caused by cadmium poisoning from mining waste [citation:2].
  • Cadmium pigments produce incredibly vibrant yellows, oranges, and reds that are stable even at high temperatures. Artists like Matisse and Monet used cadmium colors extensively – they're still prized for their brilliance [citation:3].
  • Cadmium telluride (CdTe) is the second most common photovoltaic material after silicon. CdTe solar panels are cheaper to manufacture and have a lower carbon footprint than silicon panels [citation:5].
  • Cadmium has a high neutron absorption cross-section, making it ideal for nuclear reactor control rods. When inserted into the core, they absorb neutrons and slow or stop the chain reaction [citation:7].
  • The Ni-Cd battery was one of the first rechargeable batteries widely used in portable electronics. Though largely replaced by lithium-ion, they're still used in some power tools and aircraft due to their reliability [citation:1].
  • Cadmium is often found as a byproduct of zinc refining – it's chemically similar to zinc and tends to follow it in geological processes [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

48 protons, 64 neutrons (in 112Cd), 48 electrons

Try These Cadmium Reactions

Cadmium exhibits the +2 oxidation state almost exclusively. It forms colorful compounds, particularly sulfides, and is known for its use in pigments and batteries [citation:1]. Here are some combinations to explore:

Cd Se
Cd + Selenium (CdSe quantum dots)
Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The formation of cadmium sulfide (CdS) is a classic demonstration of precipitation and pigment formation. When a solution of a cadmium salt (like Cd(NO₃)₂) is mixed with a source of sulfide ions (like Na₂S or H₂S), a brilliant yellow precipitate of CdS forms immediately. This is the famous "cadmium yellow" pigment used by artists. The color can range from lemon-yellow to orange-yellow depending on particle size and reaction conditions. In a lab, this dramatic color change is both beautiful and instructive [citation:1].
