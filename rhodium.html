Rhodium (Rh)

Atomic Number: 45

Basic Information

Symbol: Rh
Atomic Mass: 102.9055 u
Category: Transition Metal (Platinum Group)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d⁸ 5s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, hard, and durable precious metal [citation:1]
  • Very high melting point: 1964°C [citation:5]
  • Extremely reflective and corrosion-resistant [citation:3]
  • One of the rarest and most expensive precious metals [citation:7]
  • Excellent catalyst for automotive catalytic converters [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1803 by William Hyde Wollaston in London, England. He dissolved platinum ore in aqua regia, then precipitated out other metals, leaving behind a beautiful red solution from which he isolated rhodium [citation:4][citation:8].

The name comes from the Greek "rhodon" meaning rose, after the rose-red color of its salts. Wollaston discovered both palladium and rhodium in the same year [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1803: Wollaston discovers rhodium in crude platinum ore [citation:4]
  • 1805: Publishes his method for isolating rhodium [citation:1]
  • 1970s: Catalytic converters create massive demand for rhodium [citation:3]
  • 21st c.: Rhodium becomes one of the most valuable precious metals [citation:8]

Common Uses

Catalytic converters (reduces NOx) [citation:3]
Jewelry plating (white gold finishing) [citation:2]
Hypoallergenic plating for watches [citation:5]
Catalyst for hydrogenation [citation:7]
Laboratory crucibles & thermocouples [citation:1]
Glass industry (fiberglass bushings) [citation:4]
Electrical contacts & connectors [citation:8]
Optical instruments (reflective coatings) [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Rhodium is the most expensive precious metal in the world – far more valuable than gold or platinum. Its price has exceeded $20,000 per ounce, driven by demand for catalytic converters [citation:2].
  • About 80% of all rhodium produced is used in catalytic converters, where it helps convert harmful nitrogen oxides (NOx) into harmless nitrogen gas. There's no practical substitute for this application [citation:3].
  • That shiny white finish on your white gold wedding ring is actually rhodium plating. White gold is naturally yellowish, so jewelers plate it with rhodium for a brilliant, durable, hypoallergenic white surface [citation:5].
  • Rhodium is incredibly reflective – it reflects about 80% of visible light and is used in high-end optical instruments, searchlight mirrors, and even some spacecraft components [citation:7].
  • The famous "Monsanto process" for producing acetic acid (vinegar) uses a rhodium-iodine catalyst. This process was one of the first industrial applications of homogeneous catalysis [citation:1].
  • Rhodium's price is famously volatile – it can swing from $1,000 to $20,000 per ounce within a few years based on auto industry demand and mining disruptions [citation:4].

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): One of the most important reactions in organic chemistry is "Wilkinson's hydrogenation." Wilkinson's catalyst, (Ph₃P)₃RhCl (where Ph is phenyl), was the first homogeneous catalyst to efficiently add hydrogen to carbon-carbon double bonds. It revolutionized organic synthesis and earned Geoffrey Wilkinson a Nobel Prize. The rhodium center shuttles between Rh(I) and Rh(III) oxidation states, demonstrating rhodium's elegant catalytic cycle. In the lab, you can observe the red-brown color of many rhodium complexes [citation:1].
