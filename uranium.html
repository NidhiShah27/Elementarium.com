Uranium (U)

Atomic Number: 92

Basic Information

Symbol: U
Atomic Mass: 238.0289 u (most abundant isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f³ 6d¹ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Silvery-gray, dense radioactive metal that tarnishes in air
  • Named after the planet Uranus, discovered eight years earlier
  • Heaviest naturally occurring element (primordial)
  • Fissile isotope ²³⁵U used in nuclear reactors and weapons
  • About 40 times more abundant than silver in Earth's crust

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1789 by German chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth while analyzing pitchblende from the Joachimsthal silver mines. He named it after the recently discovered planet Uranus.

Its radioactivity was first detected in 1896 by Henri Becquerel, leading to the discovery of radioactivity. The fissile nature of ²³⁵U was established in 1939, ushering in the nuclear age.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1789: Klaproth discovers uranium oxide
  • 1841: Eugène-Melchior Péligot isolates pure uranium metal
  • 1896: Becquerel discovers radioactivity in uranium salts
  • 1939: Hahn and Strassmann discover nuclear fission
  • 1942: First self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction (Chicago Pile-1)

Common Uses

Nuclear power reactor fuel
Nuclear weapons (²³⁵U)
Naval propulsion (submarines, aircraft carriers)
Depleted uranium (armor, ammunition)
Dating geological samples (U-Pb dating)
Radiation shielding (depleted uranium)
Colored glass and ceramics (uranium glass)
Catalyst in organic chemistry

Interesting Facts

  • Uranium is the heaviest naturally occurring element; all heavier elements are synthetic.
  • Natural uranium is a mixture of three isotopes: ²³⁸U (99.27%), ²³⁵U (0.72%), and trace ²³⁴U (0.0055%).
  • It is about 500 times more abundant than gold in Earth's crust.
  • A kilogram of uranium-235 contains the energy equivalent of about 3 million kilograms of coal.
  • Uranium glass (vaseline glass) glows bright green under UV light and was a popular household item in the 1930s.
  • The Oklo natural nuclear reactor in Gabon, Africa, spontaneously operated 2 billion years ago using natural uranium.

Atomic Structure

92 protons, 146 neutrons (in 238U), 92 electrons

Try These Uranium Reactions

Uranium is reactive and exhibits multiple oxidation states (III, IV, V, VI). The uranyl ion (UO₂²⁺) is the most stable form in solution and in minerals. Uranium metal tarnishes rapidly in air.
