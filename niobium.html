Niobium (Nb)

Atomic Number: 41

Basic Information

Symbol: Nb
Atomic Mass: 92.90637 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 5)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d⁴ 5s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Soft, ductile, and lustrous gray metal [citation:1]
  • Very high melting point: 2477°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance (oxide layer) [citation:3]
  • Becomes a superconductor at low temperatures [citation:7]
  • Resists attack by most acids (except HF) [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1801 by Charles Hatchett in England. While analyzing a mineral from the United States (columbite), he identified a new element and named it "columbium" (Cb) after Columbia, the poetic name for America [citation:4][citation:8].

In 1846, Heinrich Rose discovered what he thought was a new element in tantalum ores and named it "niobium" after Niobe, daughter of Tantalus. It was later proven to be the same as columbium. The name "niobium" was officially adopted by IUPAC in 1949 [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1801: Hatchett discovers "columbium" [citation:4]
  • 1846: Rose isolates "niobium" [citation:1]
  • 1866: Scientists confirm they are identical [citation:3]
  • 1960s: Nb-Ti alloy becomes commercial superconductor [citation:8]

Common Uses

Superconducting magnets (MRI, accelerators) [citation:3]
Microalloyed steel (HSLA) for pipelines [citation:2]
Jet engine components & rocket nozzles [citation:5]
Lithium niobate (LiNbO₃) for mobile phones [citation:7]
Body jewelry & piercings (hypoallergenic) [citation:1]
Capacitors in electronics [citation:4]
Nuclear industry (low neutron capture) [citation:8]
Cathodic protection systems [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Niobium is a key ingredient in high-strength low-alloy (HSLA) steel – adding just 0.1% niobium doubles the strength of steel. It's used in gas pipelines, skyscrapers, and bridges, including the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge in Japan (world's longest suspension bridge) [citation:2].
  • Niobium-titanium (Nb-Ti) and niobium-tin (Nb₃Sn) alloys are the workhorses of superconductivity – they're used in MRI machines, particle accelerators (like the LHC at CERN), and fusion reactor magnets. These alloys conduct electricity with zero resistance when cooled with liquid helium [citation:3].
  • Lithium niobate (LiNbO₃) is an incredible crystal with both piezoelectric and optical properties – it's used in mobile phone filters (SAW filters), optical modulators for fiber optics, and even quantum computing research [citation:5].
  • The naming controversy lasted over 150 years – American metallurgists continued to use "columbium" (Cb) well into the 20th century, and some industry groups still prefer it. Even today, you'll find "columbium" in some metallurgical literature [citation:7].
  • Niobium is completely hypoallergenic and doesn't react with skin, making it ideal for body piercings, medical implants, and jewelry for people with metal allergies. It can be anodized to produce a rainbow of colors [citation:1].
  • In 2012, Austria issued a silver-niobium bimetallic coin featuring the Large Hadron Collider – the niobium part was anodized to produce a beautiful blue-green color (anodizing changes the oxide layer thickness) [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

41 protons, 52 neutrons (in 93Nb), 41 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): One of niobium's most beautiful properties is its anodization. When a voltage is applied to niobium metal in an electrolyte bath, the oxide layer thickness increases, and due to interference effects, the metal surface changes color. Low voltages produce blues and purples, higher voltages yield greens, yellows, and reds. This is why niobium jewelry comes in stunning iridescent colors – all without dyes or pigments, just pure physics and chemistry. You can demonstrate this in a lab with a simple power supply and electrolyte [citation:1].
