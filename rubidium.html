Rubidium (Rb)

Atomic Number: 37

Basic Information

Symbol: Rb
Atomic Mass: 85.4678 u
Category: Alkali Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid (melts at 39.3°C)
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 5s¹

Key Properties

  • Extremely reactive – ignites spontaneously in air
  • Second most electropositive element
  • Melts in your hand (just above room temperature)
  • Reacts explosively with water
  • 16th most abundant element in Earth's crust

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1861 by Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff in Germany using spectroscopy. They named it after the Latin "rubidus" meaning deep red, for its spectral lines.

First isolated in 1863 by Bunsen through electrolysis of rubidium chloride.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1861: Bunsen & Kirchhoff discover rubidium
  • 1863: First isolated by Bunsen
  • 1920s: Used in photocells
  • 1990s: Bose-Einstein condensates with Rb

Common Uses

Atomic clocks (GPS satellites)
Photocells and photomultipliers
Research chemical
Bose-Einstein condensates
Specialty glasses
Getter in vacuum tubes
Medical imaging (Rb-82)
Ion propulsion (experimental)

Interesting Facts

  • Rubidium is so reactive that it ignites spontaneously in air and must be stored under oil or in sealed ampoules
  • It melts at just 39.3°C – it would liquefy in your hand on a hot day!
  • Rubidium atomic clocks are used in GPS satellites – they're more stable than quartz clocks
  • Rubidium was the first element used to create a Bose-Einstein condensate (Nobel Prize 2001)
  • Rubidium has no biological role, but plants absorb it like potassium (it's taken up by tobacco plants)
  • The deep red spectral line that gave rubidium its name is still used to identify it

Atomic Structure

37 protons, 48 neutrons, 37 electrons
