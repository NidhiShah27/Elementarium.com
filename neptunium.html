Neptunium (Np)

Atomic Number: 93

Basic Information

Symbol: Np
Atomic Mass: [237] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f⁴ 6d¹ 7s²

Key Properties

  • Silvery metallic, tarnishes slowly in air
  • First synthetic transuranium element (after uranium)
  • Named after the planet Neptune, next beyond Uranus
  • Three crystalline forms (allotropes) with complex phase transitions
  • Exhibits multiple oxidation states from +3 to +7 in solution

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1940 by Edwin McMillan and Philip H. Abelson at the University of California, Berkeley. They bombarded uranium with neutrons to create ²³⁹Np (half-life 2.36 days), the first synthetic element beyond uranium.

McMillan shared the 1951 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for this discovery. Neptunium-237 (half-life 2.14 million years) is produced in significant quantities as a byproduct of nuclear reactors.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1940: McMillan & Abelson synthesize ²³⁹Np at Berkeley
  • 1944: First isolation of neptunium compounds
  • 1950s: Production of weighable amounts from reactor fuel
  • Modern: Studied for nuclear waste management and plutonium production

Common Uses

Precursor to plutonium-238 (via neutron capture)
Research in nuclear chemistry and physics
No commercial applications (highly radioactive)
Study of actinide behavior in spent nuclear fuel
Neutron detection devices (²³⁷Np fission chambers)
Calibration sources
Component of some nuclear waste forms
Tracer in environmental studies

Interesting Facts

  • Neptunium was the first synthetic transuranium element ever produced, opening the door to elements 94 and beyond.
  • Trace amounts of ²³⁷Np are naturally produced in uranium ores by neutron capture, making it extremely rare in nature.
  • It has the widest range of oxidation states of any actinide (+3 to +7), with all states accessible in aqueous solution.
  • ²³⁷Np is used in high-energy neutron detectors because it has a high fission cross-section for fast neutrons.
  • Neptunium metal is relatively soft and can exist in three different crystal structures depending on temperature.
  • Global inventory of neptunium from nuclear reactors is estimated at several hundred metric tons, mostly as ²³⁷Np.

Atomic Structure

93 protons, 144 neutrons (in 237Np), 93 electrons

Neptunium chemistry is dominated by the +4, +5, and +6 oxidation states. The neptunyl ion (NpO₂⁺) is unusual because it's a pentavalent actinyl cation, unlike the hexavalent uranyl ion.

Experiment Idea: In tracer-level work, neptunium's various oxidation states can be identified by their color: Np³⁺ (purple), Np⁴⁺ (yellow-green), NpO₂⁺ (green), and NpO₂²⁺ (pink). The pentavalent neptunyl ion NpO₂⁺ is unique among actinides and tends to disproportionate in acidic solution. Separation from uranium and plutonium is achieved using ion exchange or solvent extraction.
