Chromium (Cr)

Atomic Number: 24

Basic Information

Symbol: Cr
Atomic Mass: 51.9961 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 6)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Steely-grey, lustrous, hard and brittle metal [citation:1]
  • Melting point: 1907°C, Boiling point: 2671°C [citation:5]
  • Extremely corrosion resistant (forms protective Cr₂O₃ layer) [citation:3]
  • Highest hardness of all transition metals (Mohs 8.5) [citation:7]
  • Named from Greek "chroma" (color) for its colorful compounds [citation:4]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1797 by Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin in Paris, France. He isolated chromium oxide from the mineral crocoite (PbCrO₄) and then reduced it to chromium metal the following year [citation:4][citation:8].

The name comes from the Greek "chroma" meaning color, because its compounds come in a rainbow of colors – red, yellow, green, and orange [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1761: Johann Gottlob Lehmann finds orange-red mineral (crocoite) in Urals [citation:4]
  • 1797: Vauquelin discovers chromium in crocoite [citation:1]
  • 1798: Vauquelin produces pure chromium metal [citation:8]
  • 1912: Stainless steel (Fe-Cr alloy) patented in Germany [citation:3]

Common Uses

Stainless steel (kitchenware, appliances) [citation:3]
Chrome plating (bumpers, wheels) [citation:2]
Pigments (chrome yellow, chrome green) [citation:5]
Tanning leather (chromium salts) [citation:7]
Refractory bricks (chromite) [citation:1]
Synthetic rubies (Cr-doped Al₂O₃) [citation:4]
Wood preservatives (chromated copper arsenate) [citation:8]
Magnetic tape (chromium dioxide) [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Chromium's electron configuration is unusual – it's [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ instead of the expected [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s². This is because a half-filled d-subshell (d⁵) is extra stable [citation:2].
  • The name "chrome" comes from the Greek for color, and chromium compounds are indeed colorful – Cr³⁺ is green/violet, Cr⁶⁺ is yellow/orange, and Cr²⁺ is blue. This rainbow led to its use in pigments and gemstones [citation:4].
  • Ruby red color comes from chromium – corundum (Al₂O₃) is colorless, but when a tiny amount of Cr³⁺ replaces Al³⁺, it creates the deep red of rubies. The first laser (1960) used a synthetic ruby crystal [citation:7].
  • Chromium is essential for human health in trace amounts (as Cr³⁺), helping regulate blood sugar. However, Cr⁶⁺ (hexavalent chromium) is highly toxic and carcinogenic – it's the contaminant made famous by the film "Erin Brockovich" [citation:5].
  • Stainless steel typically contains at least 10.5% chromium. The chromium forms a self-healing oxide layer that reforms if scratched, making it "stainless" [citation:3].
  • Crocoite (PbCrO₄), the mineral in which chromium was discovered, is a beautiful bright orange-red crystal and is highly prized by mineral collectors [citation:8].

Atomic Structure

24 protons, 28 neutrons (in 52Cr), 24 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic "chromate-dichromate equilibrium" demonstrates Le Chatelier's principle. Potassium chromate (K₂CrO₄) is yellow, while potassium dichromate (K₂Cr₂O₇) is orange. In solution, they exist in equilibrium: 2CrO₄²⁻ (yellow) + 2H⁺ ⇌ Cr₂O₇²⁻ (orange) + H₂O. Adding acid shifts the equilibrium to orange dichromate; adding base shifts back to yellow chromate. This reversible color change is a beautiful demonstration of chemical equilibrium and the dual nature of chromium's +6 state [citation:1].
