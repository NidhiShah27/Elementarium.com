Noble Gases

The inert ones • full shells, glowing signs & cosmic abundance

What are Noble Gases?

Position: Group 18 (VIIIA) of the periodic table — helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, radon (oganesson)
Electron configuration: ns² np⁶ (full valence shell) — helium has 1s² (duet rule)
Defining traits:
  • Extremely low chemical reactivity — "inert gases"
  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless monatomic gases at standard conditions
  • Very low boiling points — all exist as gases at room temperature
  • Highest ionization energies in their respective periods
  • Do not form chemical bonds under normal conditions
  • Glow with characteristic colors when electrically excited (neon signs)

Named "noble" because of their aloofness — like noble metals, they resist chemical reactions. The term was coined by Hugo Erdmann in 1898.

Key Properties

  • Boiling points: He (-269°C), Ne (-246°C), Ar (-186°C), Kr (-152°C), Xe (-108°C), Rn (-62°C)
  • Ionization energy trend: decreases down the group — helium has highest IE of all elements
  • Reactivity: Xenon forms compounds with fluorine (XeF₂, XeF₄, XeF₆) under extreme conditions
  • Atomic radius: increases down the group (He smallest, Rn largest)
  • Abundance: Argon is most abundant in Earth's atmosphere (0.93%); helium is abundant in the universe (23% by mass)
  • Radon: Radioactive noble gas — decays to polonium and lead
Did you know? Helium was first discovered in the Sun (1868) through spectroscopy before being found on Earth. Its name comes from Greek "helios" (sun).

Fascinating facts

  • Helium is the second most abundant element in the universe (23% by mass) — created by nuclear fusion in stars.
  • Argon is the third most abundant gas in Earth's atmosphere (0.93%) — more than CO₂.
  • Neon got its name from Greek "neos" meaning "new" — discovered in 1898 by Ramsay and Travers.
  • Krypton defined the meter from 1960 to 1983 (1,650,763.73 wavelengths of krypton-86 light).
  • Xenon was the first noble gas found to form true chemical compounds (XeF₂, 1962 by Neil Bartlett).
  • Radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking — accumulates in basements from uranium decay.

The "Inert" myth & noble gas compounds

1962: A chemical revolution

Neil Bartlett synthesized XePtF₆ — the first noble gas compound. This shattered the belief that noble gases were completely inert.

Today, known compounds: XeF₂, XeF₄, XeF₆, KrF₂, and even xenon oxides (XeO₃, XeO₄).

Reactivity trend under extreme conditions

He, Ne, Ar Kr (limited) Xe (many compounds) Rn (theoretical)

Only heavier noble gases (Kr, Xe, Rn) form stable compounds — requires strong oxidizing agents like fluorine.

Endohedral fullerenes & clathrates: Noble gases can be trapped inside carbon cages (He@C₆₀) or water ice cages (gas hydrates) without forming covalent bonds.

Production method: Fractional distillation of liquid air. Helium is also extracted from natural gas fields (up to 7% He in some US wells). Global helium supply is critical for MRI machines and semiconductor manufacturing.

Environmental & economic note: Helium is a non-renewable resource — escaping into space once released. Recycling and conservation are increasingly important for medical and scientific applications.
