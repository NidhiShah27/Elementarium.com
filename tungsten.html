Tungsten (W)

Atomic Number: 74

Basic Information

Symbol: W
Atomic Mass: 183.84 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 6)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁴ 6s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Steel-gray to tin-white, extremely hard and dense [citation:1]
  • Highest melting point of all metals: 3422°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance [citation:3]
  • Highest tensile strength at high temperatures [citation:7]
  • Low thermal expansion, high creep resistance [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Tungsten has a dual discovery history. In 1781, Carl Wilhelm Scheele discovered "tungstic acid" in scheelite. In 1783, the Spanish brothers Juan José and Fausto Elhuyar isolated tungsten metal from wolframite [citation:4][citation:8].

The name "tungsten" comes from Swedish "tung sten" meaning "heavy stone." The symbol W comes from "wolfram," derived from wolframite, and is still used in many languages [citation:1].

Interesting Facts

  • Tungsten has the highest melting point (3422°C) and the lowest vapor pressure of any metal. Its boiling point (5930°C) is the highest of any element except carbon [citation:2].
  • Tungsten carbide (WC) is one of the hardest materials known – about three times as stiff as steel and nearly as hard as diamond. It's used in armor-piercing ammunition and mining drill bits [citation:3].
  • The famous "tungsten filament" in incandescent bulbs operates at temperatures over 2500°C – hotter than the surface of the Sun – and would instantly oxidize in air, which is why bulbs are filled with inert gas or evacuated [citation:5].
  • Tungsten is used in the tokamak fusion reactors (like ITER) as a plasma-facing material because it can withstand extreme heat and sputtering without contaminating the plasma [citation:7].
  • Tungsten rings have become popular in men's jewelry because they're virtually scratch-proof and maintain their polish forever. However, they cannot be resized and will shatter if dropped on hard surfaces [citation:1].
  • Tungsten is incredibly dense – about 1.7 times denser than lead. A 1-inch cube of tungsten weighs about 1.2 pounds (the same size lead cube weighs 0.7 pounds) [citation:4].

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The "tungsten halogen cycle" is a fascinating chemical phenomenon. In halogen lamps, small amounts of iodine or bromine are added. At high temperatures, tungsten evaporating from the filament reacts with halogen gas to form tungsten halides. These circulate back to the hot filament, decompose, and redeposit tungsten – effectively recycling the filament and preventing bulb blackening. This allows halogen bulbs to run hotter and brighter than standard incandescent lamps [citation:1].
