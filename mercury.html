Mercury (Hg)

Atomic Number: 80

Basic Information

Symbol: Hg
Atomic Mass: 200.592 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 12)
State at Room Temperature: Liquid
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, heavy, liquid metal at room temperature [citation:1]
  • Very low melting point: -38.8°C, boiling: 356.7°C [citation:5]
  • Poor conductor of heat, good conductor of electricity [citation:3]
  • Forms alloys (amalgams) with most metals except iron [citation:7]
  • Highly toxic – absorbed through skin and as vapor [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Mercury has been known since ancient times – its symbol Hg comes from the Latin "hydrargyrum" (liquid silver). It was known to ancient Chinese, Egyptians, and Greeks, who used it in ointments and cosmetics [citation:4][citation:8].

The Romans mined cinnabar (HgS) as a pigment. Alchemists considered mercury a prime element and believed it could transmute base metals into gold [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • ~1500 BCE: Mercury in Egyptian tombs & Chinese artifacts [citation:4]
  • ~300 BCE: Theophrastus describes obtaining mercury from cinnabar [citation:1]
  • 1759: Freezing point discovered by Braun [citation:3]
  • 20th c.: Widely used in thermometers, batteries, dentistry [citation:8]

Common Uses

Thermometers, barometers (historically) [citation:3]
Fluorescent lamps & streetlights [citation:2]
Amalgamation for gold & silver extraction [citation:5]
Dental amalgams (silver fillings) [citation:7]
Electrical switches & relays [citation:1]
Cathode in chlor-alkali production [citation:4]
Laboratory reagents & catalysts [citation:8]
Vermilion pigment (cinnabar) [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Mercury is the only metal that is liquid at standard room temperature and pressure (gallium and cesium melt just above room temperature). A heavy 1-liter bottle of mercury weighs about 13.5 kg (nearly 30 pounds) [citation:2].
  • The element's chemical symbol Hg comes from the Greek "hydrargyrum," meaning "liquid silver." Alchemists called it "quicksilver" because it moves like living silver [citation:3].
  • Mercury is extremely toxic – "mad as a hatter" refers to 19th-century hat makers who used mercury nitrate to treat felt and suffered from mercury poisoning, causing tremors and personality changes [citation:5].
  • Mercury dissolves many metals to form "amalgams" – it was essential for gold and silver extraction during the California Gold Rush. However, this also released thousands of tons of mercury into rivers [citation:7].
  • Fluorescent lamps contain a tiny drop of mercury that vaporizes and produces ultraviolet light when electrified. A single bulb contains about 3-5 mg of mercury [citation:1].
  • Mercury's uniform thermal expansion made it ideal for thermometers for centuries, but concerns over toxicity have led to their replacement by alcohol or digital thermometers [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

80 protons, 121 neutrons (in 201Hg), 80 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic "beating heart" demonstration is a mesmerizing electrochemical reaction. A drop of mercury in a watch glass is covered with an oxidizing acid (like sulfuric acid with potassium dichromate) and touched with an iron nail. The mercury begins to pulse rhythmically, appearing to beat like a heart. This occurs due to changes in surface tension caused by the redox reaction cycling between oxidation and reduction of the mercury surface – a beautiful illustration of electrochemical oscillations [citation:1].
