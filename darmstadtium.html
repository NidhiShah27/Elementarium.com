Darmstadtium (Ds)

Atomic Number: 110

Basic Information

Symbol: Ds
Atomic Mass: [281] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 10)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d⁸ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~2200°C (predicted, similar to platinum) [citation:8]
  • Density: ~34 g/cm³ (estimated, very high) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of platinum (Group 10) [citation:1]
  • Predicted to form volatile carbonyl Ds(CO)₄, similar to Ni(CO)₄ and Pd(CO)₄ [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First synthesized in 1994 by a team led by Sigurd Hofmann at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. They bombarded lead-208 with nickel-62 ions, producing three atoms of 269Ds and identifying their alpha decay [citation:1][citation:8].

Later in 1994, they also used nickel-64 to produce 271Ds. This was the first element discovered at GSI's new velocity filter SHIP [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1994: GSI team produces and identifies element 110 [citation:1]
  • 2001: IUPAC credits GSI with discovery [citation:1]
  • 2003: IUPAC officially names element 110 darmstadtium (after Darmstadt, Germany) [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Darmstadtium is named after Darmstadt, Germany, the city where the GSI laboratory is located. It's one of the few elements named after a city (others include berkelium, dubnium, and hassium) [citation:4].
  • The discovery of darmstadtium was part of a remarkable run at GSI in the 1990s, where they also discovered roentgenium (111) and copernicium (112) using similar cold fusion reactions [citation:1].
  • Chemical studies on darmstadtium are extremely challenging but have been attempted. In 2007, scientists studied the adsorption of darmstadtium atoms on a gold surface, finding it behaves like a typical Group 10 element, similar to platinum [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 281Ds, has a half-life of about 11 seconds. It is produced in the decay chain of 289Fl (flerovium) and 285Cn (copernicium) [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects in darmstadtium are predicted to be significant. The 7s electrons are contracted and stabilized, while the 6d orbitals are expanded. This affects its chemical reactivity and may make it more noble than platinum [citation:2].
  • Darmstadtium is predicted to be a noble metal, even more resistant to corrosion than platinum. It may be the "most noble" element in Group 10, but this has not been experimentally confirmed [citation:1].

Atomic Structure

110 protons, 171 neutrons (in 281Ds), 110 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): A classic darmstadtium chemistry experiment uses gas-phase chromatography to study adsorption on a gold surface. Atoms of 281Ds (half-life 11 seconds) are produced in a nuclear reaction, thermalized, and passed through a chromatography column coated with gold. By measuring where they deposit (temperature gradient), scientists can determine the adsorption enthalpy and compare it with platinum and palladium. Results show Ds behaves like a typical Group 10 element, confirming its place in the periodic table [citation:1].
