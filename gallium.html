Gallium (Ga)

Atomic Number: 31

Basic Information

Symbol: Ga
Atomic Mass: 69.723 u
Category: Post-Transition Metal
State at Room Temperature: Solid (melts in your hand)
Electron Configuration:
[Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p¹

Key Properties

  • Melts at just 29.8°C – liquefies in your hand
  • Expands when it freezes (like water)
  • Essential for semiconductors (GaAs, GaN)
  • Liquid over a wide range (up to 2400°C)
  • Predicted by Mendeleev as "eka-aluminium"

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1875 by Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran in France. He isolated it from zinc blende ore and named it after Gallia (Latin for France).

Its existence and properties were predicted by Mendeleev in 1871 as "eka-aluminium" – a triumph of the periodic table.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1871: Mendeleev predicts eka-aluminium
  • 1875: Lecoq de Boisbaudran discovers gallium
  • 1960s: Gallium arsenide semiconductors developed
  • 21st c.: Vital for LEDs and smartphones

Common Uses

Semiconductors (GaAs, GaN)
LEDs and laser diodes
Smartphone components
Solar cells (CIGS)
High-temperature thermometers
Neutrino detectors
Liquid metal alloys
Dental alloys

Interesting Facts

  • Gallium melts in your hand – a solid cube turns to liquid from body heat. It's safe to handle, but it wets skin and leaves a gray stain
  • Unlike most metals, gallium expands when it freezes – so you can't store it in glass containers or they'll crack
  • Gallium arsenide (GaAs) is faster than silicon – electrons move 5 times faster, making it ideal for high-frequency electronics
  • It's used in neutrino detectors – the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory used 100 tonnes of liquid gallium
  • Your smartphone contains gallium in its LEDs and RF amplifiers – it's essential for 5G
  • Gallium can "dissolve" aluminium – it diffuses along grain boundaries and makes aluminium extremely brittle

Atomic Structure

31 protons, 39 neutrons, 31 electrons
