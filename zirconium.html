Zirconium (Zr)

Atomic Number: 40

Basic Information

Symbol: Zr
Atomic Mass: 91.224 u
Category: Transition Metal (Group 4)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d² 5s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Grayish-white, lustrous, and very strong metal [citation:1]
  • Extremely high melting point: 1855°C [citation:5]
  • Outstanding corrosion resistance (similar to titanium) [citation:3]
  • Low neutron absorption cross-section (ideal for nuclear reactors) [citation:7]
  • Highly resistant to acids and alkalis [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1789 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth in Berlin, Germany. He analyzed a zircon gemstone from Sri Lanka and identified a new oxide, which he called "zirconia." The impure metal was first isolated by Jöns Jacob Berzelius in 1824 [citation:4][citation:8].

The name comes from the mineral "zircon" (from Persian "zargun" meaning gold-colored), which has been used as a gemstone for centuries [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:
  • Ancient: Zircon gemstones used as decorative objects [citation:4]
  • 1789: Klaproth identifies zirconium oxide [citation:1]
  • 1824: Berzelius produces impure zirconium metal [citation:3]
  • 1940s: Becomes vital for nuclear reactors (zircaloy) [citation:8]

Common Uses

Nuclear reactor fuel cladding (zircaloy) [citation:3]
High-temperature crucibles & furnaces [citation:2]

Catalyst for organic reactions [citation:6]

Interesting Facts

  • Cubic zirconia (zirconium dioxide) is a synthetic gemstone that closely resembles diamond. It's harder than most natural gems and has a high refractive index, giving it brilliant fire and sparkle – but at a fraction of the cost [citation:2].
  • Zirconium is a superhero in nuclear reactors – its special alloy "zircaloy" is used to encase nuclear fuel rods because it's practically transparent to neutrons (doesn't absorb them), allowing the chain reaction to continue efficiently [citation:3].
  • Zirconium powder is used in military pyrotechnics and explosives because it burns with an intense white light – it's a key component in some flash-bang grenades and photoflash bulbs [citation:5].
  • The mineral zircon (ZrSiO₄) is one of the oldest minerals on Earth. Some zircons from Australia have been dated to 4.4 billion years old – almost as old as the Earth itself, providing clues about early planetary formation [citation:7].
  • Yttria-stabilized zirconia is used for dental crowns because it's extremely strong, biocompatible, and tooth-colored. It can withstand the tremendous forces of chewing without cracking [citation:1].
  • Zirconium dioxide (zirconia) is an exceptional thermal barrier coating – it's used to insulate jet engine turbine blades, allowing them to operate at temperatures above their melting point [citation:4].

Atomic Structure

40 protons, 51 neutrons (in 91Zr), 40 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): Zirconium dioxide (ZrO₂) is fascinating. In its pure form, it undergoes a phase change when heated that causes cracking. But when mixed with a small amount of yttrium oxide (Y₂O₃), it forms "stabilized zirconia" – a material that remains in a cubic crystal structure at all temperatures. This stabilized form is used for everything from oxygen sensors to artificial diamonds. While a lab synthesis is advanced, the concept highlights how a little yttrium completely transforms zirconia's properties. You can demonstrate the thermal barrier property by heating one side of a zirconia ceramic tile – the other side stays cool [citation:1].
