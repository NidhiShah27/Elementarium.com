Thorium (Th)

Atomic Number: 90

Basic Information

Symbol: Th
Atomic Mass: 232.0377 u (most abundant isotope)
Category: Actinide
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 6d² 7s²

Key Properties

  • Silvery-white, radioactive metal that tarnishes to black
  • Second element in the actinide series, named after the Norse god Thor
  • One of only three radioactive elements with a primordial isotope (²³²Th)
  • Can be used as a nuclear fuel in breeder reactors
  • About three times more abundant than uranium in Earth's crust

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1828 by the Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius. He named it after Thor, the Norse god of thunder, reflecting its power and mystery.

Thorium's radioactivity wasn't recognized until the work of Marie Curie and others in the early 1900s. Its potential as a nuclear fuel was explored during the Manhattan Project and continues in advanced reactor designs.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1828: Berzelius discovers thorium in a Norwegian mineral
  • 1898: Marie Curie independently observes thorium's radioactivity
  • 1940s: Thorium researched for nuclear reactors
  • Modern: Thorium molten-salt reactors (MSRs) gain renewed interest

Common Uses

Potential nuclear fuel (Th-232 → U-233)
Gas mantles (thorium oxide incandescence)
High-quality camera lenses (high refractive index)
Catalyst in chemical industry
Alloying agent (magnesium-thorium alloys)
Heat-resistant ceramics
Aerospace components (high-temperature strength)
Electrode coating for arc welding (thoriated tungsten)

Interesting Facts

  • Thorium is named after Thor, the Norse god of thunder, because of its immense hidden power – fitting for a future energy source.
  • ²³²Th has a half-life of 14.05 billion years, about the age of the universe, so it still exists primordially.
  • It's three to four times more abundant than uranium and as common as lead or molybdenum.
  • When bombarded with neutrons, ²³²Th absorbs one and undergoes beta decay to become fissile ²³³U.
  • Thorium reactors could theoretically produce less long-lived nuclear waste than traditional uranium reactors.
  • Major deposits exist in India, Australia, the US, and Turkey; India has about 25% of the world's thorium.

Atomic Structure

90 protons, 142 neutrons (in 232Th), 90 electrons

Try These Thorium Reactions

Thorium is reactive and forms a protective oxide layer. It dissolves in concentrated HCl and H₂SO₄, and reacts with halogens at elevated temperatures. Its chemistry is dominated by the +4 oxidation state.
