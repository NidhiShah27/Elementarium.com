Phosphorus (P)

Atomic Number: 15

Basic Information

Symbol: P
Atomic Mass: 30.973762 u
Category: Nonmetal
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Ne] 3s² 3p³

Key Properties

  • White phosphorus glows in the dark and ignites in air
  • Red phosphorus is stable and used in matches
  • Essential for all living cells (DNA, ATP)
  • Major component of bones and teeth (as calcium phosphate)
  • Highly toxic in its white form

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1669 by Hennig Brand in Germany while trying to create the philosopher's stone from urine. He isolated white phosphorus by evaporating and heating urine.

The name comes from Greek "phosphoros" meaning "light-bearer" – because white phosphorus glows in the dark (chemiluminescence).

Historical Timeline:
  • 1669: Brand discovers phosphorus from urine
  • 1845: Red phosphorus discovered by Anton von Schrötter
  • 1855: Safety matches developed using red phosphorus
  • 20th c.: Phosphorus becomes essential for fertilizers

Common Uses

Fertilizers (phosphate)
Safety matches (red phosphorus)
Soft drinks (phosphoric acid)
Biochemical research
Incendiary weapons (white phosphorus)
Toothpaste (calcium phosphate)
Semiconductor doping
Food preservatives

Interesting Facts

  • White phosphorus spontaneously ignites in air and must be stored under water
  • The glow of white phosphorus (chemiluminescence) is not radioactivity – it's a slow reaction with oxygen
  • An adult human body contains about 1 kg of phosphorus (mostly in bones and teeth)
  • Red phosphorus is made by heating white phosphorus without air at 250°C
  • Phosphorus is essential for all living organisms – there is no life without it
  • The element was discovered from 50 buckets of urine – Brand's process was tedious and smelly!

Atomic Structure

15 protons, 16 neutrons, 15 electrons
