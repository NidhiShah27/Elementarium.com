Seaborgium (Sg)

Atomic Number: 106

Basic Information

Symbol: Sg
Atomic Mass: [269] u (most stable isotope) [citation:2]
Category: Transition Metal (Group 6)
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted) [citation:2]
Electron Configuration:
[Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d⁴ 7s² [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Synthetic, highly radioactive metal (does not occur naturally) [citation:1]
  • Melting point: ~2300°C (predicted, similar to tungsten) [citation:8]
  • Density: ~35 g/cm³ (estimated, one of the highest) [citation:5]
  • Behaves as heavier homolog of tungsten and molybdenum (Group 6) [citation:1]
  • Forms volatile oxychloride (SgO₂Cl₂), similar to Mo and W [citation:1]

Discovery & History

First reported in 1974 by a team led by Georgy Flerov at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Dubna, Russia. They bombarded lead-208 with chromium-54 ions and detected a spontaneous fission activity they attributed to element 106 [citation:1][citation:8].

Later in 1974, a team led by Albert Ghiorso at Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory (LBL) conclusively synthesized seaborgium by bombarding californium-249 with oxygen-18 ions, producing 263Sg and identifying its alpha decay [citation:1][citation:8].

Historical Timeline:
  • 1974: Dubna team reports element 106; Berkeley team produces it weeks later [citation:4]
  • 1992: IUPAC concludes both teams contributed; credit shared [citation:1]
  • 1994: IUPAC recommends name "rutherfordium" for element 106 (later changed) [citation:4]
  • 1997: IUPAC officially names element 106 seaborgium (after Glenn T. Seaborg) [citation:2][citation:4]

Interesting Facts

  • Seaborgium is the first element named after a living person (Glenn T. Seaborg, 1912-1999). This broke a long-standing tradition, but Seaborg had co-discovered ten transuranic elements, including plutonium [citation:4].
  • The naming was controversial – IUPAC initially proposed "rutherfordium" for element 106, but after protests from the American Chemical Society, seaborgium was accepted in 1997. Seaborg was alive to see the honor [citation:4].
  • Chemical studies of seaborgium (using just a few atoms) confirmed it behaves like a Group 6 element. In 2000, scientists synthesized seaborgium hexacarbonyl Sg(CO)₆ – a volatile compound similar to Mo(CO)₆ and W(CO)₆ – providing strong evidence for its chemical identity [citation:1].
  • The most stable known isotope, 269Sg, has a half-life of about 2.1 minutes – long enough for chemical studies. Several other isotopes with half-lives from seconds to minutes are known [citation:2].
  • Relativistic effects strongly influence seaborgium's chemistry. The 7s electrons are contracted and stabilized, while the 6d orbitals are expanded. This affects bond lengths, volatility, and complex formation compared to tungsten [citation:2].
  • The first atom-at-a-time chemistry on seaborgium was performed in 1995-1996 using the isotope 266Sg (half-life ~21 seconds). Scientists studied its oxychloride SgO₂Cl₂ and compared its volatility with MoO₂Cl₂ and WO₂Cl₂, confirming relativistic effects [citation:1].

Atomic Structure

106 protons, 163 neutrons (in 269Sg), 106 electrons

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The classic seaborgium chemistry experiment uses isothermal gas chromatography to compare SgO₂Cl₂ with MoO₂Cl₂ and WO₂Cl₂. Atoms of 266Sg (half-life ~21 seconds) are produced in a nuclear reaction, rapidly converted to oxychlorides in a hot gas stream containing O₂ and Cl₂, and passed through a chromatography column. By measuring deposition temperatures, scientists found SgO₂Cl₂ is less volatile than MoO₂Cl₂ but similar to WO₂Cl₂ – confirming relativistic effects and group 6 placement, while showing the influence of the 5f electron shell on bonding [citation:1].
