Astatine (At)

Atomic Number: 85

Basic Information

Symbol: At
Atomic Mass: [210] u (most stable isotope)
Category: Halogen
State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted)
Electron Configuration:
[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p⁵

Key Properties

  • Extremely radioactive – all isotopes decay rapidly
  • Rarest naturally occurring element
  • Dark, metallic appearance (predicted)
  • More metallic than other halogens
  • Less than an ounce exists on Earth at any time

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1940 by Dale Corson, Kenneth MacKenzie, and Emilio Segrè at the University of California, Berkeley. They synthesized it by bombarding bismuth-209 with alpha particles.

The name comes from Greek "astatos" meaning unstable, because all its isotopes are radioactive.

Historical Timeline:
  • 1940: First synthesized at Berkeley
  • 1943: Found to occur naturally in trace amounts
  • 1950s: Chemical properties studied (with difficulty)
  • 21st c.: Potential medical applications (targeted radiotherapy)

Common Uses

Interesting Facts

  • Astatine is the rarest naturally occurring element – the total amount in Earth's crust is less than 1 ounce (about 25 grams) at any time
  • Its most stable isotope, At-210, has a half-life of just 8.1 hours – too short to accumulate
  • Astatine is so radioactive that it glows and vaporizes itself due to its own heat (self-heating)
  • It was the last element of the halogen group to be discovered – filling the final gap in the periodic table
  • Astatine is concentrated by the thyroid gland (like iodine) – At-211 is being studied for cancer treatment
  • Only about 1 nanogram of astatine has ever been produced at once – all chemical studies used invisible trace amounts

Atomic Structure

85 protons, 125 neutrons (in 210At), 85 electrons
