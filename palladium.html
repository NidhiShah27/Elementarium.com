Palladium (Pd)

Atomic Number: 46

Basic Information

Symbol: Pd
Atomic Mass: 106.42 u
Category: Transition Metal (Platinum Group)
State at Room Temperature: Solid
Electron Configuration:
[Kr] 4d¹⁰ [citation:1][citation:2]

Key Properties

  • Lustrous silvery-white precious metal [citation:1]
  • Lowest melting point of platinum group: 1555°C [citation:5]
  • Excellent corrosion resistance [citation:3]
  • Absorbs up to 900× its volume of hydrogen [citation:7]
  • Highly prized for jewelry and catalytic converters [citation:2]

Discovery & History

Discovered in 1802 by William Hyde Wollaston in London, England. He dissolved platinum ore in aqua regia, neutralized the solution, and precipitated palladium as a cyanide compound. The element was named after the asteroid Pallas [citation:4][citation:8].

Wollaston kept his discovery secret for a while and even anonymously advertised pure palladium for sale to generate scientific interest before revealing his work [citation:1].

Historical Timeline:

Interesting Facts

  • Palladium can absorb up to 900 times its own volume of hydrogen! This makes it essential for hydrogen purification – only hydrogen can diffuse through hot palladium, so it's used to produce ultra-pure hydrogen for fuel cells and electronics [citation:2].
  • Palladium is more abundant and less expensive than platinum, but it's still a precious metal worth thousands of dollars per ounce. Its price surged in recent years due to demand from the auto industry [citation:3].
  • Most gasoline-powered cars today use palladium in their catalytic converters (platinum is used more for diesel). Palladium converts toxic carbon monoxide and unburned hydrocarbons into carbon dioxide and water vapor [citation:5].
  • The Nobel Prize-winning "Suzuki coupling" reaction uses palladium to join carbon atoms together. This reaction revolutionized the synthesis of complex organic molecules, from pharmaceuticals to advanced materials [citation:7].
  • Palladium was named after the asteroid Pallas, which itself was named after the Greek goddess Pallas Athena. It's one of the few elements named after an asteroid [citation:1].
  • Some high-end flutes are made from palladium – it produces a warm, rich tone and is lighter than silver or gold. It's also hypoallergenic, making it ideal for musicians with metal allergies [citation:4].

Experiment Idea (Conceptual): The Suzuki-Miyaura cross-coupling reaction is one of the most powerful tools in organic synthesis, and it earned Akira Suzuki the Nobel Prize in 2010. A palladium catalyst joins an organoboron compound with an organic halide to form a new carbon-carbon bond. This reaction is used to create everything from pharmaceuticals (like the blood pressure drug Valsartan) to OLED displays. The palladium cycles between Pd(0) and Pd(II), showing the element's incredible catalytic versatility. In the lab, you can observe the dark color of palladium catalysts [citation:1].
